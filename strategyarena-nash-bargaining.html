Why Nash maximizes the product

The Nash product is (u₁ - d₁) × (u₂ - d₂) — the product of each player's surplus over disagreement. Maximizing this product over the feasible set produces a unique solution that satisfies four axioms simultaneously: Pareto efficiency (no waste), symmetry (equal players get equal shares), scale invariance (utility units don't matter), and independence of irrelevant alternatives. Geometrically, the optimum is the point where the highest hyperbolic contour (u₁ - d₁)(u₂ - d₂) = k just touches the Pareto frontier. The two curves are tangent at that point.

1
Pareto efficiency

No agreement that makes both better off should be left on the table. The solution sits on the boundary, not the interior.

2
Symmetry

Players in identical bargaining positions receive identical shares. Symmetry of the problem ⇒ symmetry of the solution.

3
Scale invariance

If you measure Player 1's utility in dollars vs cents, the bargain doesn't change. Linear utility transformations leave the solution unchanged.

4
IIA

Removing options that wouldn't have been chosen anyway shouldn't change the bargain. The solution depends on the local geometry near itself.

Arena
Nash Bargaining
You and a counterpart must agree on how to divide a joint surplus, with a disagreement point you both fall back to if you fail to reach a deal. You propose a split somewhere on the frontier of what's jointly feasible, and your counterpart decides whether to accept. The challenge is finding a division that's both efficient and acceptable — one that leaves neither of you with a reason to walk away.
You get two attempts. After finishing your first attempt, check your grade in the app. If you're happy with it, you're done — only use your second attempt if you want to try to improve.
