Chapter 1 · Foundations

Strategic Thinking

18 min read
4 exercises + quiz
No prerequisite

Game theory is the study of how rational decision-makers behave when their outcomes depend on the choices of others. That dependence — your fate hinges partly on what someone else picks — is what separates strategy from ordinary decision-making.

You're driving toward an intersection at the same moment as another car. There are no traffic lights. You both want to get through quickly. If you both hit the gas, you crash. If you both slow down, you each waste a moment waiting. If one guns it while the other yields, the bold one sails through and the cautious one loses only a few seconds.

There's no single "best" speed for you to pick — it depends entirely on what the other driver does. And here's the twist that makes it a game and not just a gamble: the other driver is reasoning about you in exactly the same way, at the same time.

That's a strategic situation. The wrinkle that makes it interesting — and makes game theory necessary — is that each of you is trying to figure out what the other will do, and you both know that.

What this course is about

The thirteen chapters that follow walk you through every major idea in modern strategic analysis, from the most famous two-player puzzle (the prisoner's dilemma) to evolutionary biology, fair-share theory, and the strange ways real humans deviate from rational predictions.

A typical chapter introduces a problem, develops the formal tools needed to analyze it, walks through worked examples, and ends with a quiz to check that you've absorbed the ideas. Each chapter from Chapter 2 onward also has a companion arena — an interactive simulation where you can play the game yourself, against AI opponents, and feel the concept rather than just read about it. The reading and the arena are designed to reinforce each other: the chapter gives you the formal scaffolding; the arena builds the intuition.

How to use this course

Read the chapter first. Take the quiz. Then open the arena and play. After playing for a few minutes, come back and skim the chapter again — you'll notice ideas that didn't register on first reading. The combination is powerful in a way neither piece is alone.

Three ingredients of every game

Game theory uses the word "game" in a very general way. A game can be a board game, an auction, a courtroom trial, a war, a marriage, a price war between two companies, or a flock of pigeons fighting over breadcrumbs. What unites these is that every game has three ingredients:

  1. Players — the decision-makers. Could be people, companies, animals, governments, computer programs.
  2. Strategies — what each player can do. Could be a single choice ("cooperate or defect") or a complex plan ("if she opens with chess move A, I respond with B; if she opens with C, I respond with D").
  3. Payoffs — what each player gets, depending on the combination of choices made by all players. Usually this is a number (utility, dollars, fitness) but it doesn't have to be.
Key idea

Every game, however complex, boils down to three things: who is playing (the players), what they can each do (the strategies), and what they get for every combination of choices (the payoffs). Pin down those three and you can analyze any strategic situation.

To analyze a game, you need to specify all three. Once you have them, you can ask the central question: what should each player do? Let us state the formal object precisely; this is the representation we will use for every simultaneous-move game in the book.

Definition 1.1 Normal-form game

A normal-form (strategic-form) game is a triple

$$G = \langle N, (S_i)_{i\in N}, (u_i)_{i\in N}\rangle$$

where $N = {1, 2, \ldots , n}$ is the finite set of players; for each player $i$, $S_i$ is the set of strategies available to $i$; and $u_i : S \to \mathbb{R}$ is the payoff function of player $i$, where $S = S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots \times S_n$ is the set of strategy profiles. A strategy profile is written $s = (s_1, \ldots , s_n)$, and the shorthand $s_{-i}$ denotes the strategies of everyone except $i$, so that $s = (s_i, s_{-i})$.

This compact notation will recur on nearly every page, so it is worth reading slowly. The set $S_i$ can be small and finite (Anna's ${\text{Together}, \text{Alone}}$), or a continuum (a firm's choice of any price $p \geq 0$). The payoff function $u_i$ takes the whole profile as input — your payoff depends on what everyone does, which is precisely what makes the situation strategic. The notation $s_{-i}$ ("s-minus-i") is the single most useful piece of shorthand in game theory: it lets us write "player $i$'s payoff when she plays $s_i$ and everyone else plays $s_{-i}$" as simply $u_i(s_i, s_{-i})$.

Two assumptions we'll make (and later relax)

Throughout most of this course we'll assume players are rational and that this rationality is common knowledge. These are big words; let's unpack them.

Rationality

A player is rational if she does whatever maximizes her payoff given her beliefs about what other players are doing. Notice this is a low bar — it does not mean players are smart, prescient, calm under pressure, or morally good. It just means: if a player believes a certain action will bring her the most utility, she'll take it.

Rationality also doesn't say anything about what payoffs you care about. If your "payoff" is making your sister miserable, that's fine — rationality just says you'll pick the action that maximizes that.

We can make "maximizes her payoff given her beliefs" precise with the central optimization concept of the entire field: the best response.

Definition 1.2 Best response

Fix the strategies $s_{-i}$ of all players other than $i$. A strategy $s_i^* \in S_i$ is a best response to $s_{-i}$ if

$$u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \quad\text{for all } s_i \in S_i.$$

That is, given what everyone else does, no other strategy yields player $i$ a strictly higher payoff. A rational player always plays a best response to her beliefs about $s_{-i}$.

Best response is the workhorse of the whole subject: the Nash equilibrium of Chapter 4 is defined as a profile in which everyone is simultaneously best-responding to everyone else. For now, we use it to make the notion of a dominant strategy precise and to prove our first formal result.

Definition 1.3 Strictly dominant strategy

A strategy $s_i^* \in S_i$ is strictly dominant for player $i$ if

$$u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \quad\text{for all } s_i \neq s_i^* \quad\text{and all } s_{-i} \in S_{-i},$$

where $S_{-i} = \prod_{j\neq i} S_j$. A strictly dominant strategy beats every alternative no matter what the other players do.

The reason dominant strategies are so powerful is captured by our first flagship result, which formalizes the reasoning we used implicitly in the prisoner's dilemma: a player with a dominant strategy need not form any beliefs about her opponents at all.

Proposition 1.4 Dominance implies unique best response

If $s_i^*$ is a strictly dominant strategy for player $i$, then $s_i^*$ is the unique best response to every strategy profile $s_{-i}$ of the other players.

Fix any profile $s_{-i} \in S_{-i}$ of the opponents. By the definition of strict dominance, $u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, s_{-i})$ for every strategy $s_i \neq s_i^*$. Hence $s_i^*$ achieves a strictly higher payoff than any alternative against this $s_{-i}$, so it satisfies the best-response inequality of Definition 1.2 — and does so strictly, ruling out ties. Therefore $s_i^*$ is the unique best response to $s_{-i}$. Since $s_{-i}$ was arbitrary, this holds for every opponent profile. ∎

The practical consequence is the one we exploited for the prisoner's dilemma: when a player has a strictly dominant strategy, we can predict her play with certainty and without needing to model her beliefs. This is the strongest possible solution concept — and, as the prisoner's dilemma showed, it can lead inexorably to outcomes that make everyone worse off.

Common knowledge of rationality

This is the deeper assumption. It says: I know you're rational, you know I'm rational, I know that you know I'm rational, you know that I know that you know I'm rational, and so on, infinitely.

That sounds dizzying, and it is. But the upshot is practical: when you reason about what your opponent will do, you can assume she's reasoning about what you'll do using the same machinery — and you can assume she expects you to assume that, etc. This recursive mutual modeling is what gives game theory its predictive power. It's also where the field starts to push beyond ordinary economics, which often only requires individual rationality.

When we get to Chapter 13 (Behavioral Game Theory), we'll see that real people often violate both assumptions in fascinating, predictable ways. But getting the rational benchmark right is still the foundation — you need to know what the prediction should be before you can study where humans deviate from it.

Two kinds of games

Games come in two main flavors based on how players move:

Simultaneous games

In a simultaneous game, all players choose their strategies at the same time, without knowing what the others have chosen. Rock-paper-scissors is the classic example. So is bidding in a sealed-bid auction. In a simultaneous game, you can't react to your opponent's move because you don't know it yet.

Anna and Ben's study problem above is a simultaneous game — they each decide where to study at the start of the day, before knowing what the other has decided.

Sequential games

In a sequential game, players move in turn, and later movers can see what earlier movers did. Chess is sequential. So is a courtroom interaction where the defense responds to the prosecution's argument.

Sequential games are typically drawn as game trees — branching diagrams showing each possible move and what comes next. We'll formally introduce these in Chapter 4.

Many real situations have elements of both. Negotiation often involves long sequences of offers and counter-offers (sequential), but at any given moment each side is also forming beliefs about what the other will accept (which has simultaneous-game flavor). Game theory has tools for both pure cases and combinations.

Why this matters outside the classroom

Game theory was once a niche topic in mathematics. Today its tools are used to design auctions for billion-dollar spectrum licenses, to set traffic lights, to write the matchmaking algorithms in dating apps, to explain why peacocks have absurd tails, to set carbon emission policy, to design vaccines, to negotiate union contracts, and to predict when a country will declare war.

A 2007 Nobel Prize in Economics went to three game theorists for "mechanism design" — figuring out how to structure rules so that players acting in their own self-interest produce outcomes a designer wants. A 2012 Nobel went to game theorists who helped redesign how kidneys are matched to patients in the United States.

The conceptual move at the heart of all of this — taking strategic interdependence seriously — is one of the most powerful ideas of the 20th century. By the end of this course, you will have it.

Where this came from

Game theory crystallized as a discipline in 1944, when mathematician John von Neumann and economist Oskar Morgenstern published Theory of Games and Economic Behavior — an 600-page book that introduced the formal apparatus of players, strategies, and payoffs and argued that economics should be rebuilt on this foundation. Von Neumann had proven the foundational "minimax theorem" for two-player zero-sum games back in 1928, but it was the 1944 book that launched the field.

The next leap came in 1950, when a 21-year-old Princeton graduate student named John Nash extended the analysis beyond zero-sum games to a general notion of equilibrium that now bears his name. We'll meet the Nash equilibrium formally in Chapter 4. Nash's work earned him the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics — and became the subject of the film A Beautiful Mind.

Exercise 1.1 Easy

For each of the following, identify the players, the strategies available to each player, and (in words) what determines the payoffs:

(a) Two roommates each decide whether to wash the dishes tonight. (b) Three firms simultaneously set prices for an identical product. (c) A chess match between two grandmasters.

Solution

(a) Players: the two roommates. Strategies for each: {wash, don't wash}. Payoffs: each prefers clean dishes but dislikes doing the washing; the worst case is washing while the other free-rides, the best is the other washing while you relax. (This has prisoner's-dilemma structure — see Chapter 2.)

(b) Players: the three firms. Strategies for each: any price ≥ 0 (a continuum, not just two options). Payoffs: each firm's profit depends on its own price and the prices of the other two, since customers compare.

(c) Players: the two grandmasters. Strategies: complete contingent plans specifying a move for every possible board position (an astronomically large set). Payoffs: +1 for a win, 0 for a draw, −1 for a loss (or any monotonic transformation). This is a sequential, zero-sum game.

Exercise 1.2 Medium

In the Anna–Ben study game from this chapter, suppose Anna believes there is a 50% chance Ben studies Together and a 50% chance he studies Alone. Compute Anna's expected payoff from each of her two strategies, and determine her best response.

Solution

Recall Anna's payoffs: if she studies Together, she gets 90 (if Ben is Together) or 40 (if Ben is Alone). If she studies Alone, she gets 75 (if Ben Together) or 75 (if Ben Alone).

Expected payoff from Together: 0.5 × 90 + 0.5 × 40 = 45 + 20 = 65.

Expected payoff from Alone: 0.5 × 75 + 0.5 × 75 = 75.

Since 75 > 65, Anna's best response to a 50/50 belief is to study Alone. Note how this differs from her best response when she's certain Ben studies Together (where Together wins, 90 > 75): beliefs matter. The riskiness of "Together" — the chance of wasting time waiting — makes "Alone" safer when Ben's behavior is uncertain.

Exercise 1.3 Medium

At what probability p that Ben studies Together is Anna exactly indifferent between her two strategies? (This is called Anna's indifference point.)

Solution

Let p = probability Ben studies Together. Anna's expected payoffs:

Together: 90p + 40(1−p) = 40 + 50p.
Alone: 75p + 75(1−p) = 75.

Set equal: 40 + 50p = 75 → 50p = 35 → p = 0.7.

So if Anna believes Ben studies Together with probability greater than 70%, she should study Together; below 70%, she should study Alone; at exactly 70%, she's indifferent. This kind of threshold calculation is the workhorse of mixed-strategy analysis in Chapter 10.

Exercise 1.4 Hard

Consider the claim: "Common knowledge of rationality is the same as both players being rational." Construct a short scenario with two players where both are rational, but rationality is not common knowledge, and explain how the predicted behavior could differ from the common-knowledge case.

Solution

Consider two drivers approaching the uncontrolled intersection from the chapter's opening. Both are rational (each wants to avoid a crash and minimize delay). But suppose Driver A does not believe Driver B is rational — A thinks B might be drunk or distracted and could plow through without slowing.

A rational A, believing B is irrational, will slow down to avoid a crash — even though if A knew B were rational and reasoning symmetrically, they might have coordinated on a smoother passing pattern.

The point: rationality alone tells each player to optimize given beliefs, but it's common knowledge of rationality that lets each player assume the other is reasoning the same way — enabling the recursive "I think that you think that I think…" that produces coordinated equilibrium predictions. Without it, a rational player may hedge against the possibility of an irrational opponent, and the outcome shifts. This is exactly why behavioral game theory (Chapter 13) matters: when rationality isn't common knowledge in practice, predictions change.

The Prisoner's Dilemma

24 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

The prisoner's dilemma is the most famous game in the world — and the most disturbing. It shows that two perfectly rational people, each pursuing their own interest, can end up worse off than if they had been able to coordinate. It's the formal heart of why arms races happen, why countries struggle to limit emissions, and why two competing companies might both spend millions on advertising they'd rather not pay for.

The story

Two suspects are hauled in for a serious crime. The police can convict each on a minor charge, but they lack the evidence to prove the big one — unless somebody talks. So the detectives split the pair into separate interrogation rooms, and make each the very same offer.

"Confess and testify against your partner, and you'll go free if they stay silent. If you both confess, we'll have you on the major charge but I'll get you a reduced sentence — five years each. If you both stay silent, the best we can do is the minor charge — one year each. But if your partner confesses while you stay silent, you take the full ten years."

Each prisoner has to decide what to do without knowing what the other will do. Their incentives are formally identical. What's the rational choice?

Let's lay out the payoffs. Since both prisoners want to minimize jail time, we'll write payoffs as the negative of years (so −10 is the worst, 0 is the best). Or — slightly more cheerfully — we can flip it and write payoffs as years saved relative to the maximum sentence. Either way works; the structure of the game doesn't change. We'll use the second convention since it makes "higher number = better" (which matches the rest of the course).

That's the dilemma: the rational outcome is worse for both than mutual cooperation would have been.

Solving it: dominant strategies

To analyze the game, take Prisoner 1's perspective and ask: "What should I do?" Notice that Prisoner 1 doesn't know what Prisoner 2 will do, so we'll consider both cases:

Case A: Suppose Prisoner 2 stays silent. Then Prisoner 1's options are:

  • Stay silent → payoff 3 (top-left cell, row payoff)
  • Confess → payoff 5 (bottom-left cell, row payoff)

Confessing is better.

Case B: Suppose Prisoner 2 confesses. Then Prisoner 1's options are:

  • Stay silent → payoff 0 (top-right cell, row payoff)
  • Confess → payoff 1 (bottom-right cell, row payoff)

Confessing is better.

So regardless of what Prisoner 2 does, Prisoner 1 is better off confessing. Confess yields a higher payoff in both possible cases. The same logic applies to Prisoner 2 by symmetry. Both will confess. The outcome is (1, 1): each gets five years.

Confess is what we call a strictly dominant strategy — a strategy that's strictly better than every alternative, no matter what the other player does.

Key idea

Both prisoners reason their way to confessing — and both end up with five years, when staying silent together would have given them one year each. Individually rational choices produced a collectively terrible result. That gap between what's smart for each and what's good for both is the whole point of the prisoner's dilemma.

Definition
Strictly dominant strategy

A strategy s is strictly dominant for player i if, for every possible strategy combination of the other players, s gives player i a strictly higher payoff than any of player i's other strategies.

When a player has a strictly dominant strategy, predicting her behavior is easy: she'll play it. Doing anything else would mean accepting a lower payoff than she could guarantee. A rational player won't do that.

Why this is unsettling

Both players, acting rationally on their dominant strategies, end up at (1, 1) — five years each. But both would have preferred (3, 3) — one year each. Individual rationality leads to a collectively worse outcome. This is the prisoner's dilemma.

The general structure

The prisoner's dilemma isn't really about prisoners. The same game shows up everywhere. Let's strip away the story and look at what makes a game a prisoner's dilemma.

A two-player simultaneous game with two strategies each — call them Cooperate and Defect — is a prisoner's dilemma if the payoffs satisfy two conditions:

The two PD conditions
Condition 1 (defection dominates):
Defect > Cooperate when the other plays Cooperate
Defect > Cooperate when the other plays Defect

Condition 2 (mutual cooperation beats mutual defection):
Cooperate-Cooperate > Defect-Defect (for both players)

// Together: defection is dominant, but the dominant outcome is worse for both

Game theorists often label PD payoffs with letters: T (temptation), R (reward), P (punishment), S (sucker). The standard PD has T > R > P > S. Mapped to our prisoner game:

  • T (temptation) = 5 — payoff for defecting while opponent cooperates
  • R (reward) = 3 — payoff for mutual cooperation
  • P (punishment) = 1 — payoff for mutual defection
  • S (sucker) = 0 — payoff for cooperating while opponent defects

Anytime you encounter a strategic situation with this T > R > P > S ordering, you have a prisoner's dilemma. The arms race example is one: arm yourself (defect) is dominant for each country, but mutual disarmament (cooperate) would be better for both. Let us record the formal definition and then prove the result that gives the dilemma its bite.

Definition 2.1 Prisoner's dilemma

A symmetric two-player game with strategy set $S_i = {C, D}$ for each player is a prisoner's dilemma if the payoffs to a player from her own action against the opponent's action satisfy

$$T > R > P > S,$$

where $T = u(D, C)$ (temptation), $R = u(C, C)$ (reward), $P = u(D, D)$ (punishment), and $S = u(C, D)$ (sucker). The first argument is the player's own action; the second is the opponent's.

From this single ordering, two facts follow. First, $D$ strictly dominates $C$ (since $T > R$ against a cooperator and $P > S$ against a defector), so by Proposition 1.4 every rational player defects, and $(D, D)$ is the unique outcome of dominant-strategy play. Second — and this is the dilemma — that outcome is Pareto-inefficient.

Proposition 2.2 The dominant-strategy outcome is Pareto-inefficient

In any prisoner's dilemma, the unique dominant-strategy outcome $(D, D)$ is Pareto-dominated by the mutual-cooperation outcome $(C, C)$: both players strictly prefer $(C, C)$ to $(D, D)$.

At $(D, D)$ each player receives $P$. At $(C, C)$ each player receives $R$. By the defining ordering of the prisoner's dilemma, $R > P$. Hence both players strictly prefer $(C, C)$ to $(D, D)$, which is precisely the statement that $(C, C)$ Pareto-dominates $(D, D)$. Since rational play yields $(D, D)$ (each player has a strictly dominant strategy to defect, by Proposition 1.4), the dominant-strategy outcome is Pareto-inefficient. ∎

This proposition is the entire moral of the chapter in one line: individually rational play (D, D) is collectively dominated by (C, C). The gap between $R$ and $P$ measures exactly how much both players lose by being unable to cooperate. Everything in Chapter 3 — repetition, reputation, enforcement — is an attempt to close that gap.

Real-world prisoner's dilemmas

Climate policy

Each country prefers that other countries cut emissions while it keeps polluting (free-riding on cleaner air without paying the economic cost). If everyone reasons this way, no one cuts. The Paris Agreement, the Kyoto Protocol, and dozens of other climate negotiations are essentially attempts to escape the prisoner's-dilemma logic of unilateral free-riding.

Advertising arms races

Coca-Cola and Pepsi each spend billions on advertising. If both stopped advertising, they'd both keep most of their market share (consumers don't suddenly switch brands when ads disappear) and pocket the savings. But each company knows that if it stops while the other continues, it loses market share. So both keep spending. T > R > P > S, applied to ad budgets.

Studying for the curve

In a class graded on a strict curve, students collectively have an incentive not to over-study (everyone could spend less time and get the same letter grades, since only relative performance matters). But each individual student has an incentive to study a little more than the others — gaining a slight edge. Everyone reasoning this way drives up study hours without anyone's grade improving. PD again.

Example 2.1
Two firms picking prices

Two pharmacies compete for the same town. Each can charge $10 (high price) or $7 (low price). Demand at each pharmacy depends on its price relative to the rival's:

  • Both charge $10: each earns $50,000/year (industry-friendly outcome)
  • Both charge $7: each earns $30,000/year (price war)
  • One charges $7 while rival charges $10: the cheaper one captures most customers and earns $70,000; the expensive one earns $20,000

Question: Is this a prisoner's dilemma?

Answer: Map to T-R-P-S. Cooperating means "charge $10" (the joint-profitable outcome). Defecting means "charge $7" (cutting price to grab market share).

  • T = 70 (defect while rival cooperates): yes, > everything else
  • R = 50 (mutual cooperation): > P
  • P = 30 (mutual defection): > S
  • S = 20 (cooperate while rival defects)

T (70) > R (50) > P (30) > S (20). Yes, this is a prisoner's dilemma. Both pharmacies will end up at $7, earning $30K each, even though they'd both prefer $10 each at $50K. This is exactly why antitrust laws exist — to prevent firms from formally agreeing to "cooperate" by fixing prices high.

Why doesn't the obvious solution work?

Reading the above, you might think: "Surely the prisoners just need to make a deal beforehand. They both promise to stay silent. Problem solved."

The trouble is that the deal isn't enforceable in the moment of decision. When Prisoner 1 is sitting alone in the interrogation room, the past promise doesn't change the structure of the game. Right now, no matter what Prisoner 2 promised, Prisoner 1's payoffs are unchanged. Confessing still yields more years of freedom regardless. So Prisoner 1 confesses. And Prisoner 2, reasoning identically, confesses too.

The promise can only work if there's some mechanism to enforce it — a binding contract, an external authority, a reputation system that punishes promise-breakers in future interactions. We'll explore each of these escape routes:

  • Repeated play (Chapter 3): if you'll meet your opponent again, defection today might cost you future cooperation. This single change unlocks an enormous toolkit for sustaining cooperation.
  • External enforcement: laws, courts, regulators. The government punishes you if you break a contract, changing the payoffs.
  • Reputation: even with strangers, if your behavior is observed by others who'll interact with you later, defection has long-run costs.

Each of these escape routes changes the game. If you can change the game, you can escape the dilemma. If you can't — if the game is genuinely one-shot, anonymous, and unenforceable — then rational players are stuck with the bad outcome.

Pareto efficiency: the cell that should have happened

We need one more concept to talk precisely about why the PD is bad. An outcome is Pareto efficient if there's no other outcome that makes at least one player strictly better off without making any player worse off. An outcome is Pareto inefficient if such a better outcome exists.

Definition
Pareto efficient

An outcome is Pareto efficient if you cannot make any player better off without making at least one other player worse off. Outcomes that fail this test are Pareto inefficient — there's a strictly better outcome available for everyone (or at least for some, harming none).

The mutual-defection outcome (1, 1) in the prisoner's dilemma is Pareto inefficient: both players would be strictly better off at (3, 3), the mutual-cooperation outcome. Yet rational play still leads to (1, 1). The PD is the canonical example of how individual rationality produces a Pareto-inefficient outcome — a situation where everyone agrees the result is bad, but no one can unilaterally fix it.

This is the conceptual punch of the prisoner's dilemma: it shows that "everyone acted rationally" and "the outcome was bad for everyone" are not contradictory. Rationality and welfare are not the same thing.

Where this came from

The prisoner's dilemma was devised in 1950 by mathematicians Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher while working at the RAND Corporation — the Cold War think tank that funded game theory research because of its potential application to nuclear strategy. Flood and Dresher were investigating whether people playing a non-cooperative, non-zero-sum game would converge to the outcome John Nash had just defined. In an early experiment, they had two colleagues play the game 100 times in a row and recorded the results (published as a RAND report, Flood 1952).

The now-famous story of two prisoners offered a plea bargain was not part of the original formulation. It was added by Princeton mathematician Albert W. Tucker, who needed an accessible way to explain Flood and Dresher's abstract payoff matrix to an audience of psychologists at Stanford in 1950. Tucker's narrative was so vivid that the name stuck — and the dilemma became, by some counts, the single most-studied game in the social sciences, with thousands of papers devoted to it.

Exercise 2.3 Medium

In the pharmacy pricing example (Example 2.1), the regulator imposes a $10 price floor, pushing both firms to the (50K, 50K) outcome. Consumers, however, were better off under the price war. Explain who wins and who loses from the price floor, and connect this to why antitrust law generally prohibits firms from achieving the cooperative outcome.

Solution

The price floor moves the firms from mutual defection ($7 each, earning $30K) to mutual cooperation ($10 each, earning $50K). The firms win — their combined profit rises from $60K to $100K.

But the firms' "cooperation" here means charging consumers higher prices. Consumers lose: they now pay $10 instead of $7 for the same product. The surplus that was flowing to consumers under competition is now captured by the firms.

This is the crucial subtlety about the prisoner's dilemma: whether escaping it is good depends on whose perspective you take. For the two firms, the price war is a dilemma they'd love to escape. For society, the price war is the desirable outcome — it's competition working as intended. This is exactly why antitrust law (e.g., the Sherman Act) makes price-fixing illegal: it prevents firms from "solving" their prisoner's dilemma at consumers' expense. The PD is socially valuable here precisely because it keeps prices low.

Exercise 2.4 Hard

Define a strictly dominant strategy formally using mathematical notation. Then prove that if a player has a strictly dominant strategy, that strategy is also their unique best response to every strategy of the opponent.

Solution

Formal definition. Let player i have strategy set S_i and payoff function u_i. Write s_{-i} for a strategy profile of all players other than i. A strategy s*_i ∈ S_i is strictly dominant if:

u_i(s*_i, s_{-i}) > u_i(s'_i, s_{-i}) for all s'_i ≠ s*_i and all s_{-i}.

Proof that it's the unique best response to every opponent profile. Fix any opponent profile s_{-i}. By the definition above, u_i(s*_i, s_{-i}) > u_i(s'_i, s_{-i}) for every other strategy s'_i. A best response to s_{-i} is, by definition, a strategy that maximizes u_i(·, s_{-i}). Since s*_i yields strictly more than every alternative, it is the unique maximizer — hence the unique best response. Because s_{-i} was arbitrary, this holds for every opponent profile. ∎

This is why dominant-strategy reasoning is so powerful: a player with a strictly dominant strategy doesn't need any beliefs about the opponent. The optimal move is the same no matter what. This is also the deepest reason the prisoner's dilemma is so robust — both players can solve it independently without coordinating or even communicating.

Repeated Games & the Roots of Cooperation

28 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Chapter 2 left us with a puzzle: rational players in a prisoner's dilemma end up worse off than if they could cooperate. The dilemma was real and unsolvable — for a single round. But most strategic interactions in real life aren't single rounds. You see your colleagues every weekday, your suppliers every quarter, your spouse every evening. When the game repeats, something profound changes: cooperation becomes possible without changing the underlying payoffs.

This chapter shows how. We'll see why the future "casts a shadow" on present decisions, why the difference between a finite and an infinite horizon matters enormously, and why a strategy as simple as "do unto others as they did unto you last time" turns out to be surprisingly hard to beat.

Think of the little café you visit every morning. The barista could short-change you, or hand you a stale pastry, and pocket the difference — a tiny win today. But they never do. Why? Because they'll see you again tomorrow, and the next day, and the day after. One sharp move would cost them a regular. The simple fact that you'll both be back quietly enforces honesty that no contract ever spelled out.

That is the entire magic of repetition. The one-shot prisoner's dilemma had no escape. But repeat it — let the players meet again and again — and cooperation can hold itself together, with no change to the payoffs at all. Let's see exactly when, and why.

Now imagine the two players are going to play this same game for many rounds in a row, accumulating payoffs as they go. After each round, both players see what the other did. Then they play again.

With one round, defection is dominant — Chapter 2 proved this. With many rounds, the analysis changes because each round affects what happens next. If you defect now, your opponent might retaliate later, costing you future payoffs. The future, in other words, "casts a shadow" backward onto today's decision.

But this only works if the future is uncertain. If both players know exactly when the game ends, a paradox emerges that we have to address first.

A formal framework

Before analyzing repeated play, let us set up the machinery precisely. We will reuse this notation throughout the rest of the book, so it is worth stating carefully.

A stage game is the one-shot game that gets repeated. Formally:

Definition 3.1 Stage game

A finite stage game is a triple $G = \langle N, (S_i)_{i\in N}, (u_i)_{i\in N}\rangle$ where $N = {1, \ldots , n}$ is the set of players, $S_i$ is the finite set of pure strategies (actions) available to player $i$, and $u_i : S \to \mathbb{R}$ is player $i$'s payoff function, with $S = S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n$ the set of pure-strategy profiles.

For the prisoner's dilemma above, $N = {1, 2}$, each $S_i = {C, D}$, and $u_i$ is given by the matrix, e.g. $u_1(C, D) = 0$, $u_1(D, C) = 5$.

Now we repeat $G$. Let $t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ index time periods. In each period the stage game is played, and both players observe the full history of past action profiles before choosing again.

Definition 3.2 History and strategy in a repeated game

A history of length $t$ is a sequence $h^t = (a^0, a^1, \ldots , a^{t-1})$ of realized action profiles, where each $a^k \in S$. Let $H^t$ be the set of all length-$t$ histories, with $H^0 = {\varnothing }$ (the empty history at the start). A (pure) strategy for player $i$ in the repeated game is a function

$$\sigma _i : \bigcup_{t\geq 0} H^t \to S_i$$

that specifies, for every possible history, which action player $i$ takes next. A strategy is therefore a complete contingent plan, not a single action.

This definition is worth pausing on. A strategy in a repeated game is not simply "cooperate" or "defect" — it is a rule that maps every possible past into a present action. Tit-for-Tat, which we will study shortly, is one such function: it reads the opponent's most recent action out of the history and echoes it. The space of strategies in even a modestly long repeated game is astronomically large, which is part of why repeated games are so much richer than their stage games.

Aggregating payoffs across periods

A player who receives a stream of stage payoffs $(\pi ^0, \pi ^1, \pi ^2, \ldots )$ needs a way to value the whole stream as a single number. Future payoffs are discounted by a factor $\delta \in [0, 1)$, capturing both impatience and the possibility that the game ends.

Definition 3.3 Discounted payoff

Given a discount factor $\delta \in [0, 1)$ and a stream of stage payoffs $(\pi ^t)_{t\geq 0}$, player $i$'s discounted sum is

$$U_i = \sum_{t=0}^{\infty } \delta ^t \pi ^t = \pi ^0 + \delta \pi ^1 + \delta ^2\pi ^2 + \cdots$$

Sometimes we use the normalized (average) discounted payoff $(1-\delta ) U_i$, which rescales so that a constant stream of $c$ per period is worth exactly $c$.

The discount factor has a useful probabilistic interpretation. Suppose that after each period the game continues with probability $p$ and ends with probability $1 - p$. Then a payoff one period from now is worth $p$ times its face value in expectation, two periods worth $p^2$, and so on — exactly the role $\delta$ plays. So $\delta$ can encode pure impatience, a probabilistic horizon, or both at once.

We will lean repeatedly on one standard fact about geometric series, which we record for reference.

Lemma 3.4 Geometric series

For any constant $c$ and any $\delta \in [0, 1)$,

$$\sum_{t=0}^{\infty } c\cdot \delta ^t = \frac{c}{1-\delta } \quad\text{and}\quad \sum_{t=1}^{\infty } c\cdot \delta ^t = \frac{c\delta }{1-\delta }.$$

(Standard; the first identity follows from $A - \delta A = c$ where $A$ is the sum, and the second by factoring out one $\delta$. We take it as given.)

With the framework in place, we can now resolve the question of what happens under a known finite horizon — and the answer is surprising.

The unraveling problem (finite horizon)

Suppose both players know they will play exactly 10 rounds and then stop. What happens?

Reason backward from the end. In round 10 (the last round), there's no future. Whatever you do in round 10 has zero effect on rounds 11, 12, etc. — because there are no rounds 11, 12, etc. So round 10 is effectively a one-shot prisoner's dilemma. Both players defect.

Now consider round 9. Both players know that whatever happens in round 9, they will both defect in round 10 anyway. So round 9's payoff matters, but round 10 is "already determined" by backward logic. Round 9 is also effectively one-shot. Both defect.

Continue this reasoning: round 8 unravels by the same logic — whatever happens, round 9 is already locked in as mutual defection, so round 8 is one-shot, and both defect. Round 7 unravels. Round 6. All the way back to round 1.

The conclusion is jarring: under a known finite horizon with rational players, mutual defection happens in every single round. The fact that the game repeats 10 times changes nothing. Let us state this precisely and prove it.

Proposition 3.5 Unraveling of the finitely repeated PD

Consider the prisoner's dilemma stage game with $T > R > P > S$ repeated a commonly known finite number of times $T_{\text{max}} < \infty$. Then in the unique subgame-perfect equilibrium, both players defect in every period $t = 0, 1, \ldots , T_{\text{max}}-1$, regardless of history.

We argue by induction on the period, working backward from the last. Let the final period be $t = T_{\text{max}} - 1$.

Base case. In the final period there are no future periods, so each player's continuation payoff is zero no matter what is played. Each player therefore faces exactly the one-shot stage game. Since $D$ strictly dominates $C$ in the stage game (Chapter 2), both players defect in period $T_{\text{max}} - 1$ after every history.

Inductive step. Suppose it has been established that in every period $t+1, t+2, \ldots , T_{\text{max}}-1$ both players defect after every history. Then the continuation play from period $t+1$ onward is fixed at mutual defection and is independent of what happens in period $t$. Consequently a player's period-$t$ action affects only her period-$t$ stage payoff, not her continuation payoff. She therefore again faces the one-shot stage game in period $t$, where $D$ strictly dominates. Hence both players defect in period $t$.

By induction, both players defect in every period. Uniqueness follows because strict dominance in each induced one-shot decision leaves no other best response. ∎

Why "unraveling" matters

The proof's engine is that the last period has no shadow of the future, so it collapses to one-shot play; this then strips the shadow from the second-to-last period, and so on. The intuition that "we'll cooperate to build a relationship" requires that the relationship doesn't have a fixed end-date that both players see clearly. The moment a defined ending exists and is common knowledge, cooperation in the last round collapses, which makes the second-to-last round collapse, and the whole edifice falls.

This is why repeated cooperation in real life requires either an infinite horizon, a probabilistic horizon (you might play again, you don't know for sure), or some other source of uncertainty about the endgame. (We saw in the Chapter 2 exercises and will revisit in Chapter 13 that introducing a small probability that the opponent is a "naive cooperator" can also break the unraveling — the Kreps–Milgrom–Roberts–Wilson reputation result.)

Infinite (or indefinite) repetition

Suppose the players will play indefinitely — there's no last round, or at least no known last round. Maybe the game ends with some small probability after each round, but it could keep going. Now backward induction has nothing to start from. There's no "round 10" from which to unravel.

In this setting, cooperation can be sustained as a rational equilibrium. Here's the key idea:

If I can credibly threaten to punish you in future rounds for defecting now, you might choose not to defect — even though defecting today gives the higher one-shot payoff.

The credibility of the threat matters. We'll see how it works concretely with two strategies.

Tit-for-Tat: the simplest cooperative strategy

Tit-for-Tat (TFT) is a strategy with a simple rule:

  • Round 1: Cooperate.
  • Round n (for n > 1): Play whatever your opponent played in round n−1.

That's it. TFT cooperates on the first round, then mirrors. If you cooperated last round, TFT cooperates this round. If you defected last round, TFT defects this round.

The strategy was popularized by political scientist Robert Axelrod through a pair of famous computer tournaments around 1980. For the first tournament, Axelrod invited fourteen game theorists to submit strategies for an iterated prisoner's dilemma; the programs played each other (and a random strategy) in round-robin matches of exactly 200 rounds each, using the same R = 3, P = 1, T = 5, S = 0 payoffs we've been working with. TFT, submitted by mathematician and peace researcher Anatol Rapoport of the University of Toronto, was the simplest entry — just a few lines of code — and it won.

Why does TFT work so well? It has four properties Axelrod identified:

  1. Nice — never defects first. Against another cooperative strategy, TFT cooperates from round 1 forever.
  2. Retaliatory — punishes defection immediately. Doesn't let exploiters get away with it.
  3. Forgiving — punishes once and then resets. If you cooperate after being defected against, TFT cooperates back.
  4. Clear — easy for opponents to predict and adapt to. Strategies that are too complex confuse opponents into defecting permanently.

Worked example: TFT vs. TFT

When two TFT strategies play each other, the round-by-round play looks like this:

Example 3.1
TFT vs. TFT, ten rounds

Both players follow TFT. Round 1, both cooperate (TFT's opening move). Round 2, each plays what the other played last round — both cooperated, so both cooperate again. And on, and on.

Round  Player1  Player2  P1 score  P2 score
───────────────────────────────────────────
1.......C.........C..........3..........3
2.......C.........C..........3..........3

Worked example: TFT vs. Always-Defect

Now suppose TFT plays against an "Always-Defect" strategy (sometimes called Dax in the StrategyArena IPD tournament).

Example 3.2
TFT vs. AllD, ten rounds

Round 1: TFT cooperates (its opening move), AllD defects. TFT gets the sucker payoff (S = 0); AllD gets the temptation payoff (T = 5).

Round 2: TFT now copies AllD's last move — defection. AllD defects too. Both get the punishment payoff (P = 1).

Rounds 3-10: TFT keeps mirroring AllD, who keeps defecting. So both keep defecting forever.

Total payoff for TFT: 0 + 1×9 = 9.
Total payoff for AllD: 5 + 1×9 = 14.

AllD beats TFT in this head-to-head matchup. So why does TFT win tournaments?

Because tournaments aren't head-to-head — they're round-robin. Each strategy plays every other strategy. TFT's appeal is that it does extremely well against cooperative strategies (getting 30 against another TFT, vs. AllD's 14 against itself), while losing only modestly against pure defectors. Across many opponents of varying types, TFT racks up far more points than a pure defector ever can.

Notice this is exactly the result the StrategyArena Iterated PD Tournament arena reproduces — AllD wins individual matches but TFT and Grim-trigger tie for second place across the round-robin. (Mathematically: AllD = 148 points, TFT = 139, Grim = 139, AllC = 120.)

Axelrod ran a second tournament soon after, publishing the first round's results so that all 62 new entrants could try to design something that would beat TFT. This time, instead of a fixed 200 rounds, each match ended with a small probability after every round (so the horizon was genuinely uncertain — avoiding the unraveling problem we discuss below). Remarkably, TFT won again, beating even strategies specifically engineered to exploit it. Axelrod concluded that the properties that made TFT successful were robust, not a fluke of the first tournament's design.

Grim-trigger: the unforgiving strategy

A second cooperative strategy worth knowing is Grim-trigger: cooperate until the opponent defects even once, then defect forever after. No forgiveness. (In Axelrod's tournament, a grim strategy was submitted by economist James Friedman, whose 1971 work on "trigger strategies" first proved that such permanent-punishment threats could sustain cooperation in infinitely repeated games — a result we'll use below.)

Grim-trigger is more punishing than TFT. If your opponent defects in round 5, TFT will retaliate in round 6, but if you cooperate in round 6, TFT will cooperate again in round 7. Grim-trigger never returns to cooperation — one defection triggers permanent defection.

Why care about Grim-trigger? Because it provides the strongest possible threat for sustaining cooperation. If your opponent plays Grim-trigger and you defect once, you've forfeited all future cooperation. The future damage is large, which makes the threat very effective.

But Grim-trigger has a downside: it's unforgiving of mistakes. Suppose you accidentally defect in round 3 (maybe you misclicked, or there was static on the line). TFT punishes for one round and resumes cooperation; Grim-trigger condemns you to mutual defection for the rest of time. In real-world repeated interactions where mistakes happen, TFT's forgiveness is a feature, not a bug.

When does cooperation actually pay off?

We've seen that cooperation is possible in repeated games. But under what conditions is it profitable for a rational player to cooperate?

To answer this, we need the concept of a discount factor. Future payoffs aren't worth as much as present payoffs — partly because of impatience, partly because the future is uncertain. Game theorists model this with a number δ (delta) between 0 and 1, where δ is the value of receiving $1 next round expressed in today's terms.

So if δ = 0.9, a payoff of $10 next round is worth $9 today. Two rounds from now it's worth $8.10 (= 10 × 0.9 × 0.9). The "shadow of the future" gets longer when δ is larger.

With this in hand, here's the cooperation calculation. Suppose you're playing TFT against another TFT, considering whether to defect this round.

Should I defect today? (against a Grim-trigger opponent)
Option A — Cooperate forever (stay on the cooperative path):
Stream of payoffs: R, R, R, ... = 3, 3, 3, ...
Present value: 3 + 3δ + 3δ² + ... = 3 · 1/(1−δ) = 3/(1−δ)

Option B — Defect now, get punished forever after:
Today you defect against a cooperator: payoff T = 5
From tomorrow on, the opponent (Grim) defects forever, so you both defect: P, P, P, ... = 1, 1, 1, ...
Present value: 5 + 1·δ + 1·δ² + ... = 5 + δ/(1−δ)

// Cooperation is rational when Option A ≥ Option B

Let's solve that inequality step by step. Cooperation is sustainable when:

Solving for the threshold δ
3/(1−δ) ≥ 5 + δ/(1−δ)
// multiply every term by (1−δ), which is positive since δ < 1
3 ≥ 5(1−δ) + δ
3 ≥ 5 − 5δ + δ
3 ≥ 5 − 4δ
4δ ≥ 2
δ ≥ ½

The clean conclusion: if δ ≥ ½, cooperation is sustainable in the indefinitely-repeated PD with a Grim-trigger (or TFT-style) opponent. If δ < ½, you're better off defecting — the future just isn't valuable enough to discipline current behavior. The exact threshold depends on the specific payoff numbers (T, R, P, S), but the shape of the result is universal: there's always some critical discount factor above which cooperation can be sustained and below which it cannot. We now state the general result formally.

Theorem 3.6 Grim-trigger sustains cooperation (a folk theorem)

In the infinitely repeated prisoner's dilemma with stage payoffs $T > R > P > S$ and common discount factor $\delta \in [0, 1)$, the strategy profile in which both players play Grim-trigger — cooperate until any defection, then defect forever — is a subgame-perfect equilibrium if and only if

$$\delta \geq \frac{T - R}{T - P}.$$

Along this equilibrium path, both players cooperate in every period.

We check that no player can profitably deviate after any history, which is what subgame perfection requires. There are two kinds of subgame to consider.

(i) On the cooperative path (no defection has yet occurred). If player $i$ conforms, she earns $R$ this period and remains on the cooperative path, for a discounted payoff of

$$V_{\text{coop}} = R + \delta R + \delta ^2R + \cdots = \frac{R}{1-\delta }$$

by Lemma 3.4. If instead she defects this period, she earns $T$ today (her opponent still cooperates this period), but triggers permanent mutual defection thereafter, yielding $P$ in every subsequent period:

$$V_{\text{dev}} = T + \delta P + \delta ^2P + \cdots = T + \frac{\delta P}{1-\delta }.$$

Conforming is optimal iff $V_{\text{coop}} \geq V_{\text{dev}}$, i.e.

$$\frac{R}{1-\delta } \geq T + \frac{\delta P}{1-\delta }.$$

Multiplying through by $(1-\delta ) > 0$ and rearranging: $R \geq T(1-\delta ) + \delta P = T - \delta (T - P)$, hence $\delta (T - P) \geq T - R$, giving $\delta \geq \frac{T - R}{T - P}$.

(ii) Off the cooperative path (some defection has occurred). Both players' strategies prescribe permanent defection. Given that the opponent will defect in every future period regardless, a player's best response in the stage game is to defect (since $D$ strictly dominates), and no future consequence attaches to her choice because the opponent's behavior is already fixed at perpetual defection. So defecting forever is a best response — the punishment phase is itself an equilibrium, which is exactly what makes the threat credible and the equilibrium subgame-perfect.

Both conditions hold simultaneously precisely when $\delta \geq \frac{T - R}{T - P}$. ∎

For our running payoffs $T=5, R=3, P=1$, the threshold is $\frac{5-3}{5-1} = 2/4 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, recovering the number we computed by hand. Note the proof's crucial subtlety in part (ii): the punishment must itself be an equilibrium, or the threat to punish would be an empty bluff that a rational deviator would ignore. This is exactly the credibility requirement that Selten's subgame-perfect equilibrium was invented to capture, and it is why we needed both parts of the argument rather than just part (i).

This result is one of the most important in repeated game theory. It tells us that patience (high $\delta$) is a precondition for cooperation. Players who live entirely in the moment ($\delta$ below the threshold) cannot sustain cooperation even when they would both prefer it. And the threshold $\frac{T-R}{T-P}$ is intuitive: cooperation is easier to sustain when the one-period temptation gain $T - R$ is small relative to the total payoff range $T - P$.

Key idea

Cooperation in a repeated game runs on patience. If players value the future enough ($\delta \geq \frac{T-R}{T-P}$), the threat of losing future cooperation outweighs the one-time gain from cheating, and they cooperate. Value the future too little, and cooperation collapses — the future stops being able to discipline the present.

Real-world repeated games

Trade relationships

Two firms that buy and sell from each other monthly are in a repeated game. A supplier can deliver shoddy goods once for short-term profit, but the buyer will switch to a competitor. The shadow of future business disciplines current behavior. This is why long-term business relationships often build trust — and why one-off transactions with strangers are notoriously risky.

International relations

Countries that trade and diplomate frequently with each other have many "rounds" of interaction. Defection (e.g., a sudden trade war, a violation of treaty) is met with retaliation in subsequent rounds. The repeated structure is what makes international cooperation possible despite the absence of a global enforcer.

The flip side: when relationships end

Notice the unraveling logic from earlier. As a relationship approaches a known end — a CEO about to retire, a country about to lose superpower status, a divorcing couple — cooperation often breaks down. The shadow of the future shortens, δ effectively shrinks, and previously stable arrangements collapse. This is sometimes called the "endgame problem" in business — and it's a direct consequence of repeated-game theory.

Looking ahead

We've now seen two kinds of game: the simple one-shot PD where rationality forces a bad outcome, and the repeated PD where cooperation can emerge from the shadow of the future. In the next chapter (Chapter 4), we leave simultaneous play behind and turn to sequential games — games where one player moves before another, observes the move, and responds. These games introduce the concept of backward induction, which we already used informally in the unraveling argument above. In Chapter 4, we'll formalize it.

Where this came from

The idea that cooperation can be sustained in infinitely repeated games was "folk knowledge" among game theorists in the 1950s — widely understood but unpublished, which is why the central result is still called the folk theorem. The first rigorous published version came from economist James Friedman in 1971, who showed that "trigger strategies" (cooperate until someone defects, then punish) could support cooperative outcomes as equilibria of infinitely repeated games.

The notion of a credible threat — central to why punishment strategies work — was sharpened by Reinhard Selten's 1965 concept of subgame-perfect equilibrium, which we'll formalize in Chapter 4. Selten shared the 1994 Nobel Prize with John Nash and John Harsanyi.

Robert Axelrod's two computer tournaments (1980) and his book The Evolution of Cooperation (1984) brought these ideas to a wide audience and connected them to biology, showing that Tit-for-Tat-like reciprocity could explain cooperation among organisms with no capacity for conscious strategy at all. A later refinement by Nowak and Sigmund (1992) showed that when players occasionally make mistakes, a slightly forgiving variant — "Generous Tit-for-Tat" — outperforms strict TFT. That finding directly informs how the AI opponents in the StrategyArena arenas are calibrated.

Exercise 3.1 Easy

A payoff of 10 is received every round forever, starting today. With a discount factor δ = 0.8, what is the present value of this infinite stream? (Recall the geometric series formula: a + aδ + aδ² + ... = a/(1−δ) for 0 ≤ δ < 1.)

Small changes in patience produce large changes in the value of the future — which is exactly why δ is the master variable in repeated-game analysis.

Exercise 3.2 Medium

Repeat the chapter's cooperation-threshold derivation, but with payoffs T = 10, R = 6, P = 2, S = 0. Against a Grim-trigger opponent, find the minimum discount factor δ* above which cooperation is sustainable.

Solution

Cooperate-forever value: R/(1−δ) = 6/(1−δ).

Defect-now value: T + δ·P/(1−δ) = 10 + 2δ/(1−δ).

Cooperation sustainable when 6/(1−δ) ≥ 10 + 2δ/(1−δ). Multiply through by (1−δ):

6 ≥ 10(1−δ) + 2δ = 10 − 10δ + 2δ = 10 − 8δ.

So 8δ ≥ 4, giving δ* = ½ again. (It's a coincidence of these numbers that the threshold is the same as the chapter's example. The general formula is δ* = (T−R)/(T−P); here (10−6)/(10−2) = 4/8 = ½.)

Exercise 3.3 Medium

Derive the general threshold formula δ* = (T−R)/(T−P) used in the previous exercise's solution. Start from the condition that cooperate-forever beats defect-now-against-Grim.

Solution

Cooperation sustainable when:

R/(1−δ) ≥ T + δ·P/(1−δ).

Multiply by (1−δ) > 0:

R ≥ T(1−δ) + δP = T − Tδ + δP = T − δ(T − P).

Rearrange: δ(T − P) ≥ T − R, so δ ≥ (T − R)/(T − P).

Thus δ* = (T − R)/(T − P). This elegant formula shows cooperation is easier to sustain (lower threshold) when the temptation gain T − R is small relative to the total range T − P. A tiny temptation to cheat is easy to resist; a huge one requires very patient players.

Exercise 3.4 Hard

The chapter argues that a finitely repeated PD with a known endpoint unravels to all-defection. But real experimental subjects playing a 10-round PD usually cooperate for the first several rounds. Give two distinct reasons (drawing on ideas from this chapter and Chapter 1) why real behavior departs from the backward-induction prediction.

Solution

Several defensible answers; here are two strong ones.

Reason 1 — Rationality is not common knowledge. Backward unraveling requires not just that both players are rational, but that this is common knowledge (Chapter 1). If I think there's even a small chance you're a "naive cooperator" who plays Tit-for-Tat, it can be rational for me to cooperate early to build a profitable cooperative relationship, defecting only near the end. This is the Kreps–Milgrom–Roberts–Wilson "gang of four" reputation argument (1982): a little uncertainty about types breaks the unraveling.

Reason 2 — Payoffs aren't purely monetary. Real people often have preferences over fairness, reciprocity, or their own self-image as a cooperative person (Chapter 13). If "cooperating" yields psychological payoff beyond the monetary reward, the effective game may not even be a prisoner's dilemma — mutual cooperation might be a genuine equilibrium. The backward-induction prediction assumes the monetary payoffs are the payoffs; if they're not, the prediction doesn't apply.

Sequential Games & the Art of Looking Ahead

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Every game so far has been simultaneous — players choosing at the same instant, blind to each other. But so much of real strategy unfolds in turns. You make a move, your rival sees it, and then responds. The crucial new skill is looking ahead and reasoning back: figuring out what you should do now by first working out how everyone will respond later.

Picture two firms. One already owns the market — call it the incumbent. The other, an entrant, is deciding whether to muscle in. The incumbent glares across the table and growls: "Enter my market and I will start a price war so brutal we'll both bleed. Don't try me."

Should the entrant be scared off? That depends on a single question: if the entrant actually did walk in, would the incumbent really start that ruinous price war — or would it grumble and make room? The whole chapter is, in a sense, about how to answer that question rigorously. The answer will turn out to be: the threat is usually a bluff, and there's a clean way to prove it.

To get there we need a way to draw games that happen in sequence, a method for solving them, and a sharp idea of what makes a threat believable. Let's build all three — starting with a picture.

Drawing a sequential game: the game tree

A simultaneous game fits in a payoff matrix. A sequential game is better drawn as a game tree: you read it top to bottom, each branch is a choice, and the numbers at the bottom are what each player walks away with. Here is the entry game from the story.

Figure 4.1 · The entry game
The Entrant (E) moves first: stay out, or enter. If it enters, the Incumbent (I) chooses to fight a price war or accommodate. Each leaf — an endpoint of the tree where the game is over — shows the payoff pair $(\text{Entrant}, \text{Incumbent})$, with the Entrant's payoff first. Solid green branches are the choices each player actually makes; dashed branches are the roads not taken. We'll see why these are the choices in a moment.

Reading the tree: the entrant starts at the top node — a decision point, drawn as a circle and labeled with whoever moves there (here, E for Entrant). If it chooses "Stay Out," the game ends immediately at the payoff $(0, 4)$ — the entrant gets nothing new, the incumbent keeps its comfortable monopoly worth $4$. If the entrant chooses "Enter," play moves down to the incumbent's node (labeled I), who then picks Fight or Accommodate.

Notice what the tree makes obvious that a matrix would hide: the incumbent only ever has to decide after seeing that entry happened. Its choice is a response, not a simultaneous guess. That ordering is the whole point.

Definition 4.1 Extensive-form game (perfect information)

A finite extensive-form game with perfect information is a game tree: a set of players, decision nodes each assigned to the player who moves there, branches representing the available actions, and a payoff for every player at each terminal node (leaf). Perfect information means that whenever a player moves, she knows exactly where she is in the tree — she has seen every move made before hers.

"Perfect information" is the key phrase. Chess, checkers, and tic-tac-toe have it: the whole board is visible, nothing is secret. Poker does not — you can't see your opponent's cards. This chapter lives in the world of perfect information, where the method we're about to learn works flawlessly. (Chapters 8 and 9 take on hidden information.)

Backward induction: solve the end first

How do you solve a game tree? You don't start at the top. You start at the bottom — at the very last decision — figure out what that player will do, and then work your way back up. The trick has a name.

Key idea

To solve a sequential game, reason backward. Find the last player's best move first. Then, knowing how they'll respond, work out the second-to-last player's best move. Keep folding back until you reach the start.

Let's do it on the entry game. The last decision belongs to the incumbent, and only happens if the entrant has entered. So put yourself in the incumbent's chair at that moment. Entry has already happened. Your choices:

  • Fight the price war → you get $1$
  • Accommodate and share → you get $2$

Two is bigger than one. So you accommodate. The threatened price war? You wouldn't actually start it — it hurts you more than sharing does. That's the green "Accommodate" branch in Figure 4.1, and the dashed "Fight" branch is the bluff you'd never carry out.

Now fold that knowledge back up to the entrant's decision. The entrant, looking ahead, realizes the incumbent will accommodate. So the entrant's real choice is:

  • Stay Out → get $0$
  • Enter (knowing the incumbent then accommodates) → get $2$

Two beats zero, so the entrant enters. The whole game resolves to Enter, then Accommodate, with payoffs $(2, 2)$. The scary threat changed nothing.

Definition 4.2 Backward induction

Backward induction solves a game tree by starting at the decision nodes nearest the leaves (the endpoints) and working toward the root (the starting node at the top). At each node, the player chooses the action giving the highest payoff, given the choices already worked out for everything that comes after. The chosen actions, taken together, form one complete strategy for each player.

Does this always work? For any finite game with perfect information, yes — and that fact is the first great theorem of game theory, proved by Ernst Zermelo in 1913 in a paper about chess.

Theorem 4.3 Existence (Zermelo–Kuhn)

Every finite extensive-form game with perfect information can be solved by backward induction, yielding at least one equilibrium in which every player's planned move is optimal at every point. If no player is ever exactly tied between two outcomes, the solution is unique.

The argument is by induction on the height $h$ of the tree — the number of moves in its longest branch.

Base case $(h = 1)$. A height-one tree is a single decision node whose branches all lead straight to payoffs. The player faces a finite list of numbers and picks the largest; a finite list always has a maximum, so an optimal move exists. (If the largest value is unique, so is the choice.)

Inductive step. Assume the claim holds for every perfect-information tree of height at most $h-1$. Take a tree of height $h$ with root $r$. Each branch out of $r$ leads to a smaller tree of height at most $h-1$, which by assumption can be solved, producing a definite payoff. Replace each of those subtrees by its solution payoff. Now the player at $r$ again faces a finite list of payoffs and picks her best. Combining her choice with the already-solved subtrees gives a full solution. Uniqueness holds whenever each of these "pick the largest" steps has no ties. ∎

Zermelo's point about chess follows: chess is finite and has perfect information, so it has a determinate value — either White can force a win, Black can, or both can force a draw. We just can't compute which, because the tree is unimaginably large.

Where this came from

Ernst Zermelo — famous to mathematicians for his set theory axioms — wrote the first-ever game theory paper in 1913, and its subject was chess. He proved chess has a definite "solved" value even though nobody could ever compute it. The modern framework of game trees was set down by Harold Kuhn in 1953, and the idea of credible threats that closes this chapter came from Reinhard Selten in 1965, who later shared the 1994 Nobel Prize with John Nash.

The empty threat, made precise

Come back to that growled threat: "Enter and I'll fight." We've seen the incumbent wouldn't actually fight. But here's the subtle part that trips people up — "the entrant stays out because it fears a fight" is technically a Nash equilibrium.

Why? Recall a Nash equilibrium just requires each player's strategy to be a best response to the other's — that is, given what the other player is doing, neither could get a higher payoff by switching to a different strategy. Suppose the incumbent's strategy is "fight if entry happens" and the entrant's is "stay out." Given the incumbent threatens to fight, staying out (payoff $0$) beats entering into a fight (payoff $-1$). And given the entrant stays out, the incumbent's threat is never tested, so it costs nothing to make. Neither player can do better by unilaterally switching. That's a Nash equilibrium — and it predicts the entrant is successfully scared off.

But it relies on a bluff. The fix is a stronger requirement: a player's plan must be optimal not just on the path that actually happens, but at every point in the tree — including the ones we expect never to reach. That requirement is called subgame-perfect equilibrium.

Definition 4.4 Subgame-perfect equilibrium

A subgame is any node of the tree together with everything below it — a smaller game in its own right. A strategy profile — one complete strategy for each player, specifying what they do at every node — is a subgame-perfect equilibrium (SPE) if it is a Nash equilibrium in every subgame, including the ones off the path actually played.

This is exactly what kills the bluff. Look at the subgame that starts right after the entrant enters. In that little game, the incumbent alone chooses, and fighting ($1$) is worse than accommodating ($2$). So "fight" is not a Nash equilibrium of that subgame — which means any overall plan that includes "fight here" fails to be subgame-perfect. The bluff is ruled out automatically.

Key idea

A credible threat is one you'd actually want to carry out when the moment comes. Subgame-perfect equilibrium keeps only credible threats — and backward induction finds exactly these equilibria.

That last sentence is the second key theorem: backward induction and subgame perfection are two views of the same thing.

Proposition 4.5 Backward induction = subgame perfection

In a finite perfect-information game, a strategy profile is a subgame-perfect equilibrium if and only if it is a backward-induction solution. The "Nash but not credible" profiles are exactly the ones that prescribe a suboptimal move at some node that play never reaches.

(Backward-induction solution ⟹ subgame-perfect.) Take any subgame, rooted at a node $v$. Backward induction chose, at $v$ and everywhere below it, the payoff-maximizing action given optimal continuation. That is precisely a backward-induction solution of the subgame, and such a solution is a Nash equilibrium of it (no player can gain by changing a single move — the one-deviation principle — and in a finite game that rules out all profitable deviations). So the profile is Nash in every subgame: subgame-perfect.

(Subgame-perfect ⟹ backward-induction solution.) Take a node $v$ whose branches all lead to leaves. Its subgame is a one-move game, and an SPE must be Nash there, so the profile picks the best payoff at $v$ — the same as backward induction. Move up one level: given the optimal choices below, an SPE must again be optimal at each node. Proceeding up to the root, the profile matches backward induction at every node. ∎

One-deviation principle. The shortcut doing the work here: in a finite game, to confirm a plan is optimal you only need to check that no player gains by changing her move at a single node, holding the rest fixed. That turns an impossible search over all full strategies into a quick node-by-node check.

Turning a bluff into a real threat

Here's where it gets genuinely interesting. If the incumbent's problem is that fighting isn't worth it, what if the incumbent could change its own payoffs to make fighting worthwhile? Counterintuitively, deliberately tying your own hands can be a winning move.

Example 4.1
Building a factory you hope never to use

Suppose the incumbent, before the entrant decides, spends money building excess production capacity. This investment is wasted if it accommodates — but it makes a price war cheap to wage, raising the Fight payoff from $1$ to $3$ (while Accommodate stays at $2$).

Now redo the backward induction. After entry, the incumbent compares Fight $= 3$ against Accommodate $= 2$. This time it really would fight. Anticipating that, the entrant compares Enter (leading to a fight, payoff $-1$) against Stay Out (payoff $0$) — and stays out.

The incumbent keeps its monopoly without ever fighting, simply because the threat became believable. Thomas Schelling called this the strategic value of "burning your bridges": by destroying your own option to retreat, you make your commitment credible. Cortés scuttling his ships, a union publicly vowing never to accept less than 5%, a country writing an automatic-retaliation treaty — all the same move.

This is the deep lesson of sequential games: commitment is power, and sometimes the way to commit is to remove your own choices. We'll see the same logic drive bargaining in Chapter 6.

When looking ahead gives a strange answer: the centipede

Backward induction is logically airtight. But sometimes it predicts something that feels deeply wrong — and watching where it clashes with human behavior is one of the most illuminating things in all of game theory. Meet the centipede game.

Two players face a growing pile of money. On your turn you can Take — grab the larger share and end it — or Pass, which grows the pile but hands the next, larger decision to your opponent. Pass and the pie keeps growing for everyone. It seems obvious you should both keep passing and split a big pile. Backward induction says: take everything on the very first move. Who's right?

Figure 4.2 · A three-step centipede
Players alternate. At each node, Take ends the game at the payoff below; Pass moves right to the next node. Payoffs are $(\text{P1}, \text{P2})$. The green branch is the backward-induction choice: P1 takes immediately, at $(4, 1)$ — even though passing could reach the far richer $(8, 16)$.

Let's fold it back, right to left. At the last node, P1 chooses between Take $= 16$ and Pass $= 8$; she takes. Knowing that, at the middle node P2 chooses between Take $= 8$ and passing into P1's grab, which leaves P2 with only $4$; so P2 takes. Knowing that, at the first node P1 chooses between Take $= 4$ and passing into P2's grab, which leaves P1 with only $2$; so P1 takes at once.

The logic unravels from the end exactly like the finitely repeated prisoner's dilemma in Chapter 3. Each player, distrusting that the next will pass, grabs first. The "rational" outcome is the meager $(4, 1)$.

The puzzle

Real people almost never take at the first opportunity. In experiments they pass for several rounds, reaching the cooperative outcomes that backward induction declares unreachable — and often end up richer than "rational" players would. The math isn't wrong; one of its assumptions is. Either players aren't certain the other is a relentless maximizer, or the payoffs they care about aren't just the dollar amounts. We pick this apart in Chapter 13.

Wrapping up

You now have the two master tools of game theory side by side: Nash equilibrium for simultaneous games, and subgame-perfect equilibrium — found by backward induction — for sequential ones. The thread running through this chapter is credibility: a plan only counts if every move in it is one you'd actually want to make when you got there. Empty threats get pruned; credible commitments reshape the game. In Chapter 5 we put these tools to work on auctions, where a seller designs the rules and every bidder has to look ahead to what a given bid will really cost.

This is why firms sometimes make costly, seemingly wasteful commitments: the commitment's job is to be believed, not used.

Lengthen the centipede of Figure 4.2 by one node: after P1's last node, if P1 passes, P2 gets a final choice — Take $= (8, 16)$ or Pass $= (14, 14)$. (P1's Take at the previous node still pays $(16, 4)$.) Re-solve. Does the longer pie change anything?

Solution

At the new last node, P2 compares Take $= 16$ against Pass $= 14$: P2 takes $(8, 16)$.

At P1's node, Take $= 16$ vs Pass (P2 then takes, leaving P1 with $8$): P1 takes $(16, 4)$.

At P2's node, Take $= 8$ vs Pass (P1 then takes, leaving P2 with $4$): P2 takes.

At P1's first node, Take $= 4$ vs Pass (P2 then takes, leaving P1 with $2$): P1 takes immediately, $(4, 1)$. Adding length changes nothing — as long as each player prefers taking now to what they'd get after the next player takes, the whole thing collapses to the first move. The unravelling is brutally robust.

Exercise 4.4 Hard

Prove the one-deviation principle: in a finite perfect-information game, a strategy profile is subgame-perfect if and only if no player can gain by changing her action at a single node while keeping all her other actions fixed.

Solution

(⟹) If the profile is subgame-perfect, it's a Nash equilibrium in every subgame, so in particular no single-node change can be a profitable deviation in any subgame.

(⟸) Suppose no one-node change helps anyone. Argue by induction on subgame height. In a height-one subgame the only possible deviations are single-node changes, so the profile is optimal there. Assume the result for subgames of height up to $h-1$. In a height-$h$ subgame rooted at $v$, any deviation splits into a change at $v$ plus a deviation inside a successor subgame. By the inductive hypothesis the profile is already optimal inside every successor subgame, so the only way to possibly gain is to change the action at $v$ alone — and by assumption that doesn't help. So the profile is optimal at $v$, hence Nash in this subgame.

By induction it's Nash in every subgame — subgame-perfect. ∎ This principle is the engine of backward induction: it certifies that checking one move at a time is enough to certify the whole strategy.

Auctions & the Secrets of Bidding

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Auctions are everywhere — eBay listings, art houses, Treasury bonds, Google's ad slots, the wireless spectrum your phone runs on. They are also one of game theory's great success stories: the same tools we built for sequential games tell you exactly how much to bid, why honesty is sometimes your best move, and why "winning" can be the worst thing that happens to you.

You're at a charity auction, eyeing a signed guitar. In your heart you'd pay up to $300 for it — not a penny more. The bidding starts. Someone shouts $120. Now what? Do you bid your full $300 to be safe? Bid a little and hope? Wait and snipe at the end? Your answer should depend on one thing most people never think about: the rules of this particular auction. Change the rules, and the smart bid changes completely.

That last point is the heart of the chapter. There isn't one "right way to bid" — there's a right way to bid in each format, and the formats reward very different behavior. Let's meet them.

The four classic auction formats

Almost every auction you'll encounter is a variation on four basic designs. Two are open (bids are called out in the room) and two are sealed-bid (everyone submits one secret bid).

  • English auction (ascending, open): the price starts low and climbs as bidders top each other, until only one remains. This is the classic "going once, going twice" auction.
  • Dutch auction (descending, open): the price starts high and drops until someone shouts "mine!" — used for decades in the Dutch flower markets, where speed matters.
  • First-price sealed-bid: everyone submits one secret bid; the highest bidder wins and pays their own bid. Common in government contracts and construction tenders.
  • Second-price sealed-bid (the Vickrey auction): everyone submits one secret bid; the highest bidder wins but pays only the second-highest bid. This sounds strange — why would a seller leave money on the table? — but it has a beautiful property we'll prove shortly.

Before we can say how to bid, we need to pin down what a bidder actually wants. We'll assume each bidder has a private value — a personal worth they place on the item, known to themselves but not to others.

Definition 5.1 Private-value auction

In a private-value auction, each bidder $i$ has a value $v_i$ — the most they would willingly pay — known only to bidder $i$. A bidder who wins at price $p$ earns payoff (sometimes called surplus) $v_i - p$; a bidder who loses earns $0$. Each bidder wants to maximize their own expected surplus.

"Private value" means your worth for the item doesn't depend on what anyone else thinks it's worth — a painting you love is worth what you'd pay, regardless of the bidder next to you. (Later in the chapter we'll meet common-value auctions, where everyone is really after the same underlying worth — like an oil field — and the picture changes dramatically.)

The Vickrey auction: why honesty wins

Start with the second-price (Vickrey) auction, because it has the cleanest answer of any auction in this book. The question: in a second-price auction, how much should you bid? The answer is startling in its simplicity.

Key idea

In a second-price sealed-bid auction, bidding your true value is a weakly dominant strategy — it's at least as good as any other bid, no matter what everyone else does. You never need to guess, shade, or strategize. Just bid what the item is worth to you.

Recall from Chapter 2 that a weakly dominant strategy is one that does at least as well as any alternative against every possible play of the others (and sometimes strictly better). Let's see why truthful bidding has this property.

The intuition first. Your bid in a second-price auction does not set the price you pay — the other bidders set it (you pay the second-highest bid). Your bid only decides whether you win. So your bid is really just a threshold: "win for me whenever the going price is below this number." The honest threshold — your true value — is exactly the one that wins precisely when winning is profitable. Bid higher and you risk winning at a price above your value (a loss); bid lower and you risk missing a win you'd have profited from.

Theorem 5.2 Truthful bidding is weakly dominant (Vickrey, 1961)

In a second-price sealed-bid auction with private values, bidding $b_i = v_i$ is a weakly dominant strategy for every bidder: it yields a payoff at least as high as any other bid $b_i \neq v_i$, regardless of the bids submitted by the others.

Fix bidder $i$ with value $v = v_i$. Let $p$ denote the highest bid among all the other bidders. Bidder $i$ wins if and only if their bid exceeds $p$, and then pays $p$ (the second-highest bid overall). We compare truthful bidding $b = v$ against any deviation.

Case 1: overbidding $(b > v)$. The only outcomes that differ from truthful bidding occur when $p$ falls between the two bids, i.e. $v < p < b$ (roughly). There, overbidding wins the item at price $p > v$, giving payoff $v - p < 0$ — a loss — whereas truthful bidding loses and earns $0$. For every other value of $p$, the two bids give identical outcomes. So overbidding is never better and sometimes worse.

Case 2: underbidding $(b < v)$. The only outcomes that differ occur when $b < p < v$. There, underbidding loses and earns $0$, whereas truthful bidding wins at price $p < v$, giving payoff $v - p > 0$ — a gain forgone. For every other $p$, the outcomes are identical. So underbidding is never better and sometimes worse.

In both directions, deviating from $b = v$ can only leave bidder $i$ worse off or unchanged, for every possible $p$. Hence truthful bidding is weakly dominant. ∎

This is a genuinely remarkable result. In most games, optimal play depends on what you think your opponents will do. The Vickrey auction dissolves that problem: there's nothing to think about. Bid your value and walk away. That property — making honesty the easy choice — is why second-price logic underlies real systems from Google's ad auctions to the way some stock exchanges clear orders.

Where this came from

The economist William Vickrey worked out the second-price auction's honesty property in a now-famous 1961 paper. For this and related work on decisions under hidden information, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1996 — sadly, just three days before he died. The format now bears his name: the Vickrey auction. A nice irony, uncovered by later historians: second-price auctions had quietly been used by stamp collectors for decades before Vickrey "invented" them.

First-price auctions: the art of shading

Now switch to the first-price sealed-bid auction, where the winner pays their own bid. Here honesty is a terrible strategy. If you bid your true value and win, you pay exactly your value — your surplus is zero. You gained nothing! To profit, you must bid below your value. This deliberate underbidding is called bid shading.

But how much should you shade? Shade too little and you barely profit when you win; shade too much and you lose auctions you could have won. The answer balances these, and it depends on how many rivals you face. Let's work out the classic case.

Proposition 5.3 Equilibrium bidding in a first-price auction

Suppose $n$ bidders have private values drawn independently and uniformly from the interval $[0, 1]$. Then it is a symmetric equilibrium for each bidder with value $v$ to bid

$$b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}\, v.$$

Each bidder shades below their value by the factor $\frac{n-1}{n}$, and shades less as the number of competitors grows.

Assume every other bidder follows the increasing strategy $b(\cdot)$. A bidder with value $v$ considers bidding as if their value were $z$ — that is, submitting $b(z)$. They win only if all $n-1$ rivals have lower values, which (for uniform values on $[0,1]$) happens with probability $z^{n-1}$. Their expected payoff is

$$U(z) = (v - b(z))\, z^{n-1}.$$

Differentiate with respect to $z$ and set the derivative to zero (the first-order condition for the best choice of $z$):

$$-b'(z)\,z^{n-1} + (v - b(z))(n-1)z^{n-2} = 0.$$

In equilibrium a bidder should want to bid honestly "in $z$," i.e. the optimum is $z = v$. Substituting $z = v$ and dividing by $v^{n-2}$:

$$-b'(v)\,v + (v - b(v))(n-1) = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; v\,b'(v) + (n-1)b(v) = (n-1)v.$$

Try the linear form $b(v) = c\,v$, so $b'(v) = c$. Then $cv + (n-1)cv = (n-1)v$, giving $cn = n-1$, hence $c = \frac{n-1}{n}$. Therefore $b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n} v$. ∎

The formula captures a deep and intuitive truth about competition. Look at how the shading factor $\frac{n-1}{n}$ behaves as rivals multiply:

Figure 5.1 · How competition shrinks bid shading
Equilibrium bid $b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}v$ for different numbers of bidders $n$. With just two bidders you bid half your value; with ten you bid 90% of it. As $n$ grows, the bid lines climb toward the dashed truthful line $b = v$ — fierce competition forces you to bid closer and closer to your true worth, squeezing your surplus toward zero.

With two bidders you bid half your value ($\frac{1}{2}v$); with five you bid $\frac{4}{5}v$; with a hundred you bid $\frac{99}{100}v$. The lesson is one every seller knows in their bones: more bidders means higher bids and less room to shade. Competition is the seller's best friend.

A surprising tie: revenue equivalence

We now have two very different auctions. In the second-price auction, bidders are honest but the seller collects only the second-highest value. In the first-price auction, bidders shade down but the winner pays their (higher) own bid. Which auction makes the seller more money?

The astonishing answer: on average, exactly the same. The two effects — honest-but-second-price versus shaded-but-first-price — cancel out perfectly.

Key idea — the Revenue Equivalence Theorem

Under standard conditions (private values drawn independently from the same distribution, risk-neutral bidders), all four standard auction formats yield the same expected revenue to the seller. How you run the auction doesn't change what you earn on average — it only changes who pays what in any single sale.

Example 5.1
Checking revenue equivalence with two bidders

Two bidders, values uniform on $[0,1]$.

Second-price auction. The seller collects the second-highest of two values. The expected value of the smaller of two uniform draws is $\frac{1}{3}$. So expected revenue $= \frac{1}{3}$.

First-price auction. Each bidder bids $b(v) = \frac{1}{2}v$. The winner is the higher-valued bidder; the expected higher value is $\frac{2}{3}$, and they pay half of it. So expected revenue $= \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$.

Both give the seller $\frac{1}{3}$ — identical, exactly as the theorem promises. The honest second price and the shaded first price land in precisely the same place.

Revenue equivalence was glimpsed by Vickrey in 1961 and proved in full generality by Roger Myerson and (independently) John Riley and William Samuelson in 1981. It is the theoretical backbone of modern auction design: if the basic formats all raise the same revenue, a seller should choose among them based on other things — simplicity, resistance to cheating, how they handle collusion — rather than chasing a revenue difference that, under these assumptions, isn't there.

The phrase "under standard conditions" carries weight, though. Break the assumptions — bidders who fear risk, values that are correlated, the threat of collusion — and the formats do diverge. That's where real auction design earns its keep, and it's exactly the territory the next idea opens up.

Common values and the winner's curse

So far each bidder's value has been personal and private. But some auctions are different: everyone is bidding for the same underlying prize, whose true worth nobody knows exactly. Think of drilling rights to an oil field — the oil is worth the same to whoever wins; bidders just have different estimates of how much is down there. This is a common-value auction, and it hides a vicious trap.

Ten oil companies bid for a drilling tract. The field holds some fixed amount of oil — say it's truly worth $10 million — but no one knows that figure. Each company's geologists produce an estimate; some guess high, some low, averaging out around the truth. Each firm bids based on its estimate. Who wins? The company whose geologists were the most optimistic of all ten. And that company has very likely overestimated the field. Winning the auction is itself bad news: it means you were the biggest optimist in the room.

This trap is called the winner's curse: in a common-value auction, the mere fact that you won tells you that you probably overvalued the prize. Win the bidding, lose the money.

Definition 5.4 Winner's curse

In a common-value auction — where the item is worth the same (unknown) amount to all bidders, who hold different estimates of it — the winning bidder is typically the one whose estimate was the highest, and therefore most likely an overestimate. The winner's curse is the resulting tendency for naive winners to overpay. The remedy: shade your bid downward to account for the bad news implicit in winning.

Key idea

Don't bid what you estimate the prize is worth. Bid what it's worth given that you win — which is less, because winning means yours was the rosiest estimate. Sophisticated bidders shade down to escape the curse; naive bidders win auctions and lose money.

The winner's curse was first described in 1971 by three petroleum engineers — Capen, Clapp, and Campbell — who noticed that oil companies kept earning dismal returns on offshore tracts they had "won." The companies weren't unlucky; they were systematically overbidding because they ignored what winning revealed. Once you understand the curse, you bid more cautiously — and the more rivals you face, the more cautious you should be, since out-bidding a larger crowd means you were an even more extreme optimist.

Auctions in the wild

Auction theory is not a chalkboard curiosity. When the U.S. government sold off wireless spectrum in 1994, it hired game theorists to design the rules; the resulting simultaneous ascending auction raised tens of billions of dollars and became the template for spectrum sales worldwide. Two of its architects, Paul Milgrom and Robert Wilson, won the 2020 Nobel Prize in Economics for their work on auction theory and design.

You take part in auctions constantly. Every Google search fires off a near-instant auction among advertisers for the slots beside your results — built on second-price-style logic precisely because it discourages strategic gaming. eBay's proxy bidding is essentially a second-price auction in disguise. Governments raise debt through Treasury auctions. Even electricity markets clear supply and demand through auction mechanisms every few minutes. The bidding instincts you build in this chapter are, quite literally, running the modern economy.

Wrapping up

Auctions reward understanding the rules. In a second-price auction, honesty is weakly dominant — bid your value and never look back. In a first-price auction, shade below your value by a factor that shrinks as competitors multiply. Remarkably, these different behaviors leave the seller with identical expected revenue. And when bidders chase a common prize, the winner's curse turns victory into a warning — bid as if winning already tells you that you aimed too high. Exercise 5.3 Medium

Verify revenue equivalence for $n = 3$ bidders with values uniform on $[0,1]$. You may use these facts: for three uniform draws, the expected highest value is $\frac{3}{4}$ and the expected second-highest is $\frac{1}{2}$.

Solution

Second-price: the seller collects the second-highest value, whose expectation is $\frac{1}{2}$. Expected revenue $= \frac{1}{2}$.

First-price: with $n = 3$ the bid is $b(v) = \frac{2}{3}v$. The winner has the highest value (expected $\frac{3}{4}$) and pays $\frac{2}{3}$ of it. Expected revenue $= \frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{3}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$.

Both equal $\frac{1}{2}$, confirming revenue equivalence for $n=3$. (The general formula is $\frac{n-1}{n+1}$; here $\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$.)

Exercise 5.4 Hard

A common-value tract is worth the same unknown amount $V$ to all 10 bidders. Each bidder $i$ privately observes an unbiased estimate $s_i = V + \varepsilon_i$, where the errors $\varepsilon_i$ are independent with mean zero. A naive bidder bids their raw estimate $s_i$. Explain, using the idea of conditional expectation, why the winning naive bidder tends to lose money, and what a rational bidder should do instead.

Solution

Each estimate is unbiased: $\mathbb{E}[s_i] = V$. So before knowing who wins, bidding $s_i$ looks fair on average. But the auction selects the winner non-randomly: the winner is the bidder with the highest estimate. The highest of ten estimates is, on average, well above $V$ — it sits in the upper tail of the error distribution.

Formally, the relevant quantity is not $\mathbb{E}[V]$ but $\mathbb{E}[V \mid s_i \text{ is the highest of all } 10]$, which is strictly less than $s_i$. A naive bidder who pays $s_i$ therefore pays more than the item is worth conditional on having won — they suffer the winner's curse and lose money on average.

A rational bidder corrects for this by shading their bid below their estimate, by an amount that grows with the number of competitors and the noisiness of the estimates. The bid should reflect the value of the prize given that this estimate turns out to be the winning one, not the value given the estimate alone. In short: condition on winning before you bid.

Bargaining & the Division of the Spoils

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Auctions decide who gets a fixed prize. Bargaining decides how to split one. Whenever two parties can do better together than apart — a buyer and seller, an employer and worker, two countries drawing a border — there is a surplus to divide, and the question becomes: who gets how much? Game theory gives two strikingly different answers, one about fairness and one about patience.

You and a colleague are offered a $100 bonus to split between you — but only if you can agree on how. Disagree, and you each get a small consolation: you'd pocket $20 from another project, she'd pocket $10 from hers. So there's $100 on the table, but walking away isn't worth nothing to either of you. How should you carve it up? Straight down the middle feels fair — but you have a better outside option than she does. Shouldn't that count for something? And if you can drag the negotiation out while she's in a hurry, shouldn't that count too?

Those two instincts — that your fallback matters and that your patience matters — turn out to be the two great pillars of bargaining theory. We'll build each into a precise prediction.

The bargaining problem

Let's set the stage formally. Two players can together produce some set of possible outcomes, each giving the pair a pair of payoffs. If they fail to agree, they each fall back on a guaranteed disagreement point — what they get by walking away. The whole problem is choosing a point from the feasible set, and the disagreement point anchors everything.

Definition 6.1 Bargaining problem

A bargaining problem is a pair $(\mathcal{F}, d)$, where $\mathcal{F} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ is the feasible set of achievable payoff pairs $(u_1, u_2)$ the two players can reach by agreement, and $d = (d_1, d_2) \in \mathcal{F}$ is the disagreement point — the payoffs each receives if no deal is struck. A solution selects one outcome $(u_1^*, u_2^*) \in \mathcal{F}$ from every such problem.

The disagreement point (also called the threat point or, in modern usage, the BATNA — "best alternative to a negotiated agreement") is the heart of bargaining power. The more you'd get by walking away, the more you can demand at the table, because your threat to leave is more credible. Hold that thought — it shapes both solutions to come.

We also assume players only ever agree to Pareto-efficient outcomes — points on the upper-right frontier of the feasible set, where you can't make one player better off without making the other worse off (the idea we met in Chapter 2). Leaving surplus unclaimed would be irrational for both, so a sensible deal always lands on that frontier.

The Nash bargaining solution: fairness from axioms

In 1950 — the same year he gave us the equilibrium concept — a 21-year-old John Nash asked a beautiful question: if a "fair and reasonable" bargaining outcome exists, what properties must it have? Rather than model the haggling, he wrote down a short list of common-sense requirements and asked which split satisfies all of them at once. The answer was unique and surprising.

Nash's requirements (his axioms, meaning self-evident starting assumptions) were, in plain terms:

  • Efficiency — don't leave surplus on the table; pick a point on the frontier.
  • Symmetry — if the two players face identical situations, they get identical shares.
  • Scale invariance — the outcome shouldn't change if you merely rescale how a player measures their own payoff (dollars vs. cents shouldn't matter).
  • Independence of irrelevant alternatives — removing an option neither party would have chosen doesn't change the deal.

From just these four, Nash proved there is exactly one solution — and it has a remarkably clean form: pick the deal that maximizes the product of the two players' gains over their disagreement payoffs.

Theorem 6.2 The Nash bargaining solution (Nash, 1950)

There is a unique bargaining solution satisfying efficiency, symmetry, scale invariance, and independence of irrelevant alternatives. It selects the feasible point $(u_1, u_2)$ that maximizes the Nash product

$$(u_1 - d_1)(u_2 - d_2)$$

over all feasible $(u_1, u_2)$ with $u_1 \geq d_1$ and $u_2 \geq d_2$.

The full proof is involved; here is the essential idea. First, scale invariance lets us rescale each player's payoff so that the disagreement point sits at the origin $(0,0)$ and the symmetric solution point sits on the line $u_1 = u_2$. Under this normalization, symmetry and efficiency force the solution to be the point on the frontier where $u_1 = u_2$.

Now observe that the point maximizing the product $u_1 u_2$ on a frontier is exactly where the frontier is tangent to a rectangular hyperbola $u_1 u_2 = \text{const}$ — and for a symmetric frontier that tangency lies on the $45^\circ$ line, matching the symmetric solution. Independence of irrelevant alternatives then extends this: any feasible set can be enclosed in a symmetric one sharing the same maximizer of the product, and shrinking back to the original set leaves the product-maximizer unchanged because it was never the "irrelevant" discarded part. Undoing the rescaling restores the general formula: maximize $(u_1 - d_1)(u_2 - d_2)$. Uniqueness follows because the product is strictly concave on the feasible region, so it has a single maximizer. ∎

What does this look like? For a simple "divide a pie of size 1" problem, where the frontier is the line $u_1 + u_2 = 1$, the Nash solution has a wonderfully intuitive form: each player keeps their disagreement payoff, then splits the leftover surplus equally.

Figure 6.1 · The Nash bargaining solution
The shaded triangle is the feasible set; the green line is the Pareto frontier $u_1 + u_2 = 1$. From the disagreement point $d = (0.2, 0.1)$ (rose), the Nash solution $N = (0.55, 0.45)$ (gold) gives each player their fallback plus an equal half of the remaining surplus of $0.7$ — so $0.2 + 0.35 = 0.55$ and $0.1 + 0.35 = 0.45$. It is the point on the frontier maximizing the product of the two gains.
Key idea

The Nash bargaining solution says: each player first secures their fallback, then they split the surplus evenly. A better outside option (a higher disagreement payoff) directly raises your share — your fallback is your bargaining power. The formula $u_i = d_i + \tfrac{1}{2}(\text{surplus})$ captures it exactly.

Example 6.1
Splitting the $100 bonus

Return to the story. The bonus is $100, your fallback is $20, your colleague's is $10. In the units of the diagram (pie of size 100), the disagreement point is $d = (20, 10)$.

The surplus — the gain from agreeing rather than walking away — is $100 - 20 - 10 = 70$. The Nash solution gives each of you your fallback plus half of that surplus:

You: $20 + \tfrac{70}{2} = 55$.   Your colleague: $10 + \tfrac{70}{2} = 45$.

You get $55, she gets $45 — not the 50/50 that "fairness" first suggested. The $10 edge in your outside option translates into a $10 edge in the split. The lesson every negotiator learns eventually: improve your alternative, and you improve your deal, even before you say a word at the table.

Rubinstein bargaining: patience is power

Nash's solution is elegant, but it sidesteps the actual process of bargaining — the back-and-forth of offer and counter-offer. In 1982, Ariel Rubinstein filled that gap with a model so clean it became the standard way economists think about negotiation. And it connects directly to the backward induction we learned in Chapter 4.

The setup: two players bargain over a pie of size 1 by alternating offers. Player 1 proposes a split; Player 2 accepts (and the deal is done) or rejects. If rejected, time passes and now Player 2 proposes; Player 1 accepts or rejects; and so on, potentially forever. The crucial friction is that delay is costly: each round, the pie is discounted by a factor $\delta \in (0,1)$ — the same impatience parameter from Chapter 3. A pie worth 1 today is worth only $\delta$ a round from now.

Who has the advantage? Intuitively, whoever is more patient (higher $\delta$) can afford to wait out the other, and whoever moves first has a small edge. Rubinstein made this exact.

Theorem 6.3 Rubinstein alternating offers (1982)

In the infinite-horizon alternating-offers game with common discount factor $\delta \in (0,1)$, there is a unique subgame-perfect equilibrium. Agreement is reached immediately: the first proposer offers a split in which they keep

$$\frac{1}{1+\delta} \quad\text{and the responder receives}\quad \frac{\delta}{1+\delta},$$

and the responder accepts at once. No costly delay ever actually occurs in equilibrium.

The model looks the same every time it's a player's turn to propose, so look for a stationary equilibrium where the proposer always keeps the same share $v$. Consider Player 1's turn. If Player 2 rejects Player 1's offer, then next round Player 2 becomes the proposer and — by stationarity — secures share $v$ of the pie, which is worth $\delta v$ to Player 2 today after discounting.

So to get Player 2 to accept now, Player 1 must offer Player 2 at least $\delta v$. Player 1 offers exactly that and keeps the rest: $1 - \delta v$. But by stationarity, the share Player 1 keeps as proposer is also $v$. Setting these equal:

$$v = 1 - \delta v \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; v(1 + \delta) = 1 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; v = \frac{1}{1+\delta}.$$

The responder gets $1 - v = \frac{\delta}{1+\delta}$. Since the responder is offered exactly their continuation value, they are indifferent and accept immediately, so agreement happens in round one with no delay. Rubinstein's deeper contribution was proving this stationary equilibrium is the unique subgame-perfect equilibrium — even allowing non-stationary strategies, no other outcome survives. ∎

Look at what the formula says as patience changes. The proposer's share is $\frac{1}{1+\delta}$:

Figure 6.2 · Patience determines the split
The first proposer's equilibrium share, $\frac{1}{1+\delta}$, as a function of the discount factor $\delta$. When players are impatient ($\delta$ near 0), delay is very costly, so the proposer's first-mover power is huge — they take almost the whole pie. When players are patient ($\delta$ near 1), waiting costs almost nothing, the first-mover edge shrinks, and the split approaches a near-equal $\tfrac{1}{2}$ each.
Key idea

Patience is bargaining power. The more costly it is for you to wait (the lower your $\delta$), the worse your deal — because your opponent knows delay hurts you more. A negotiator who can credibly say "I'm in no rush" holds the stronger hand. And notice: the threat of endless haggling is what disciplines the split, yet in equilibrium they agree instantly — exactly the kind of credible-threat logic from Chapter 4.

There's a deep connection between the two models. Rubinstein's bargaining outcome, as the time between offers shrinks toward zero, converges to the Nash bargaining solution. The fairness story (Nash) and the process story (Rubinstein) describe the same destination from two different roads — one axiomatic, one strategic. That convergence, established by Binmore, Rubinstein, and Wolinsky in 1986, is one of the most satisfying unifications in game theory.

Bargaining in the world

These models are the backbone of how economists analyze negotiation everywhere. In labor disputes, a union's strike fund and a firm's ability to weather a stoppage are exactly their disagreement payoffs and discount factors — which is why both sides invest in being able to hold out longer. In divorce and legal settlements, the "fallback" is what each party expects from going to trial, and patience (or the cash to keep paying lawyers) shifts the settlement. In international trade talks, a country's ability to walk away — its other trading partners — is its BATNA, and the side that can afford delay extracts better terms.

The single most actionable insight from this whole chapter is the one negotiators pay consultants for: before you ever sit down, work on your alternative. A stronger outside option and a credible willingness to wait do more for your final share than any clever tactic at the table.

Wrapping up

Bargaining is the art of dividing a surplus, and game theory gives two complementary answers. The Nash bargaining solution derives a fair split from axioms: secure your fallback, then share the surplus equally — so a better outside option means a bigger slice. The Rubinstein model derives the split from the strategic process of alternating offers, and reveals that patience is power: the cost of delay decides who gets the larger share. Remarkably, the two converge on the same answer. In Chapter 7 we turn to a new complication that pervades real negotiations and markets alike — what happens when one side knows something the other doesn't, and must find a way to signal it credibly.

Exercise 6.1 Easy

Two roommates split a $200 utility refund. If they can't agree, each separately gets a $40 partial credit. Using the Nash bargaining solution (pie = $200), how much does each receive?

Solution

The disagreement point is $d = (40, 40)$ — symmetric. The surplus over disagreement is $200 - 40 - 40 = 120$, split evenly gives $60$ each.

Each roommate receives $40 + 60 = 100$, so $100 each. Because their fallbacks are equal, symmetry gives an even split — exactly what you'd expect.

Exercise 6.2 Medium

A supplier and a retailer bargain over a $1,000 joint profit. The supplier's outside option is worth $300; the retailer's is worth $100. (a) Find the Nash bargaining split. (b) Now the retailer finds a second supplier, raising its outside option to $400. Recompute. Who benefited, and by how much?

Solution

(a) Disagreement $d = (300, 100)$. Surplus $= 1000 - 300 - 100 = 600$, half is $300$. Supplier: $300 + 300 = 600$; retailer: $100 + 300 = 400$.

(b) New disagreement $d = (300, 400)$. Surplus $= 1000 - 300 - 400 = 300$, half is $150$. Supplier: $300 + 150 = 450$; retailer: $400 + 150 = 550$.

The retailer's share jumped from $400 to $550 — a $150 gain — while the supplier fell from $600 to $450. Improving its outside option by $300 shifted $150 of the split toward the retailer. Note the gain is half the improvement in the outside option: raising your fallback by $x$ raises your share by $x/2$ (the other half comes out of the shrinking shared surplus). This is exactly why firms cultivate alternative partners before negotiating.

Exercise 6.3 Medium

In Rubinstein bargaining over a pie of size 1, compute the first proposer's share for $\delta = 0.5$, $\delta = 0.9$, and $\delta = 0.99$. What happens to the first-mover advantage as players become more patient?

Solution

Using the proposer's share $\frac{1}{1+\delta}$:

$\delta = 0.5$: $\frac{1}{1.5} \approx 0.667$ (responder gets $0.333$).
$\delta = 0.9$: $\frac{1}{1.9} \approx 0.526$ (responder gets $0.474$).
$\delta = 0.99$: $\frac{1}{1.99} \approx 0.503$ (responder gets $0.497$).

As $\delta \to 1$, the proposer's share falls toward $\tfrac{1}{2}$: the first-mover advantage vanishes as players become perfectly patient. The edge from moving first comes entirely from the responder's cost of waiting one round — remove that cost and the edge disappears.

Exercise 6.4 Hard

Suppose the two players in Rubinstein bargaining have different discount factors: Player 1 (the first proposer) has $\delta_1$ and Player 2 has $\delta_2$. Using the same stationarity argument, show that Player 1's equilibrium share is $\frac{1 - \delta_2}{1 - \delta_1\delta_2}$. Then verify it reduces to the symmetric formula when $\delta_1 = \delta_2$.

Solution

Let $x$ be Player 1's share when they propose, and $y$ be Player 2's share when Player 2 proposes. Stationarity gives two equations, one per proposer.

When Player 1 proposes, Player 2 must be offered their continuation value. If Player 2 rejects, Player 2 proposes next round and gets share $y$, worth $\delta_2 y$ today. So Player 1 offers Player 2 exactly $\delta_2 y$ and keeps $x = 1 - \delta_2 y$.

By the symmetric logic, when Player 2 proposes they keep $y = 1 - \delta_1 x$ (offering Player 1 their continuation value $\delta_1 x$).

Substitute the second into the first: $x = 1 - \delta_2(1 - \delta_1 x) = 1 - \delta_2 + \delta_1\delta_2 x$. Collect terms: $x(1 - \delta_1\delta_2) = 1 - \delta_2$, so

$$x = \frac{1 - \delta_2}{1 - \delta_1\delta_2}.$$

When $\delta_1 = \delta_2 = \delta$: $x = \frac{1-\delta}{1-\delta^2} = \frac{1-\delta}{(1-\delta)(1+\delta)} = \frac{1}{1+\delta}$, recovering the symmetric result. Notice the asymmetric formula shows that a lower $\delta_2$ (a more impatient opponent) raises Player 1's share toward 1 — being matched against an impatient rival is a windfall.

Signaling & the Value of a Costly Message

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Until now, everyone in our games knew the same things. But the most interesting strategic problems arise when one side knows something the other doesn't — your true ability, the real quality of your product, your genuine intentions. This chapter opens the second half of the book, on games of hidden information, with its most elegant idea: how someone with private information can prove it credibly, through an action that would be too costly to fake.

A start-up is hiring, and two candidates apply. One is genuinely brilliant; the other is mediocre. Both, naturally, claim to be brilliant — talk is cheap, so the claim tells the employer nothing. Yet one of them holds a degree from a punishing four-year program, and the other doesn't. The employer can't peer inside their heads to measure talent. But the degree is something it can see. The question that launched a whole field: can a credential reveal hidden ability even if the schooling taught no skill whatsoever — purely because the brilliant candidate found it easier to endure?

That was the radical thought a young economist named Michael Spence had in 1973. His answer — yes, a costly signal can credibly reveal what words cannot — earned him a share of the 2001 Nobel Prize and reshaped how we understand education, advertising, branding, and much else. Let's build it.

The problem: when talk is cheap

The setup is a market with asymmetric information — a situation where one party knows something relevant that the other does not. Here, a worker knows their own type (their productivity), but the employer cannot observe it directly.

Suppose workers come in two types: High productivity (worth 20 to an employer) and Low productivity (worth 10). In a competitive labor market, employers pay each worker their expected productivity. If employers can't tell the types apart, they're forced to pay everyone the same average wage — which overpays the Low types and, crucially, underpays the High types. The talented worker desperately wants to prove they're worth 20, but simply saying so is cheap talk: a costless message anyone can send, so it carries no information. The Low type would happily say "I'm brilliant" too.

Key idea

Words are free, so anyone can say anything — which is exactly why claims alone can't be believed. To credibly reveal private information, you need an action that is differentially costly: cheap enough for the genuine type to undertake, but too expensive for a pretender to imitate.

The signal: education as a credential

Enter education. Suppose acquiring a level of education $e$ (say, years of a demanding degree) is costly — and here is Spence's pivotal assumption — more costly for the Low type than the High type. The talented worker finds the coursework manageable; the less able worker finds the same coursework grueling, expensive, and time-consuming. Concretely, let the cost of education be $10e$ for a Low type but only $5e$ for a High type.

The provocative twist: assume education adds nothing to actual productivity. It teaches no useful skill. Its only role is as a signal — an observable, costly action that an informed party takes to credibly convey hidden information. Can it still do its job? Remarkably, yes.

Definition 7.1 Signal and the single-crossing property

A signal is an observable, costly action chosen by an informed player to convey private information to an uninformed player. A signal can separate types when it satisfies the single-crossing property: the marginal cost of the signal is lower for the type that is more attractive to the uninformed party. Formally, if the High type's cost of signal $e$ is $c_H(e)$ and the Low type's is $c_L(e)$, single-crossing requires $c_L(e) > c_H(e)$ for the relevant range — the higher type can always acquire any given signal level more cheaply.

The phrase single-crossing refers to the fact that, drawn as curves, the two types' cost (or indifference) lines cross just once — which is exactly the geometric condition that lets a single signal level cleanly divide them. It is the mathematical heart of every signaling model.

The separating equilibrium

Now we look for a separating equilibrium — an outcome where the two types choose different signal levels, so the employer can tell them apart perfectly. The candidate equilibrium: High types acquire some education level $e^*$, Low types acquire none, and the employer's rule is "pay 20 to anyone with education $\geq e^*$, pay 10 to anyone with less."

For this to actually hold together, two conditions — the incentive constraints — must be satisfied. Neither type must want to deviate from its assigned behavior.

Theorem 7.2 Existence of a separating equilibrium (Spence, 1973)

With High productivity 20, Low productivity 10, and education costs $c_H(e) = 5e$ and $c_L(e) = 10e$, there is a separating equilibrium in which High types choose any education level $e^* \in [1, 2]$, Low types choose $e = 0$, and employers pay $20$ for education $\geq e^*$ and $10$ otherwise. The signal credibly reveals type even though education adds no productivity.

We need both types to prefer their assigned choice. Write each type's net payoff as wage minus education cost.

Low type won't mimic (IC-Low). Staying at $e = 0$ earns the Low type wage $10$ at zero cost, for net $10$. Mimicking by acquiring $e^*$ would earn wage $20$ but cost $10 e^*$, for net $20 - 10e^*$. The Low type stays put provided

$$10 \geq 20 - 10e^* \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; e^* \geq 1.$$

High type will signal (IC-High). Acquiring $e^*$ earns the High type wage $20$ at cost $5e^*$, for net $20 - 5e^*$. Deviating to $e = 0$ earns wage $10$ for net $10$. The High type signals provided

$$20 - 5e^* \geq 10 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; e^* \leq 2.$$

Both constraints hold simultaneously exactly when $e^* \in [1, 2]$. The gap between the constraints exists because of single-crossing: the Low type's steeper cost $(10e)$ makes mimicking unattractive at a signal level the High type $(5e)$ still finds worthwhile. Without the cost difference, no separating $e^*$ would exist. ∎

The picture below shows exactly why a signal level in this window does the trick.

The specific numbers aren't special. The same logic pins down the separating window for any productivities and costs — and reveals exactly when separation is possible at all.

Proposition 7.3 The separating window and the necessity of single-crossing

Let the High type have productivity $y_H$ and marginal education cost $\gamma_H$, and the Low type productivity $y_L < y_H$ and marginal cost $\gamma_L$. A separating equilibrium (High chooses $e^*$, Low chooses $0$) exists if and only if

$$\frac{y_H - y_L}{\gamma_L} \;\leq\; e^* \;\leq\; \frac{y_H - y_L}{\gamma_H},$$

and this interval is nonempty if and only if $\gamma_H < \gamma_L$ — the single-crossing condition.

The Low type declines to mimic when staying (net $y_L$) beats mimicking (net $y_H - \gamma_L e^*$): that gives $y_L \geq y_H - \gamma_L e^*$, i.e. $e^* \geq \frac{y_H - y_L}{\gamma_L}$. The High type chooses to signal when signaling (net $y_H - \gamma_H e^*$) beats deviating to $e=0$ (net $y_L$): that gives $e^* \leq \frac{y_H - y_L}{\gamma_H}$. Combining yields the stated interval.

The interval is nonempty precisely when its lower bound does not exceed its upper bound:

$$\frac{y_H - y_L}{\gamma_L} \leq \frac{y_H - y_L}{\gamma_H} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \frac{1}{\gamma_L} \leq \frac{1}{\gamma_H} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \gamma_H \leq \gamma_L,$$

using $y_H - y_L > 0$ to divide without flipping the inequality. Strict single-crossing $\gamma_H < \gamma_L$ gives a window with positive width, leaving room for a strict separating equilibrium; equality collapses the window to a single indifference point. ∎

The payoffs at e* = 1.5

Take the least-wasteful-but-strict separating signal, $e^* = 1.5$ years.

Low type: earns wage $10$ at $e = 0$, for net $10$. If they tried to mimic, they'd pay $10 \times 1.5 = 15$ for a wage of $20$ — net just $5$. Mimicking is far worse, so they stay home.

The types separate cleanly. The employer sees the credential and knows, with certainty, who is who — purely from the choices each type rationally made.

Pooling, and the waste of signaling

The separating equilibrium isn't the only possibility. There can also be a pooling equilibrium, in which both types choose the same signal level (often zero education), so the employer learns nothing and pays everyone the average wage. Which equilibrium prevails depends on beliefs and on the fraction of High types in the population — a subtlety we'll sharpen in Chapter 9 when we make beliefs explicit.

But the separating equilibrium carries a sobering lesson. In it, High types spend real resources — $7.5$ in our example — on education that, by assumption, made them no more productive. That expenditure is pure social waste: it produces nothing except a sorting of workers. From society's point of view, everyone might be better off if the signal could be skipped and types revealed some cheaper way.

Key idea

Signaling is privately rational but can be socially wasteful. The talented worker gains from proving their type, but the cost of the proof — education that taught nothing — is burned with no productive return. Much of the debate over the value of higher education turns on how much of it is genuine skill-building versus pure Spencian signaling.

Signaling is everywhere

Once you see the logic, you spot it constantly. A company that spends lavishly on a Super Bowl ad may be signaling confidence in its product — only a firm expecting repeat customers would find such spending worthwhile, so the very wastefulness is the message. A firm that pays a dividend signals that its earnings are real and sustainable. A peacock's extravagant tail — biology's most famous signal — works precisely because only a genuinely fit bird can afford to haul it around, an idea Amotz Zahavi called the "handicap principle." A retailer's costly warranty signals belief in product quality. In every case the signal is credible for the same reason Spence identified: it would be too expensive for a pretender to fake.

Wrapping up

Signaling solves a problem that cheap talk cannot: it lets an informed party credibly reveal hidden information through an action too costly to fake. The key is the single-crossing property — the signal must be cheaper for the more desirable type — which opens a window of signal levels that produce a separating equilibrium. The catch is that the signal can be socially wasteful, valuable to the signaler but unproductive for the world. We assumed the employer's beliefs ("high education means high type") rather than deriving them. Both constraints hold (and $1.2 \in [1,2]$), so yes, $e^* = 1.2$ is a valid separating equilibrium.

Exercise 7.2 Medium

Keep High productivity 20 and Low 10, but now suppose education is equally costly for both types: $c_H(e) = c_L(e) = 8e$. Show that no separating equilibrium exists, and explain which assumption broke.

Solution

For separation at some $e^*$, we'd need the Low type to not mimic, $10 \geq 20 - 8e^*$, i.e. $e^* \geq 1.25$; and the High type to signal, $20 - 8e^* \geq 10$, i.e. $e^* \leq 1.25$.

The only value satisfying both is $e^* = 1.25$ exactly — and there both types are indifferent. At any common cost, the two incentive constraints meet at a single point with no slack, so there is no robust separating equilibrium: whatever signal the High type can profitably afford, the Low type can equally afford to copy.

The broken assumption is single-crossing. With identical cost functions the types' cost lines coincide rather than cross, so no signal level can distinguish them. The cost difference is essential — it is the entire engine of signaling.

Exercise 7.3 Medium

Suppose a fraction $h = 0.4$ of workers are High type. Compute the pooling wage if both types choose $e = 0$. Then determine whether a High type prefers this pooling outcome to the separating equilibrium with $e^* = 1.5$ from Example 7.1.

Solution

Pooling wage: the employer pays expected productivity, $0.4 \times 20 + 0.6 \times 10 = 8 + 6 = 14$. In pooling, both types acquire $e=0$, so the High type nets $14$.

Separating: from Example 7.1, the High type nets $12.5$.

Since $14 > 12.5$, the High type actually prefers pooling here — the average wage of 14 beats the 12.5 left after paying for the signal. When High types are common enough, the cost of separating exceeds its benefit, and the wasteful signaling becomes a bad deal even for the talented. (With fewer High types, the pooling wage drops and separation becomes attractive again.)

Exercise 7.4 Hard

Generalize: High productivity $y_H$, Low productivity $y_L$ (with $y_H > y_L$), costs $c_H(e) = e/a_H$ and $c_L(e) = e/a_L$ with $a_H > a_L > 0$ (so High has lower marginal cost). Derive the range of separating signals $e^*$ in terms of these parameters, and state the single-crossing condition needed for the range to be nonempty.

Solution

IC-Low (won't mimic): $y_L \geq y_H - e^*/a_L$, which rearranges to $e^* \geq a_L(y_H - y_L)$.

IC-High (will signal): $y_H - e^*/a_H \geq y_L$, which rearranges to $e^* \leq a_H(y_H - y_L)$.

So the separating range is

$$a_L(y_H - y_L) \;\leq\; e^* \;\leq\; a_H(y_H - y_L).$$

This interval is nonempty precisely when $a_H > a_L$ — the single-crossing condition that the High type's marginal cost $(1/a_H)$ is lower than the Low type's $(1/a_L)$. The width of the window, $(a_H - a_L)(y_H - y_L)$, grows with both the productivity gap and the cost-advantage gap: the more different the types, the easier they are to separate. With our numbers $(a_H = \tfrac{1}{5}, a_L = \tfrac{1}{10}, y_H - y_L = 10)$ this gives $[1, 2]$, matching Theorem 7.2.

Bayesian Games & Playing Against the Unknown

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

In Chapter 7 the employer simply assumed that high education meant high ability. But where do such beliefs come from, and how do you play a game rationally when you don't know who you're up against — whether your rival is tough or weak, whether their costs are high or low, whether they value the prize more than you? This chapter gives the framework for exactly that: games of incomplete information, and the elegant trick that tames them.

You're about to enter a market dominated by one incumbent firm. If that firm is the aggressive sort, it will start a brutal price war the moment you arrive, and you'll regret entering. If it's the accommodating sort, it will quietly make room and you'll both profit. The trouble is, you can't see inside the boardroom — you don't know which kind of rival you face. You only know that, say, aggressive firms are fairly rare in this industry. Do you enter? Suddenly the game isn't just about what your opponent will do, but about who your opponent secretly is.

Until now, every game assumed players knew each other's payoffs. Real strategic life rarely offers that luxury. The breakthrough that brought such uncertainty into game theory came from John Harsanyi in 1967, and it was clever enough to earn him a share of the 1994 Nobel Prize alongside Nash and Selten.

Incomplete information and the idea of a "type"

We need to distinguish two kinds of not-knowing. Imperfect information (Chapter 4) means you can't see what move an opponent made — like simultaneous play. Incomplete information is deeper: you don't know the opponent's payoffs in the first place — their costs, their preferences, their very nature. A Bayesian game is the tool for incomplete information.

Harsanyi's key move was to package everything a player might privately know into a single label: their type.

Definition 8.1 Type and Bayesian game

A player's type $\theta_i$ is a complete description of their private information — everything they know that others don't, such as their payoffs, costs, or preferences. A Bayesian game consists of: players $i = 1, \ldots, n$; a set of possible types $\Theta_i$ for each player; a prior probability distribution over type profiles (capturing everyone's beliefs about everyone's types); action sets; and payoff functions $u_i(a_1, \ldots, a_n; \theta_i)$ that may depend on a player's own type.

The word type is doing a lot of work: it bundles all of a player's hidden characteristics into one variable, so "not knowing my opponent" becomes "not knowing my opponent's type." And the word prior means the probability beliefs players hold before any private information is revealed — the shared starting point for everyone's reasoning.

Harsanyi's trick: let Nature move first

Here is the move that made incomplete information tractable. Harsanyi imagined adding a fictional player called Nature who, at the very start of the game, randomly draws each player's type according to the prior — and privately tells each player their own type. After Nature's draw, every player knows their own type but only has probabilistic beliefs about the others'.

This is the Harsanyi transformation, and it is genuinely magical: it converts a baffling game of incomplete information (you don't know the payoffs) into an ordinary game of imperfect information (you don't know one move — Nature's draw). The latter we already know how to analyze.

Key idea

The Harsanyi transformation turns "I don't know my opponent's payoffs" into "a fictional player called Nature secretly chose my opponent's type at the start." Uncertainty about who you face becomes uncertainty about one hidden move — and that we can handle with the tools we already have.

Picture the market-entry story this way. Nature first flips a weighted coin to decide whether the incumbent is Tough (with probability $p$) or Soft (with probability $1-p$). The incumbent learns its own nature; the entrant does not.

Figure 8.1 · The Harsanyi transformation of the entry game
Nature (Nat) moves first, drawing the incumbent's type — Tough with probability $p$, Soft with probability $1-p$. The Entrant (E) must choose without knowing the draw: its two decision nodes lie in a single information set (the gold dashed link), meaning it cannot tell the branches apart. Each Incumbent (I) type then responds. Payoffs are $(\text{Entrant}, \text{Incumbent})$. In equilibrium the Tough type fights and the Soft type accommodates (green branches) — so the entrant must weigh the odds.

Bayesian Nash equilibrium

With types in hand, we extend the Nash equilibrium idea. The twist: a strategy must now specify what each player does for each of their possible types, and "best response" means best on average, weighing the unknown types of others by their probabilities.

Definition 8.2 Bayesian Nash equilibrium

A strategy in a Bayesian game specifies an action for each of a player's types, $s_i(\theta_i)$. A profile of such strategies is a Bayesian Nash equilibrium (BNE) if every type of every player is choosing an action that maximizes that type's expected payoff, where the expectation is taken over the other players' types (using the prior beliefs) and given the others' strategies. In short: each type best-responds, in expectation, to everyone else.

The phrase to hold onto: in a Bayesian Nash equilibrium, every type best-responds in expectation. Each version of you — the you that Nature might have made — plays optimally given what you'd believe in that situation.

There is a deeper reason this definition is the "right" one. Harsanyi proved that solving the transformed game — treating each type as if it were a separate player choosing an action — recovers exactly the equilibria of the original incomplete-information game.

Theorem 8.3 Harsanyi equivalence (1967)

Every Bayesian game of incomplete information has an equivalent game of complete (but imperfect) information — its Bayes-equivalent game, in which Nature first draws the type profile according to the prior. A strategy profile is a Bayesian Nash equilibrium of the original game if and only if it is a Nash equilibrium of the Bayes-equivalent game. Consequently, every finite Bayesian game has at least one Bayesian Nash equilibrium (allowing mixed strategies).

Construct the Bayes-equivalent game as follows. Replace each player $i$ by a collection of "agents," one for each possible type $\theta_i \in \Theta_i$. Each type-agent must choose an action, and is paid according to the original payoff function evaluated at the realized type profile, weighted by the prior probability that this type profile occurs. Nature's initial draw of types according to the prior is the single move that no player fully observes — making this a standard game of imperfect information.

Now compare equilibrium conditions. In the original Bayesian game, the requirement is that each type $\theta_i$ choose an action maximizing its expected payoff, the expectation taken over the other players' types under the prior. In the Bayes-equivalent game, the requirement is that each type-agent choose a Nash best response given the others. These two conditions are literally the same expectation, written from two points of view: the type-agent's payoff in the constructed game is precisely type $\theta_i$'s prior-weighted expected payoff in the original. Hence a profile satisfies the BNE conditions exactly when it is a Nash equilibrium of the Bayes-equivalent game.

Existence then follows from Nash's theorem: the Bayes-equivalent game is a finite normal-form game (finitely many type-agents, finite actions), so it has a Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies, which is a Bayesian Nash equilibrium of the original. ∎

Solving the entry game under uncertainty

Let's solve Figure 8.1. Suppose the payoffs are: staying out gives the entrant $0$; entering and facing a Fight gives the entrant $-1$; entering and being Accommodated gives the entrant $2$. For the incumbent, a Tough type earns more by fighting, and a Soft type earns more by accommodating.

Start with the incumbent. Because each type has a clear best response if entry occurs, the equilibrium has the Tough type fight and the Soft type accommodate — each type does what's best for itself. Now the entrant, who can't tell the types apart, must reason about the odds.

Proposition 8.4 The entrant's threshold

In the Bayesian entry game, where a Tough incumbent (probability $p$) fights and a Soft incumbent (probability $1-p$) accommodates, entering is optimal for the entrant if and only if

$$2 - 3p \geq 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad p \leq \tfrac{2}{3}.$$

The entrant enters when a tough rival is sufficiently unlikely, and stays out otherwise.

Given the incumbent's type-contingent strategy (Tough fights, Soft accommodates), the entrant who enters faces a Fight with probability $p$ and an Accommodate with probability $1-p$. The entrant's expected payoff from entering is therefore

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{Enter}] = p\cdot(-1) + (1-p)\cdot(2) = 2 - 3p.$$

Staying out yields $0$ with certainty. The entrant's best response is to enter exactly when the expected payoff from entering is at least the payoff from staying out:

$$2 - 3p \geq 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad p \leq \tfrac{2}{3}.$$

This entrant strategy, paired with the two incumbent types' best responses, forms a Bayesian Nash equilibrium: each incumbent type best-responds to entry, and the entrant best-responds in expectation to the type-contingent behavior it anticipates. ∎

Example 8.1
Should you enter?

Suppose tough incumbents make up a quarter of the industry, so $p = \tfrac{1}{4}$. The entrant's expected payoff from entering is

$2 - 3(\tfrac{1}{4}) = 2 - 0.75 = 1.25 > 0$,

so the entrant enters. Now suppose the industry is mostly aggressive, $p = \tfrac{3}{4}$. Then the expected payoff is $2 - 3(\tfrac{3}{4}) = 2 - 2.25 = -0.25 < 0$, and the entrant stays out.

Notice the entrant's decision flips not because any firm's behavior changed, but because the belief about the rival's type changed. In Bayesian games, beliefs are as much a part of the strategic landscape as actions.

Auctions revisited: a Bayesian game in disguise

You have already solved a Bayesian game — back in Chapter 5. When bidders in a first-price auction each privately know their own valuation, that valuation is their type. The prior is the distribution from which values are drawn (uniform on $[0,1]$ in our example), and the equilibrium bidding rule $b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}v$ is precisely a Bayesian Nash equilibrium: each "type" $v$ chooses the bid that maximizes its expected surplus, averaging over rivals' unknown values.

Key idea

Bayesian games are not an exotic special case — they are the natural setting for most of economics. A bidder's value, a firm's cost, a worker's ability, a country's resolve: all are private types. The first-price auction, the signaling model, and the entry game are all Bayesian games, unified by Harsanyi's framework.

This unification is why Harsanyi's contribution was so foundational. Asymmetric information — the lemons problem, signaling, screening, auctions, mechanism design — all rests on the type-and-prior machinery laid down here. Bayesian games are the grammar of modern information economics.

Wrapping up

Bayesian games let us play rationally against the unknown. By packaging private information into a type, positing a shared prior, and letting Nature draw types at the outset (the Harsanyi transformation), we turn incomplete information into a game we can solve. The solution concept, Bayesian Nash equilibrium, asks every type to best-respond in expectation. We saw that beliefs about an opponent's type can flip a decision even when no behavior changes, and that auctions and signaling were Bayesian games all along. One question remains, though, and it's the one Chapter 7 left open: when players move in sequence and update their beliefs as they observe each other, how should those beliefs evolve? That is the subject of Chapter 9, perfect Bayesian equilibrium.

Exercise 8.1 Easy

In the Bayesian entry game (enter payoff $-1$ against Fight, $2$ against Accommodate; stay-out payoff $0$), the Tough type fights and the Soft type accommodates. If $p = 0.6$ of incumbents are Tough, should the entrant enter? Show the expected-payoff calculation.

Expected payoff from entering: $0.6(-1) + 0.4(2) = -0.6 + 0.8 = 0.2$.

Exercise 8.2 Medium

Find the exact probability $p$ of a Tough incumbent at which the entrant is indifferent between entering and staying out. What does the entrant do at probabilities just above and just below this value?

Solution

Indifference occurs when the expected payoff from entering equals the stay-out payoff of $0$: $2 - 3p = 0$, giving $p = \tfrac{2}{3}$.

For $p < \tfrac{2}{3}$ the expected payoff is positive, so the entrant enters; for $p > \tfrac{2}{3}$ it is negative, so the entrant stays out. At exactly $p = \tfrac{2}{3}$ the entrant is indifferent and either choice is a best response. This threshold is the precise point at which a more-aggressive industry tips the entrant from entering to staying away.

Exercise 8.3 Medium

Two firms compete, and firm 2 privately knows whether its marginal cost is Low (probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$) or High (probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$). Suppose, given firm 1's output, firm 2's profit-maximizing output is $40$ when Low-cost and $20$ when High-cost. If firm 1 must choose its output before learning firm 2's cost, what output should firm 1 plan against in expectation? Explain why firm 1 cannot simply "wait and see."

Solution

Firm 1 cannot observe firm 2's cost type, so it must respond to firm 2's expected output. The expected output of firm 2 is

$\tfrac{1}{2}(40) + \tfrac{1}{2}(20) = 20 + 10 = 30$ units.

Firm 1 best-responds to an anticipated rival output of 30 on average. It cannot "wait and see" because the firms choose simultaneously under incomplete information — firm 2's cost is its private type, drawn by Nature, and never revealed to firm 1 before it must commit. Each cost type of firm 2 best-responds to firm 1, and firm 1 best-responds to the probability-weighted average — a Bayesian Nash equilibrium. (This is exactly the Cournot-with-private-costs setup we'll solve with calculus in the applications chapter.)

Exercise 8.4 Hard

Explain the difference between imperfect information and incomplete information, and describe precisely how the Harsanyi transformation converts one into the other. Why is this conversion so useful?

Solution

Imperfect information means a player cannot observe some action or move that has been taken — for instance, simultaneous play, where you don't see your opponent's choice before making your own. The payoffs themselves are common knowledge; only a move is hidden.

Incomplete information is deeper: a player does not know some structural feature of the game itself — typically an opponent's payoffs, costs, or preferences. You don't merely fail to see a move; you don't know what game your opponent is playing.

The Harsanyi transformation introduces a fictitious player, Nature, who moves first and draws each player's type (their private payoff information) according to the common prior, revealing each type only to its owner. After this device, the players do know the full structure of the enlarged game — including Nature's probabilities — and merely fail to observe one move: Nature's draw. Incomplete information has become imperfect information.

This is useful because we already possess a complete toolkit for games of imperfect information (information sets, expected payoffs, Nash equilibrium). By recasting incomplete information in those terms, Harsanyi made an entire class of previously intractable problems solvable with existing methods — and showed every incomplete-information game has a well-defined "Bayes-equivalent" complete-information game whose Nash equilibria are the Bayesian Nash equilibria of the original.

Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium & Believing What You See

32 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Chapter 7 let an employer assume "high education means high ability," and Chapter 8 gave us types and priors. But a sharp question was left dangling: when players move in sequence and watch each other, where exactly do their beliefs come from, and which beliefs are reasonable? This chapter delivers the answer — perfect Bayesian equilibrium — the tool that finally makes beliefs an explicit, disciplined part of the analysis.

A poker player slides a huge stack of chips into the pot. You can't see her cards — but you can see the bet, and from it you try to read her hand. Is she holding aces, or bluffing with junk? Your decision to call or fold rests entirely on the belief you form from that single observed action. And she knows you're reading her, so she chooses her bet partly to shape what you'll believe. Strategy, here, is a contest of beliefs — and the question is which beliefs hold up under scrutiny.

That is the world of perfect Bayesian equilibrium (PBE). It pairs every strategy with an explicit belief at each decision point, insists those beliefs be updated rationally from what's observed, and requires every action to be optimal given those beliefs. Let's see why we need it.

Why subgame perfection isn't enough

Recall subgame-perfect equilibrium from Chapter 4: it required optimal play in every subgame. The trouble is that a subgame can only start at a node the player knows they're at. When there's hidden information — an information set containing several nodes a player can't tell apart (the dashed-linked nodes from Chapter 8) — there is often no proper subgame to discipline behavior there. Subgame perfection simply has nothing to say.

To make decisions at such information sets, a player needs a belief: a probability assessment of which node within the set they're actually at. Give them that, and "play optimally" regains its meaning — optimal given the belief. PBE is built from exactly two ingredients.

Definition 9.1 Beliefs and assessment

A belief system $\mu$ assigns to each information set a probability distribution over the nodes it contains — the player's assessment of "where am I, given that I'm here?" An assessment is a pair $(\sigma, \mu)$: a strategy profile $\sigma$ together with a belief system $\mu$. Perfect Bayesian equilibrium is a condition on assessments, not strategies alone.

The two ingredients are sequential rationality (every action is optimal given beliefs and others' strategies) and belief consistency (beliefs are computed from strategies by Bayes' rule wherever possible).

Definition 9.2 Perfect Bayesian equilibrium

An assessment $(\sigma, \mu)$ is a perfect Bayesian equilibrium if it is:

(1) Sequentially rational: at every information set, the player's continuation strategy maximizes their expected payoff given the belief $\mu$ there and the others' strategies; and

(2) Belief-consistent: wherever an information set is reached with positive probability under $\sigma$, the belief $\mu$ is derived from $\sigma$ by Bayes' rule. (At information sets reached with zero probability — "off the equilibrium path" — beliefs are unrestricted by Bayes' rule.)

Key idea

A perfect Bayesian equilibrium is a strategy plus a belief at every information set, where (1) every move is optimal given the beliefs, and (2) the beliefs are updated by Bayes' rule from what's actually observed. Beliefs stop being an assumption and become a derived, disciplined object.

Bayes' rule: turning observations into beliefs

The engine of belief consistency is Bayes' rule — the formula for updating a probability after observing evidence. If the prior probability of the High type is $P(H)$, and each type sends a given message with known probability, then upon seeing that message the updated (posterior) belief is

$$\mu(H \mid \text{msg}) = \frac{P(\text{msg}\mid H)\,P(H)}{P(\text{msg}\mid H)\,P(H) + P(\text{msg}\mid L)\,P(L)}.$$

In words: the chance you're facing a High type, given the message, is the share of the message's total probability that comes from the High type. This is exactly how the poker player reads a bet, and how an employer reads a diploma.

Proposition 9.3 On-path beliefs are pinned down by Bayes' rule

In any PBE, beliefs at information sets reached with positive probability are uniquely determined by the players' strategies via Bayes' rule. In a separating equilibrium (each type sends a distinct message), these beliefs are degenerate: observing a message reveals the sender's type with certainty. In a pooling equilibrium (all types send the same message), the on-path belief equals the prior — the message conveys no new information.

Apply Bayes' rule to each case. In a separating equilibrium, suppose the High type sends message $m_1$ with probability 1 and the Low type sends a different message $m_2$. Then $P(m_1 \mid H) = 1$ and $P(m_1 \mid L) = 0$, so

$$\mu(H \mid m_1) = \frac{1 \cdot P(H)}{1\cdot P(H) + 0 \cdot P(L)} = 1.$$

The belief is degenerate: $m_1$ reveals a High type for certain (and symmetrically $m_2$ reveals Low). The receiver learns the type exactly.

In a pooling equilibrium, both types send the same message $m_1$, so $P(m_1\mid H) = P(m_1 \mid L) = 1$ and

$$\mu(H \mid m_1) = \frac{1\cdot P(H)}{1\cdot P(H) + 1\cdot P(L)} = P(H).$$

The posterior equals the prior — the message is uninformative, exactly as intuition demands when everyone sends it. Any other message is now off-path (sent with probability zero), so Bayes' rule does not apply there and the belief is free to be chosen. ∎

Example 9.1
Reading a partial bluff

Suppose High and Low types are equally likely $(P(H) = P(L) = \tfrac{1}{2})$. The High type always sends message $m_1$, but the Low type sends $m_1$ only $30\%$ of the time (a partial bluff). On seeing $m_1$, what should the receiver believe?

By Bayes' rule:

$$\mu(H \mid m_1) = \frac{1 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}}{1\cdot\tfrac{1}{2} + 0.3\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}} = \frac{0.5}{0.65} = \frac{10}{13} \approx 0.77.$$

The receiver should believe there's about a $77\%$ chance the sender is High — up from the $50\%$ prior, because High types send $m_1$ more reliably. The message shifted the belief but didn't settle it, exactly as a poker bet narrows your read without confirming it.

Signaling games, properly solved

Now we can finish what Chapter 7 began. The job-market signaling story is a signaling game: Nature picks the sender's type, the informed sender chooses a message (education), and the uninformed receiver (employer) observes the message, forms a belief, and chooses an action (a wage). PBE is the right solution concept because the receiver acts at an information set — they see the message but not the type.

The freedom of off-path beliefs

Here is where PBE reveals its subtlety — and its main limitation. In a pooling equilibrium, where both types take the same action, any other action is never observed in equilibrium. It is off the equilibrium path, reached with probability zero, so Bayes' rule is silent and the receiver's belief there can be anything. This freedom is what lets pooling equilibria survive.

Proposition 9.4 Off-path beliefs can sustain a pooling equilibrium

Suppose both types pool on the same message $m_1$. This is supported as a PBE if the receiver holds off-path beliefs at the unused message $m_2$ pessimistic enough that the resulting response makes deviation unprofitable for both types. Because Bayes' rule does not constrain off-path beliefs, such supporting beliefs can typically be found — which is precisely why PBE often admits many equilibria and motivates further refinements.

In a pooling PBE on $m_1$, on-path the receiver believes $\mu(H\mid m_1) = P(H)$ (the prior, by Proposition 9.3) and best-responds to that. Consider the unused message $m_2$. Since no type sends it in equilibrium, $m_2$ is reached with probability zero, and Bayes' rule places no restriction on $\mu(H \mid m_2)$.

Choose the off-path belief to be the most damning one — say $\mu(H \mid m_2) = 0$, i.e. "anyone who deviates to $m_2$ must be a Low type." The receiver then responds to $m_2$ with the action worst for the sender (the low wage). If even the type most tempted to deviate finds that the punishment response makes $m_2$ yield less than staying with $m_1$, then neither type deviates, and the pooling assessment is sequentially rational. Since the off-path belief was free to choose, this pessimistic belief is admissible, and the pooling equilibrium stands.

The catch: nothing forces the off-path belief to be sensible. Refinements such as the Cho–Kreps intuitive criterion (1987) ask whether a deviation could only have come from a type that would benefit — and prune beliefs that ignore this. Such refinements often eliminate implausible pooling equilibria, sharpening PBE's predictions. ∎

Key idea

On the equilibrium path, Bayes' rule pins beliefs down. Off the path, PBE leaves them free — and a sufficiently pessimistic off-path belief ("only a low type would do that") can deter deviations and prop up a pooling equilibrium. This freedom is PBE's flexibility and its weakness, which is why refinements like the intuitive criterion exist.

The refinement ladder

PBE sits on a ladder of ever-stricter solution concepts, each taming the one below by disciplining beliefs further. Nash equilibrium ignores sequence entirely; subgame perfection (Chapter 4) adds credibility within subgames; PBE adds explicit beliefs and Bayes' rule; sequential equilibrium (Kreps and Wilson, 1982) further requires off-path beliefs to be limits of beliefs from "trembles" — tiny mistakes — ruling out the most arbitrary ones; and the intuitive criterion prunes still more. Each rung trades a little simplicity for sharper, more credible predictions.

For most applied work — signaling, reputation, bargaining with incomplete information — PBE is the workhorse. It is exactly strong enough to make beliefs explicit and rational without the heavier machinery of sequential equilibrium, which is why it appears throughout modern economics.

Wrapping up

Perfect Bayesian equilibrium completes the logic of hidden-information games. By attaching an explicit belief to every information set, demanding sequential rationality given those beliefs, and tying on-path beliefs to Bayes' rule, it makes "what should I believe, and how should I act on it?" a precise question with a disciplined answer. Exercise 9.4 Hard

Explain why subgame-perfect equilibrium is inadequate for games with non-trivial information sets, and state precisely how perfect Bayesian equilibrium repairs the gap. In your answer, define what a "subgame" requires and why an information set spanning several nodes usually fails to start one.

Solution

A subgame must begin at a single node that is its own information set (a node the mover knows they are at), and it must include all of that node's successors without slicing through any information set. When a player has a non-trivial information set — several nodes they cannot distinguish — no single node in it qualifies as the start of a subgame, because the player doesn't know which node they're at. Consequently the only "subgame" is often the whole game, and subgame perfection reduces to Nash equilibrium there, imposing no extra discipline at the hidden-information point.

The gap: without a subgame, subgame perfection cannot ask "is the action at this information set optimal?" — there is no well-defined continuation value, because optimality depends on which node the player is at, which they don't know.

The repair: PBE attaches a belief — a probability distribution over the nodes in the information set — so the player can compute an expected continuation payoff and choose optimally (sequential rationality). It then ties those beliefs to the strategies via Bayes' rule wherever possible (belief consistency). Together these restore meaningful optimization at exactly the points subgame perfection couldn't reach, generalizing subgame perfection to games of incomplete and imperfect information. Every PBE is a Nash equilibrium, and in games where proper subgames do exist, every PBE induces subgame-perfect play — so PBE is a genuine refinement.

Mixed Strategies & the Power of Being Unpredictable

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Every equilibrium so far has been a definite choice: cooperate or defect, enter or stay out, study or slack. But some games have no stable pure choice at all — whatever you settle on, your opponent can exploit. The escape is to stop being predictable: to randomize. This chapter shows why deliberate randomness is sometimes the only rational play, how to compute the right odds, and why every finite game has an equilibrium once we allow it.

A tennis player stands ready to receive a serve. If she commits to guarding the left, a smart server aims right; if she leans right, the serve goes left. There is no single spot she should always cover — any fixed habit gets punished. Her best approach is to keep the server guessing: defend left some fraction of the time, right the rest, in proportions exactly calibrated so the server can't profit from aiming either way. Top players do this without naming it. Game theorists call it a mixed strategy.

Penalty kicks, tennis serves, poker bluffs, tax audits, military feints — all share this structure. Predictability is fatal, so rational play requires randomization. Let's make the idea precise.

When pure strategies fail: matching pennies

The cleanest example is matching pennies. Two players each secretly turn a coin to Heads or Tails. Player 1 (the "matcher") wins if the coins match; Player 2 (the "mismatcher") wins if they differ. The winner takes a point from the loser.

Try to find a stable pair of pure choices. If both play Heads, the matcher is happy but the mismatcher wants to switch to Tails. Once the mismatcher switches, the matcher wants to switch too. Chase this around and you find no pure-strategy resting point exists — every combination has someone wanting to deviate. The game has no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium.

What does exist is a mixed strategy: a deliberate randomization over your pure choices.

Definition 10.1 Mixed strategy

A mixed strategy for player $i$ is a probability distribution $\sigma_i$ over their pure strategies — a rule like "play Heads with probability $p$ and Tails with probability $1-p$." A pure strategy is the special case that puts all probability on one action. The support of a mixed strategy is the set of pure strategies it plays with positive probability. Payoffs are computed as expected values over the randomization.

The indifference principle

How do you find the right probabilities? The answer is a beautiful and slightly counterintuitive rule. When you choose your mixing odds, you don't pick them to make yourself better off across your options — you pick them to make your opponent indifferent. And in equilibrium, your opponent's odds make you indifferent. This is the indifference principle, the master key to every mixed equilibrium.

Key idea

In a mixed-strategy equilibrium, each player randomizes in exactly the proportions that leave the other player indifferent among the strategies they're mixing over. If you're genuinely mixing between two actions, both must yield you the same expected payoff — otherwise you'd abandon the worse one. So your opponent's mix is pinned down by your indifference, and vice versa.

Theorem 10.2 The indifference principle

If a player uses a mixed strategy in a Nash equilibrium, then every pure strategy in its support yields that player the same expected payoff (given the others' strategies), and no pure strategy outside the support yields more. Consequently, a player who randomizes over several actions must be exactly indifferent among them.

Let player $i$'s mixed strategy $\sigma_i$ be a best response to the others' strategies, and suppose its support contains pure strategies that do not all give the same expected payoff. Let $a$ be a pure strategy in the support with the highest expected payoff $U_{\max}$, and $b$ one in the support with a strictly lower expected payoff $U_b < U_{\max}$.

The expected payoff of the whole mixture $\sigma_i$ is the probability-weighted average of the payoffs of the pure strategies in its support. Because $b$ is played with positive probability and earns strictly less than $a$, this average is strictly below $U_{\max}$. But then player $i$ could strictly increase their expected payoff by shifting all the probability weight from $b$ onto $a$ — contradicting the assumption that $\sigma_i$ was a best response.

Hence every pure strategy in the support must earn the same expected payoff. A pure strategy outside the support cannot earn strictly more, or shifting weight onto it would again improve the mixture. ∎

Solving matching pennies

Now apply the principle. Let Player 1 play Heads with probability $p$ and Player 2 play Heads with probability $q$. We find each player's mix by making the other indifferent.

Player 2 (the mismatcher) is indifferent between Heads and Tails only if Player 1's mix makes both equally good. Working out Player 2's expected payoffs and setting them equal gives $p = \tfrac{1}{2}$. By the symmetric calculation, Player 1 is indifferent only if $q = \tfrac{1}{2}$. So the unique equilibrium is for both players to flip a fair coin — play Heads and Tails each with probability one-half.

We can see this equilibrium as the unique crossing point of the two players' best-response correspondences — the rules giving each player's optimal mix as a function of the other's.

Figure 10.1 · The mixed equilibrium as an intersection
Each axis is a mixing probability: $p$ for Player 1's Heads, $q$ for Player 2's Heads. The violet path is Player 1's best response to each $q$ (match: go all-Heads when $q > \tfrac{1}{2}$, all-Tails when $q < \tfrac{1}{2}$); the amber path is Player 2's best response (mismatch: the opposite). The step-shaped correspondences cross at exactly one point, $(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$ — the unique mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium, where each player is indifferent and neither can be exploited.
Example 10.1
Rock-Paper-Scissors

Rock-Paper-Scissors has the same flavor: any predictable pattern loses. By symmetry, the equilibrium is to play each of the three actions with probability $\tfrac{1}{3}$.

Check the indifference principle. Against an opponent playing $(\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3})$, any single move — say Rock — wins against one option, loses to one, and ties one, each a third of the time. Its expected payoff is

$$\tfrac{1}{3}(+1) + \tfrac{1}{3}(-1) + \tfrac{1}{3}(0) = 0,$$

and the same holds for Paper and Scissors. All three moves yield zero, so you are perfectly indifferent — exactly what the indifference principle demands. The uniform mix is the unique Nash equilibrium, which is why no fixed RPS strategy can beat a player who truly randomizes.

Mixing in coordination games: Battle of the Sexes

Mixed strategies aren't only for pure-conflict games. Consider Battle of the Sexes: two friends want to spend the evening together but disagree on where. Player 1 prefers the Opera, Player 2 prefers the Football match; both would rather be together at either venue than alone. There are two pure equilibria (both Opera, or both Football), but also a mixed one — capturing the genuine risk of a coordination failure.

With the natural payoffs (you get 2 at your preferred venue together, 1 at the other venue together, 0 if you end up apart), the indifference principle gives a mixed equilibrium in which Player 1 chooses Opera with probability $\tfrac{2}{3}$ and Player 2 chooses Opera with probability $\tfrac{1}{3}$.

Example 10.2
The cost of miscoordination

In that mixed equilibrium, each player's expected payoff works out to $\tfrac{2}{3}$. Compare that to the pure equilibria, where the players earn $2$ and $1$. The mixed equilibrium is worse for both than either coordinated outcome.

Why would rational players land there? Because when each independently randomizes to hedge against the other's choice, they frequently miscoordinate — both insisting on their own favorite, or both deferring — and end up apart. The mixed equilibrium quantifies the real cost of failing to coordinate, which is why pre-play communication (agreeing where to meet) is so valuable: it selects one of the good pure equilibria over the lossy mixed one.

Nash's great theorem: equilibrium always exists

Matching pennies had no pure equilibrium but did have a mixed one. Is that always true? In a one-page paper in 1950, John Nash proved the answer is yes — and it is the result that won him the Nobel Prize and made modern game theory possible.

Theorem 10.3 Nash's existence theorem (1950)

Every finite game — any game with finitely many players, each having finitely many pure strategies — has at least one Nash equilibrium, possibly in mixed strategies.

The argument is one of the most elegant in economics; here is its skeleton. Consider the set of all mixed-strategy profiles — one probability distribution per player. This set is compact (closed and bounded) and convex (a mix of two valid profiles is a valid profile), and it lives in a finite-dimensional space.

Define the best-response correspondence: a map that takes any strategy profile and returns the set of profiles in which each player best-responds to the others. A Nash equilibrium is precisely a fixed point of this map — a profile that is among its own best responses, so no one wants to move.

Nash showed this correspondence satisfies the hypotheses of Kakutani's fixed-point theorem: the best-response set is non-empty, convex (because expected payoffs are linear in one's own probabilities, so the set of best responses is a face of the simplex), and the correspondence has a closed graph (best responses vary "without jumps"). Kakutani's theorem then guarantees a fixed point exists — and that fixed point is a Nash equilibrium. Because the construction never required pure strategies, the equilibrium it delivers may be mixed. ∎

Nash's two-page argument generalized von Neumann's 1928 minimax theorem (which had guaranteed equilibrium only for two-person zero-sum games) to all finite games, cooperative or competitive, with any number of players. That leap is what turned game theory into a universal tool.

Key idea

Allowing mixed strategies guarantees that every finite game has an equilibrium. A game might have no stable pure outcome (matching pennies), but it can never have no equilibrium at all. Nash equilibrium is therefore a universal solution concept — it always has something to say.

Why randomness is rational

A common objection: "Surely real people don't compute probabilities and flip mental coins." True — but the prediction still holds, for two reasons. First, in repeated competitive settings, any detectable pattern will be exploited, so survival pressure pushes skilled players toward unpredictability whether or not they reason it out. Studies of professional tennis serves and soccer penalty kicks find players randomize remarkably close to the game-theoretic proportions. Second, a mixed equilibrium can be reinterpreted: your opponent doesn't need you to actually randomize, only to be unpredictable from their vantage point — which a population of slightly different players, or your own private hesitations, can supply. Either way, the indifference conditions describe the stable resting point.

Wrapping up

When no pure strategy is safe, rationality demands randomization. A mixed strategy spreads probability across your actions, and the indifference principle pins down the exact odds: you mix so as to make your opponent indifferent, and their mix makes you indifferent. Matching pennies settles at $(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$, Rock-Paper-Scissors at the uniform mix, and even coordination games have mixed equilibria that measure the cost of miscoordination. Above all, Nash's existence theorem assures us that once mixing is allowed, every finite game has an equilibrium — game theory never comes up empty. Exercise 10.1 Easy

In matching pennies, suppose Player 1 (the matcher) plays Heads with probability $p$. Write Player 2's (the mismatcher's) expected payoff from playing Heads and from playing Tails, then find the $p$ that makes Player 2 indifferent. (Payoffs: matcher gets $+1$ on a match, $-1$ on a mismatch; mismatcher the reverse.)

Solution

Player 2 wins (gets $+1$) on a mismatch and loses ($-1$) on a match. If Player 2 plays Heads, they match P1's Heads (prob $p$, payoff $-1$) and mismatch P1's Tails (prob $1-p$, payoff $+1$):

$U_2(\text{Heads}) = p(-1) + (1-p)(+1) = 1 - 2p.$

If Player 2 plays Tails, they mismatch P1's Heads (prob $p$, $+1$) and match P1's Tails (prob $1-p$, $-1$):

$U_2(\text{Tails}) = p(+1) + (1-p)(-1) = 2p - 1.$

Setting them equal: $1 - 2p = 2p - 1 \Rightarrow 4p = 2 \Rightarrow p = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Player 1 must play Heads half the time to keep Player 2 indifferent — the equilibrium mix.

Exercise 10.2 Medium

In Battle of the Sexes (payoff 2 at your favorite venue together, 1 at the other together, 0 apart), let Player 1 play Opera with probability $p$ and Player 2 play Opera with probability $q$. Find the mixed equilibrium by making each player indifferent.

Make Player 1 indifferent (depends on Player 2's $q$). Player 1 prefers Opera. From Opera: meets P2 there with prob $q$ for payoff 2, else 0, giving $2q$. From Football: meets P2 there with prob $1-q$ for payoff 1, giving $1-q$. Indifference: $2q = 1-q \Rightarrow q = \tfrac{1}{3}$.

Make Player 2 indifferent (depends on Player 1's $p$). Player 2 prefers Football. From Opera: meets P1 there with prob $p$ for payoff 1, giving $p$. From Football: meets P1 there with prob $1-p$ for payoff 2, giving $2(1-p)$. Indifference: $p = 2(1-p) \Rightarrow 3p = 2 \Rightarrow p = \tfrac{2}{3}$.

Exercise 10.3 Medium

A defending soccer goalkeeper can dive Left or Right; the kicker can aim Left or Right. The kicker scores with high probability by aiming where the keeper does not dive. Explain why this game has no pure-strategy equilibrium, and what form its equilibrium must take. What does the indifference principle predict about how often each should choose each side if the two sides are symmetric?

Solution

This is matching pennies in disguise: the kicker wants to mismatch the keeper's dive, and the keeper wants to match the kicker's aim. For any pure pair, the loser of that pair wants to switch — if the keeper always dives Left, the kicker always aims Right, but then the keeper wants to dive Right, and so on. No pure pair is stable, so no pure-strategy equilibrium exists.

The equilibrium must be in mixed strategies. With perfectly symmetric sides, the indifference principle predicts each player randomizes 50/50 — diving/aiming Left and Right equally often — so neither can be exploited. (With asymmetric sides — e.g. a kicker stronger to one side — the equilibrium tilts, but both still randomize. Empirical studies of real penalty kicks find proportions strikingly close to these predictions.)

Exercise 10.4 Hard

Prove the indifference principle: if a player plays a mixed strategy with support $S$ in a Nash equilibrium, every pure strategy in $S$ earns the same expected payoff, and no pure strategy outside $S$ earns more. Then explain why this makes computing mixed equilibria a matter of solving indifference equations.

Solution

Equal payoffs within the support. Suppose, for contradiction, that two pure strategies $a, b \in S$ earn different expected payoffs against the others' strategies, say $U(a) > U(b)$. The expected payoff of the mixed strategy is the probability-weighted average of the payoffs of the pure strategies in its support. Since $b$ is played with positive probability and earns strictly less than $a$, moving all of $b$'s probability weight onto $a$ strictly raises the average — a profitable deviation. That contradicts the mixed strategy being a best response (Nash equilibrium). So all pure strategies in $S$ must earn equal expected payoffs, call it $U^*$.

Nothing outside earns more. If some pure strategy $c \notin S$ earned $U(c) > U^*$, then shifting weight onto $c$ would again raise the player's payoff — another profitable deviation. So every pure strategy outside the support earns at most $U^*$.

Why this makes computation tractable. The principle converts "find the optimal mix" into "find the opponents' probabilities that equalize the payoffs of my support strategies." Each player's mix is the solution to the other player's indifference equations. For a $2 \times 2$ game this is one linear equation per player — quick to solve — which is exactly the method used throughout this chapter.

Evolutionary Game Theory & Strategies Without Strategists

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Everything so far has assumed players who reason: who weigh payoffs, form beliefs, and choose. But what if no one is thinking at all? Evolutionary game theory makes a startling discovery — the logic of strategic equilibrium emerges even among organisms that never reason, driven by nothing but survival and reproduction. Strategies need no strategist. The same mathematics that describes a chess match also describes a meadow of competing wildflowers.

In a population of animals contesting food, some are born aggressive — they escalate every fight — and some are born cautious, backing down to avoid injury. Neither type chooses its temperament; each simply inherits it and passes it on in proportion to how well it survives and breeds. If aggression spreads too far, aggressors mostly meet other aggressors and tear each other apart, so caution starts to pay. If caution spreads too far, a rare aggressor bullies everyone and prospers, so aggression creeps back. The population drifts toward a balance no individual designed — an equilibrium reached by dying and being born rather than by thinking.

This idea, introduced by the biologist John Maynard Smith and George Price in 1973, transplanted game theory into biology and then back into economics and the social sciences. It replaces the rational chooser with a population of competing strategies, and rational best response with the blind arithmetic of reproduction.

From rational choice to reproductive success

The reinterpretation is sweeping but simple. A strategy is no longer a deliberate plan but a heritable behavioral type — a phenotype, hardwired into an organism. Payoff is no longer utility but fitness: expected reproductive success. And instead of one player facing another, we imagine a large population in which individuals are repeatedly, randomly paired to interact, each earning fitness according to the encounter. Strategies that earn high fitness leave more offspring, so their share of the population grows.

Key idea

Evolutionary game theory swaps the rational chooser for a population of inherited strategies, and best-response reasoning for reproductive success. A strategy "wins" not by being chosen but by leaving more copies of itself. Remarkably, the equilibria this blind process settles into are closely related to the Nash equilibria of the corresponding game.

The evolutionarily stable strategy

The central concept asks: which strategies, once common in a population, cannot be displaced by a rare alternative? A strategy that resists invasion by any mutant is evolutionarily stable.

Definition 11.1 Evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS)

A strategy $s$ is an evolutionarily stable strategy if a population playing $s$ cannot be invaded by any rare mutant strategy $s'$. Formally, writing $E(a, b)$ for the fitness of playing $a$ against $b$, strategy $s$ is an ESS if for every mutant $s' \neq s$, either

(1) $E(s, s) > E(s', s)$ — the incumbent does strictly better against the population than the mutant does; or

(2) $E(s, s) = E(s', s)$ and $E(s, s') > E(s', s')$ — they tie against the population, but the incumbent does better against the rare mutants.

The phrase to remember: an ESS is uninvadable. If almost everyone plays $s$ and a few mutants appear playing $s'$, the incumbents out-reproduce the mutants, and the mutation dies out. This is a refinement of Nash equilibrium tailored to populations: every ESS corresponds to a Nash equilibrium, but not every Nash equilibrium is evolutionarily stable. Evolutionary stability is the stricter, more demanding test.

Hawk-Dove: the evolution of restraint

The founding example is the Hawk-Dove game, a model of conflict over a resource. Two animals contest a resource worth $V$. A Hawk escalates and fights; a Dove displays but retreats rather than risk injury. When two Hawks meet they fight, and the loser pays an injury cost $C$; when a Hawk meets a Dove the Hawk simply takes the resource; when two Doves meet they share it. The interesting case is when fighting is dangerous — $C > V$.

Is "always Hawk" evolutionarily stable? No. In a population of all Hawks, every contest is a costly fight, so the average Hawk does poorly — and a rare Dove, who never gets injured, does better by stepping aside. Doves invade. But "always Dove" isn't stable either: in a peaceful Dove population, a rare Hawk wins every encounter unopposed and spreads. The only stable outcome is a mixture — a precise balance of aggression and restraint.

Proposition 11.2 The Hawk-Dove ESS

In the Hawk-Dove game with resource value $V$ and injury cost $C > V$, neither pure strategy is an ESS. The unique ESS is the mixed strategy that plays Hawk with probability

$$p^* = \frac{V}{C},$$

equivalently, a population in which a fraction $V/C$ of contestants behave as Hawks. At this mix, Hawk and Dove earn equal fitness, so the proportions stay put.

Use the standard payoffs: a Hawk meeting a Hawk earns $\tfrac{V-C}{2}$ (wins half the contests, pays the injury cost the other half); a Hawk meeting a Dove earns $V$; a Dove meeting a Hawk earns $0$; two Doves share, earning $\tfrac{V}{2}$ each.

Let the fraction of Hawks in the population be $p$. A Hawk's expected fitness is its payoff averaged over the opponents it meets:

$$E(\text{Hawk}) = p\cdot\frac{V-C}{2} + (1-p)\cdot V.$$

A Dove's expected fitness is

$$E(\text{Dove}) = p\cdot 0 + (1-p)\cdot \frac{V}{2}.$$

At an interior ESS the two types must earn equal fitness (otherwise the fitter type's share would grow and the mix would not be stationary). Setting $E(\text{Hawk}) = E(\text{Dove})$ and solving:

$$p\cdot\frac{V-C}{2} + (1-p)V = (1-p)\frac{V}{2}.$$

Expanding and simplifying, the $V$ terms collect to give $pC = V$, hence $p^* = \tfrac{V}{C}$. Because $C > V$, this lies strictly between 0 and 1, a genuine mixed strategy. One checks the ESS inequality holds: if the Hawk share rises above $p^*$, Doves become fitter and push it back down; if it falls below, Hawks become fitter and push it back up — so the mix is uninvadable. ∎

The diagram shows the mechanism: the two fitness lines cross at exactly $p^* = V/C$, and the population is driven toward that crossing from either side.

With $V = 2$ and $C = 4$, Hawk fitness (rose) falls as Hawks become common — too many costly fights — while Dove fitness (teal) also declines but less steeply. They cross at the ESS $p^* = V/C = \tfrac{1}{2}$. To the left of the crossing Hawks are fitter, so their share rises; to the right Doves are fitter, so it falls. Example 11.1
Reading the Hawk-Dove balance

Take $V = 2$ (the resource) and $C = 4$ (the injury cost). The ESS hawk fraction is $p^* = V/C = \tfrac{2}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2}$: half the contests are settled by aggression, half by restraint.

At that mix, a Hawk's fitness is $\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{2-4}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1) + 1 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, and a Dove's is $\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 1 = \tfrac{1}{2}$ — equal, as required.

Now make fighting deadlier, $C = 8$: the ESS hawk fraction drops to $\tfrac{2}{8} = \tfrac{1}{4}$. Costlier conflict breeds more restraint — the model predicts that species with more dangerous weaponry evolve more ritualized, less lethal displays, exactly as biologists observe.

The ESS isn't a wholly new idea — it sits neatly inside one we already know. It sharpens Nash equilibrium rather than replacing it.

Proposition 11.3 Every ESS is a Nash equilibrium

If a strategy $s$ is evolutionarily stable, then $(s, s)$ is a (symmetric) Nash equilibrium of the underlying game. The converse fails: there are symmetric Nash equilibria that are not evolutionarily stable. Thus ESS is a strict refinement of Nash equilibrium.

ESS implies Nash. The first ESS condition requires that for every mutant $s' \neq s$, $E(s, s) \geq E(s', s)$ — with strict inequality, or with equality backed by the tie-breaking second condition. In particular $E(s, s) \geq E(s', s)$ holds for all alternatives $s'$. But that statement is exactly what it means for $s$ to be a best response to itself: no deviation against an $s$-playing opponent does better. A strategy that is a best response to itself constitutes a symmetric Nash equilibrium $(s,s)$. So every ESS is a Nash equilibrium.

The converse fails. The even mix in Rock-Paper-Scissors, $(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$, is a symmetric Nash equilibrium — every strategy earns the same payoff against it, so it is a best response to itself. Yet it is not an ESS: a mutant ties it on the first condition $(E(s,s) = E(s',s))$, and the required strict tie-breaker $E(s,s') > E(s',s')$ also fails, because the cyclic structure leaves the mix unable to out-compete invaders against themselves. Hence the population never settles — the cycling of Figure 11.2.

So the ESS conditions are strictly stronger than Nash's: the second condition adds a stability requirement that pure best-response logic doesn't impose. Evolutionary stability demands not just that no mutant does better against the incumbents, but that the incumbents actively beat back the mutants. ∎

The replicator dynamic: evolution as an equation

The ESS describes where evolution settles. The replicator dynamic, introduced by Taylor and Jonker in 1978, describes how it gets there — the actual flow of the population over time. Its logic is the essence of natural selection in one line: strategies that do better than average grow; strategies that do worse shrink.

Definition 11.4 The replicator equation

Let $x_i$ be the fraction of the population playing strategy $i$, let $f_i(x)$ be that strategy's current fitness, and let $\bar{f}(x) = \sum_j x_j f_j(x)$ be the population's average fitness. The replicator equation governs how the strategy shares change over time:

$$\dot{x}_i = x_i\,\bigl(f_i(x) - \bar{f}(x)\bigr).$$

A strategy's share grows when its fitness exceeds the average and shrinks when it falls below — selection in its purest mathematical form.

Notice what this equation does not contain: no reasoning, no foresight, no choice. Just relative fitness. Yet its resting points — where $\dot{x}_i = 0$ for all $i$ — are Nash equilibria of the underlying game, and its stable resting points are closely tied to ESSs. Rationality, it turns out, is one route to equilibrium; selection is another, and they arrive at nearly the same place.

Key idea

The replicator dynamic encodes natural selection in one equation: above-average strategies grow, below-average strategies shrink. It needs no rationality, yet its rest points are Nash equilibria and its stable rest points are ESSs — selection and reason converge on the same destinations. But "rest point" is not "stable": a population can sit at an equilibrium and still be blown off it by a single advantageous mutant.

When evolution never settles: Rock-Paper-Scissors

Not every game has an ESS, and the replicator dynamic doesn't always converge. The vivid case is a population playing Rock-Paper-Scissors — found in nature among certain lizards whose mating types cycle exactly this way. Rock beats Scissors beats Paper beats Rock: a loop of cyclic dominance with no strategy on top.

The unique Nash equilibrium is the even mix $(\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3})$ from Chapter 10 — but it is not an ESS. Under the replicator dynamic the population doesn't settle there; it cycles forever around it. When Rock is common, Paper invades and rises; as Paper grows, Scissors invades; as Scissors grows, Rock returns — an endless rotation.

Why this matters beyond biology

Evolutionary game theory's reach extends far past the meadow. In economics, firms that don't reason optimally but imitate successful rivals, or are selected by market survival, converge on the same equilibria as if they had calculated — a powerful defense of equilibrium predictions even when perfect rationality is implausible. In cultural evolution, norms, conventions, and fashions spread by imitation in exactly the replicator's "copy what's succeeding" pattern. The emergence of cooperation — why helping behavior survives despite the temptation to defect — is studied through evolutionary tournaments, where strategies like Tit-for-Tat (Chapter 3) prove robust over generations. And the framework explains stable polymorphisms throughout nature: why aggression and restraint, or multiple mating types, coexist in fixed proportions rather than one driving out the rest.

Wrapping up

Evolutionary game theory shows that equilibrium does not require reasoning — only differential reproduction. An evolutionarily stable strategy is one no rare mutant can invade, a population-level refinement of Nash equilibrium; the Hawk-Dove game shows why conflict settles at a mix of aggression and restraint, with the ESS hawk-fraction $V/C$ falling as fighting grows costlier; and the replicator dynamic turns selection into a differential equation whose flow can converge to an ESS — or, as in Rock-Paper-Scissors, cycle forever. Strategies need no strategists. In the final chapter we turn the lens back on ourselves: Chapter 13 asks how real human beings play these games, and where they depart, revealingly, from every ideal we have built.

Exercise 11.1 Easy

In the Hawk-Dove game, the ESS fraction of Hawks is $p^* = V/C$. Compute it for (a) $V = 3, C = 6$; (b) $V = 4, C = 5$; (c) $V = 1, C = 10$. What general trend do you see as $C$ rises relative to $V$?

Solution

(a) $p^* = 3/6 = \tfrac{1}{2}$.   (b) $p^* = 4/5 = 0.8$.   (c) $p^* = 1/10 = 0.1$.

As the injury cost $C$ grows relative to the resource value $V$, the ESS Hawk fraction $V/C$ falls — more dangerous conflict produces a more peaceful population. When the prize is large relative to the danger (case b), aggression dominates; when fighting is very costly (case c), restraint prevails.

Exercise 11.2 Medium

Show explicitly that the pure strategy "always Dove" is not an ESS when $V > 0$. (Hint: consider a rare Hawk mutant in an all-Dove population and compare fitnesses.)

Solution

In an all-Dove population, a resident Dove almost always meets another Dove, earning $E(\text{Dove}, \text{Dove}) = \tfrac{V}{2}$. A rare Hawk mutant also almost always meets a Dove, earning $E(\text{Hawk}, \text{Dove}) = V$ — it takes the whole resource unopposed.

Compare: $E(\text{Hawk}, \text{Dove}) = V > \tfrac{V}{2} = E(\text{Dove}, \text{Dove})$ whenever $V > 0$. The mutant Hawk earns strictly more against the population than the incumbent Dove does, so the ESS condition fails at its first inequality.

The Hawk mutant therefore out-reproduces the Doves and spreads: "always Dove" is invadable, hence not an ESS. A peaceful population is always vulnerable to a rare aggressor — which is exactly why the stable outcome must include some Hawks.

Exercise 11.3 Medium

Using the replicator equation $\dot{x}_i = x_i(f_i - \bar{f})$, explain why every state in which the population is entirely one strategy ($x_i = 1$ for some $i$) is a rest point of the dynamic, and why most such monomorphic rest points are nonetheless not evolutionarily stable.

Solution

Why they are rest points. If $x_i = 1$ and all other shares are $0$, then for the present strategy $\dot{x}_i = x_i(f_i - \bar f)$: but with only strategy $i$ present, the average fitness $\bar f$ equals $f_i$, so $f_i - \bar f = 0$ and $\dot{x}_i = 0$. For every absent strategy $j$, $x_j = 0$ makes $\dot{x}_j = 0\cdot(f_j - \bar f) = 0$ regardless of fitness. So nothing changes — the state is a rest point. (The replicator dynamic can never create a strategy that is wholly absent; it only re-weights strategies already present.)

Why they're usually not stable. Being a rest point only means the dynamic is momentarily still, not that it resists perturbation. If a small number of mutants playing some $j$ appear and $f_j > f_i$ in the resident population, then $\dot{x}_j = x_j(f_j - \bar f) > 0$ and the mutants grow — the rest point is unstable, and the strategy is not an ESS. A monomorphic state is evolutionarily stable only if no mutant can earn above-average fitness against it, exactly the ESS condition. (For instance, all-Hawk and all-Dove are both rest points, yet neither is an ESS when $C > V$.)

Exercise 11.4 Hard

Prove that every ESS is a Nash equilibrium (of the symmetric game), but give an example showing the converse fails — a symmetric Nash equilibrium that is not an ESS.

Solution

Every ESS is a Nash equilibrium. Let $s$ be an ESS. The first ESS condition says that for every alternative $s'$, $E(s,s) \geq E(s',s)$ (with strict inequality, or equality backed by the second condition). The inequality $E(s,s) \geq E(s',s)$ for all $s'$ is precisely the statement that $s$ is a best response to itself — which is the definition of a symmetric Nash equilibrium. So every ESS is a Nash equilibrium.

The converse fails. Consider Rock-Paper-Scissors. The mixed strategy $(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$ is a symmetric Nash equilibrium: against an opponent using it, every strategy earns the same expected payoff (zero), so it is a best response to itself. But it is not an ESS. Against a mutant $s'$ (say "always Rock"), the incumbent ties on the first condition — $E(s,s) = E(s',s) = 0$ — so we must check the second condition, $E(s, s') > E(s', s')$. But one can verify $E(s, s') = E(s', s') = 0$ as well (the even mix earns zero against pure Rock, and pure Rock earns zero against itself). The strict inequality fails, so the second condition is violated.

Thus $(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$ is a Nash equilibrium that is not an ESS — consistent with the perpetual cycling we saw in Figure 11.2. ESS is the strictly stronger concept.

Cooperative Games & Dividing the Gains Fairly

32 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Every game so far has been non-cooperative: players act alone, and any cooperation has to be self-enforcing. But sometimes players can sign binding agreements, form coalitions, and pool their gains — the only question is how to split the proceeds. Cooperative game theory steps back from individual moves and asks a different question: when a group creates value together, what division of the spoils is stable, and what division is fair?

Three musicians can busk together. Alone, none of them draws much of a crowd. Any two of them together pull in a decent tip jar. All three, with harmony and a fuller sound, fill it to the brim. At the end of the night they tip out the jar — but how? Equal thirds feels fair until you notice the guitarist's solos are what stop people, or that any pair could have walked off and done nearly as well without the third. The money on the table is real; the puzzle is who deserves what, and which splits would actually hold together without someone storming off.

This is the world of cooperative — or coalitional — games. We won't track individual strategies; instead we'll ask what each possible coalition can guarantee itself, and then seek principled ways to divide the total. Two ideas dominate the field: the core (which splits are stable) and the Shapley value (which split is fair).

Coalitions and the characteristic function

A cooperative game throws out the strategy tables and records just one thing: the value each group can create on its own.

Definition 12.1 Coalitional game and characteristic function

A coalitional game (with transferable utility) consists of a set of players $N = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ and a characteristic function $v$ that assigns to every coalition $S \subseteq N$ a number $v(S)$ — the total payoff that the members of $S$ can guarantee themselves by cooperating, with $v(\varnothing) = 0$. The full set $N$ is the grand coalition, and $v(N)$ is the total value to be divided. An allocation is a vector $(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ with $\sum_i x_i = v(N)$ specifying each player's share.

"Transferable utility" means payoffs are like money — freely divisible and shiftable between players, so a coalition's worth is a single number it can split any way it likes. The characteristic function is the whole game: tell me what every coalition is worth, and the strategic problem becomes one of division.

The core: which divisions hold together

The first question is stability. A proposed split of the busking money is fragile if some subgroup could do better by walking off on its own. The core collects exactly the splits where no coalition has that temptation.

Definition 12.2 The core

An allocation $(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is in the core if it distributes the full value, $\sum_{i \in N} x_i = v(N)$, and no coalition can do better on its own:

$$\sum_{i \in S} x_i \;\geq\; v(S) \quad \text{for every coalition } S \subseteq N.$$

That is, every group receives at least what it could guarantee by breaking away. No coalition has a profitable objection, so the allocation is stable.

The core captures coalitional stability the way Nash equilibrium captured individual stability: in the core, no group — not just no individual — wants to deviate. Let's see what it looks like.

Figure 12.1 · The core of a three-player game
Every point in the big triangle is an allocation of the total value $v(N) = 1$ among three players; each corner gives everything to one player. For a symmetric game where every pair can secure $0.6$, the stability constraints $x_i + x_j \geq 0.6$ force each share below $0.4$, carving out the small green central triangle — the core. The Shapley value $(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$ sits at its centroid, so here the fair split is also stable.

When the core is empty

A striking feature of the core is that it can be completely empty — there may be no stable way to divide the spoils. The cleanest illustration is a simple majority vote.

Proposition 12.3 The majority game has an empty core

Consider three players dividing a prize of 1, where any majority (any two players) controls the whole prize: $v(S) = 1$ if $|S| \geq 2$ and $v(S) = 0$ otherwise. This game has an empty core — no allocation is stable.

Suppose, for contradiction, that some allocation $(x_1, x_2, x_3)$ with $x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 1$ lies in the core. Every two-player coalition can secure the whole prize, so the core constraints require

$$x_1 + x_2 \geq 1, \qquad x_1 + x_3 \geq 1, \qquad x_2 + x_3 \geq 1.$$

Add these three inequalities. The left side counts each player's share twice, giving $2(x_1 + x_2 + x_3)$, and the right side sums to $3$:

$$2(x_1 + x_2 + x_3) \geq 3 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x_1 + x_2 + x_3 \geq \tfrac{3}{2}.$$

But the total to divide is only $x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 1$, and $1 \geq \tfrac{3}{2}$ is false. The contradiction shows no core allocation exists. ∎

Intuitively: whatever the three agree to, some pair is getting less than the whole prize they could seize by themselves — so that pair always has an incentive to defect and cut out the third. Stable agreement is impossible. This is the mathematical heart of why shifting coalitions plague majority-rule politics.

Key idea

The core is the set of allocations no coalition can improve upon — the stable splits. But the core can be empty: in a pure majority game, every division leaves some pair able to grab more by defecting, so no agreement holds. Stability is not guaranteed. (The Bondareva–Shapley theorem states exactly when a core exists: precisely when the game is "balanced.")

The Shapley value: a formula for fairness

Stability and fairness are different questions. Even when many splits are stable (a fat core), which one is fair? And when the core is empty, we still need some principled division. In 1953 Lloyd Shapley answered both with a single formula, derived — like Nash's bargaining solution — from a short list of fairness axioms.

Shapley's idea: reward each player by their average marginal contribution — how much they add to a coalition by joining it — averaged over every possible order in which the players might assemble.

Definition 12.4 The Shapley value

Imagine the players joining the grand coalition one at a time, in a random order (all $n!$ orders equally likely). Each player's marginal contribution is the extra value they bring when they join — the value of the coalition with them minus its value without them. The Shapley value $\varphi_i$ is player $i$'s marginal contribution averaged over all orderings:

$$\varphi_i(v) = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|!\,(n - |S| - 1)!}{n!}\,\bigl[v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S)\bigr].$$

The weights count the orderings in which player $i$ arrives right after exactly the players in $S$.

Shapley proved this is the unique division satisfying four natural fairness requirements, in plain terms: efficiency (the whole value is distributed), symmetry (players who contribute identically get identical shares), the dummy property (a player who adds nothing to any coalition gets nothing), and additivity (the value of two combined games is the sum of the separate values).

Theorem 12.5 Shapley's theorem (1953)

There is a unique allocation rule satisfying efficiency, symmetry, the dummy property, and additivity: the Shapley value $\varphi$. In particular, the Shapley values always sum to the total worth, $\sum_{i \in N} \varphi_i(v) = v(N)$.

We prove the efficiency property — that the Shapley values exactly distribute the total worth — using the "random order" description, which makes it transparent.

Fix any single ordering of the $n$ players, say they arrive as $\pi_1, \pi_2, \ldots, \pi_n$. As each arrives, credit them with their marginal contribution to the coalition already present. The first player gets $v(\{\pi_1\}) - v(\varnothing)$; the second gets $v(\{\pi_1,\pi_2\}) - v(\{\pi_1\})$; and so on. Summing these marginal contributions down the ordering, every intermediate term cancels with the next — the sum telescopes:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} \bigl[v(\{\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_k\}) - v(\{\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_{k-1}\})\bigr] = v(N) - v(\varnothing) = v(N).$$

So in every ordering, the marginal contributions sum to exactly $v(N)$. The Shapley value of each player is their average marginal contribution across all $n!$ orderings. Averaging a collection of orderings whose totals are each $v(N)$ gives a total that is still $v(N)$:

$$\sum_{i \in N} \varphi_i(v) = \frac{1}{n!}\sum_{\text{orderings}} \underbrace{\bigl(\text{sum of marginals}\bigr)}_{=\,v(N)} = v(N).$$

Hence the Shapley value always divides the total worth completely — it is efficient. (Symmetry and the dummy property follow directly from the formula's structure; additivity holds because the formula is linear in $v$. Shapley's deeper result is that these four properties pin down this formula uniquely.) ∎

Example 12.1
The glove market: scarcity is rewarded

Player 1 owns a single left glove; players 2 and 3 each own a right glove. A matched pair sells for $1$; an unmatched glove is worthless. So $v(\{1,2\}) = v(\{1,3\}) = 1$ (a pair can be formed), but $v(\{2,3\}) = 0$ (two right gloves, no pair), and any single player has $v = 0$.

Compute the Shapley value by averaging marginal contributions over all $3! = 6$ arrival orders. Player 1 (the scarce left glove) adds value in most orders; the interchangeable right-glove owners add value only when they arrive before the other right glove and after the left. Working through the six orderings gives

$$\varphi_1 = \tfrac{2}{3}, \qquad \varphi_2 = \tfrac{1}{6}, \qquad \varphi_3 = \tfrac{1}{6}.$$

The shares sum to $1$ (efficiency), and the scarce left glove captures two-thirds of the value while the two abundant right gloves split the rest. Scarcity is rewarded — a result that drops out of pure fairness axioms, with no bargaining assumed. (Interestingly, this game's core is the single point $(1,0,0)$: stability would hand the left-glove owner everything, so here the fair split and the stable split genuinely differ.)

Key idea

The Shapley value pays each player their average marginal contribution across all orders of joining. It is the unique division satisfying efficiency, symmetry, the dummy property, and additivity — a formula for fairness. The core asks what is stable; the Shapley value asks what is fair, and the two answers need not coincide.

Cooperative thinking in the world

The Shapley value is not just elegant — it is used. In cost-sharing, when several airlines share a runway whose cost depends on the largest plane, the Shapley value splits the bill so each airline pays its fair marginal share; the same logic divides the cost of a shared data center or a joint research facility. In politics, the closely related Shapley–Shubik index measures a voter's or party's power by how often they are the pivotal member who swings a coalition from losing to winning — revealing that voting power is not proportional to seats. In machine learning, "SHAP values" — a direct application of Shapley's formula — explain a model's prediction by fairly attributing it among the input features, treating each feature as a player in a cooperative game. And the core underlies the analysis of which trade agreements, partnerships, and matching markets are stable enough to last.

Wrapping up

Cooperative game theory changes the question from "what will each player do?" to "how should a group divide what it creates?" The characteristic function records each coalition's worth; the core collects the stable divisions where no coalition wants to defect — though it can be empty, as the majority game shows; and the Shapley value delivers the unique fair division based on average marginal contributions, rewarding scarcity and pivotality. Stability and fairness are distinct goals that sometimes agree and sometimes clash. In Chapter 13 we close the book by turning from how players should behave to how they actually do — the findings of behavioral game theory, where real humans depart, systematically and revealingly, from the rational ideal.

Exercise 12.1 Easy

A two-player venture has $v(\{1\}) = 20$, $v(\{2\}) = 30$, and $v(\{1,2\}) = 100$. Compute the Shapley value of each player. (With two players, just average the two arrival orders.)

Solution

There are two orders. Order (1 then 2): player 1 contributes $v(\{1\}) = 20$; player 2 contributes $v(\{1,2\}) - v(\{1\}) = 100 - 20 = 80$. Order (2 then 1): player 2 contributes $v(\{2\}) = 30$; player 1 contributes $100 - 30 = 70$.

Average each player's contributions:

$\varphi_1 = \tfrac{20 + 70}{2} = 45, \qquad \varphi_2 = \tfrac{80 + 30}{2} = 55.$

The shares sum to $100 = v(\{1,2\})$ (efficiency ✓). Equivalently, each player gets their stand-alone value plus half the surplus from cooperating — exactly the Nash bargaining split from Chapter 6, which the two-player Shapley value reproduces.

Exercise 12.2 Medium

For the majority game on three players ($v(S) = 1$ if $|S| \geq 2$, else $0$), compute the Shapley value. Then explain how a player can have a positive Shapley value even though the game's core is empty.

Solution

By symmetry, all three players have the same Shapley value, and the values must sum to $v(N) = 1$ (efficiency). Therefore each gets

$\varphi_1 = \varphi_2 = \varphi_3 = \tfrac{1}{3}.$

(Check via orderings: a player is pivotal — turns a losing coalition into a winning one — exactly when they arrive second, which happens in $\tfrac{1}{3}$ of orders. So each has marginal contribution 1 a third of the time, giving $\tfrac{1}{3}$.)

The Shapley value always exists and distributes the whole prize, because it is computed by a formula — it never asks whether the split is stable. The core, by contrast, demands stability (no coalition can defect profitably), and here no split meets that demand. So the Shapley value happily reports $(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$ even though that split is not in the core — any two players could grab the whole prize and beat it. Fairness and stability are simply different criteria.

Exercise 12.3 Medium

A game has $v(\{1\}) = v(\{2\}) = v(\{3\}) = 0$, every pair worth $v(\{i,j\}) = 60$, and $v(N) = 90$. Is the equal split $(30, 30, 30)$ in the core? Find the full set of core allocations.

Solution

The core requires $x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 90$, each $x_i \geq 0$, and each pair $x_i + x_j \geq 60$. Since $x_i + x_j = 90 - x_k$, the pair condition becomes $90 - x_k \geq 60$, i.e. $x_k \leq 30$ for every player.

Equal split: $(30, 30, 30)$ has each $x_i = 30 \leq 30$ ✓ and each pair $= 60 \geq 60$ ✓. So yes, it is in the core (on its boundary).

Full core: all allocations with $x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 90$ and $0 \leq x_i \leq 30$ for each player. Combined with the sum, this forces each $x_i \geq 90 - 30 - 30 = 30$, so in fact $x_i = 30$ for all $i$ — the core is the single point $(30,30,30)$. The strong pair values squeeze the core down to the equal split. (By symmetry this also equals the Shapley value.)

Exercise 12.4 Hard

Prove that if an allocation lies in the core, it must be efficient (distribute exactly $v(N)$) and individually rational (give each player at least $v(\{i\})$). Then show that any core allocation gives the grand coalition itself no profitable objection, so the grand coalition is stable.

Solution

Efficiency. By definition a core allocation satisfies $\sum_{i \in N} x_i = v(N)$ — the grand coalition's entire worth is distributed, with nothing wasted and nothing conjured. (If we defined the core with $\sum x_i \leq v(N)$ plus the coalition constraints, the constraint for $S = N$, namely $\sum x_i \geq v(N)$, would force equality anyway.)

Individual rationality. Apply the core's coalition constraint to each singleton coalition $S = \{i\}$: it requires $x_i \geq v(\{i\})$. So every player receives at least what they could guarantee entirely alone — no one is made worse off by participating than by going solo.

Grand-coalition stability. The defining inequalities say that for every coalition $S$, the members of $S$ already receive at least $v(S)$ in the proposed allocation. So no coalition — of any size — can secure more by breaking away than it is already getting; there is no profitable objection. Since the allocation also exactly distributes $v(N)$, the grand coalition forms and holds: everyone is in, and no subgroup wants out. That is precisely what it means for the grand coalition to be stable, and it is the entire purpose of the core.

Behavioral Game Theory & How People Actually Play

30 min read
4 exercises + quiz
Has arena

Twelve chapters have built an elegant theory of perfectly rational players. There is just one problem: people aren't perfectly rational. They care about fairness, they reason only a few steps deep, they make mistakes, and they punish unfairness at cost to themselves. Behavioral game theory takes the gloves off the laboratory and asks what humans actually do — and the gap between theory and behavior turns out to be not noise, but a deeper signal about human nature.

A stranger is handed $10 and told to offer you some share of it. If you accept, you split it as proposed; if you refuse, you both get nothing. He offers you $1, keeping $9. Pure logic says take it — a dollar beats zero. Yet almost everyone in your position refuses, walking away with nothing rather than letting the stranger get away with such an insult. You have just paid a dollar to punish unfairness. Standard game theory says you blundered. Behavioral game theory says you did something deeply human — and that understanding why is essential to understanding real strategic life.

That scenario is the ultimatum game, and the way people play it launched a revolution. Behavioral game theory, surveyed definitively by Colin Camerer in 2003, keeps the formal apparatus of the previous chapters but tests it against real human choices — and rebuilds the theory where the data demand it.

The ultimatum game: fairness over money

The setup is brutally simple. A proposer is given a sum, say $10, and offers some split to a responder. The responder either accepts (the split stands) or rejects (both get nothing). It is a one-shot, anonymous, sequential game — exactly the kind we solved by backward induction in Chapter 4.

What does our theory predict? Work backward. A rational, self-interested responder prefers any positive amount to zero, so they should accept even $1. Foreseeing this, a rational proposer offers the smallest possible positive amount and keeps the rest. The subgame-perfect prediction is stark: offer a penny, keep $9.99.

Proposition 13.1 The self-interested ultimatum prediction

If both players are rational and care only about their own monetary payoff, the unique subgame-perfect equilibrium of the ultimatum game has the proposer offer the smallest positive amount and the responder accept any positive offer.

Solve by backward induction, as in Chapter 4. Consider the responder's decision last. Faced with an offer of $x > 0$, accepting yields $x$ and rejecting yields $0$. Since a self-interested responder values only their own money and $x > 0$, accepting is strictly better; the responder accepts every positive offer. (At exactly $x = 0$ they are indifferent.)

Now the proposer, anticipating this, chooses the offer $x$ that maximizes their own retained share $10 - x$ subject to the responder accepting. Since any $x > 0$ is accepted, the proposer drives $x$ as low as possible — to the smallest positive unit (a penny) — and keeps essentially the whole pie.

This is the unique subgame-perfect equilibrium under pure self-interest. It is logically airtight — and almost nobody plays it. The failure is not in the mathematics but in the assumption that people value only their own money. ∎

Now the data. Across thousands of experiments and dozens of cultures, the actual behavior is dramatically different: proposers typically offer 40-50% of the pie, and responders routinely reject offers below about 30%, choosing nothing over a lopsided split. The clean theory is decisively rejected — not by a little, but at the heart of its prediction.

Key idea

The ultimatum game cleanly separates self-interest from fairness. Pure self-interest predicts "offer almost nothing, accept almost anything." Real people offer near-even splits and reject unfair ones at personal cost. The deviation is large, systematic, and cross-cultural — evidence that human payoffs include fairness and reciprocity, not just money.

Social preferences: payoffs beyond money

The fix is not to abandon game theory but to enrich the payoffs. People act as if their utility depends not only on their own material reward but also on how it compares to others' — a phenomenon called inequity aversion, modeled influentially by Fehr and Schmidt in 1999. A responder who suffers a fairness penalty from a lopsided split will reject it when that penalty outweighs the money on offer; the rejection is perfectly rational once fairness is in the utility function.

These social preferences — caring about fairness, reciprocity, others' welfare — explain a whole family of experimental findings: generosity in the dictator game, trust and repayment in the trust game, and cooperation in public-goods games sustained by costly punishment of free-riders. The lesson is subtle: people are not irrational; they are optimizing a richer objective than the textbook assumed.

Bounded reasoning: the level-k model

Fairness explains the ultimatum game, but a second, distinct departure concerns how deeply people reason about others. Equilibrium analysis assumes everyone thinks through everyone else's reasoning, infinitely deep. Real people stop after a step or two. The cleanest demonstration is the beauty contest game.

Everyone picks a number from 0 to 100. The winner is whoever comes closest to $\tfrac{2}{3}$ of the average of all guesses. What's the equilibrium? If everyone is fully rational and knows everyone else is, the only Nash equilibrium is everyone picks 0: whatever the average, you want two-thirds of it, which pulls every best response downward until the only consistent point is zero. Yet almost no one picks 0.

Definition 13.2 Level-k reasoning

In the level-k model, players differ in their depth of strategic reasoning. A level-0 player acts naively or randomly (in the beauty contest, guessing around the midpoint 50). A level-1 player best-responds to the belief that everyone else is level-0. A level-2 player best-responds to a population of level-1 players, and so on. Most people are level 1 or 2 — they reason a step or two about others, but not to the infinite depth equilibrium requires.

Trace it through. A level-0 guess anchors at 50. A level-1 player assumes others guess 50 and picks $\tfrac{2}{3} \times 50 \approx 33$. A level-2 player assumes others are level-1 and picks $\tfrac{2}{3} \times 33 \approx 22$. Each extra step of reasoning multiplies the guess by $\tfrac{2}{3}$, marching toward the Nash equilibrium of 0 — but real populations stop early.

Figure 13.1 · Level-k reasoning in the beauty contest
Each level of reasoning multiplies the guess by $\tfrac{2}{3}$: a level-0 anchor of 50 becomes 33 at level 1, 22 at level 2, and so on, converging to the Nash equilibrium of 0 only in the limit of infinite reasoning. Experimentally (Nagel, 1995), winning numbers cluster around 20-35 — exactly where one or two levels of reasoning land. Almost no one plays the equilibrium, because almost no one reasons infinitely deep.
Proposition 13.3 Level-k guesses converge to Nash

In the $\tfrac{2}{3}$-beauty contest, a level-$k$ player anchored at 50 guesses $50 \cdot \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^k$. As the reasoning depth $k \to \infty$, this converges to $0$, the unique Nash equilibrium. Finite reasoning yields guesses strictly above 0, matching observed behavior.

By definition a level-0 player guesses the anchor $g_0 = 50$. A level-1 player believes everyone is level-0, so believes the average will be 50, and best-responds by guessing two-thirds of it: $g_1 = \tfrac{2}{3}\cdot 50$. A level-2 player believes everyone is level-1, expects the average to be $g_1$, and guesses $g_2 = \tfrac{2}{3} g_1 = \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^2 50$. By induction, a level-$k$ player guesses

$$g_k = 50 \cdot \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^{k}.$$

Since the common ratio $\tfrac{2}{3}$ satisfies $0 < \tfrac{2}{3} < 1$, this geometric sequence converges to $0$ as $k \to \infty$:

$$\lim_{k \to \infty} 50\left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^k = 0.$$

The limit $0$ is exactly the Nash equilibrium reached by iterated elimination of dominated strategies (no guess above $\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot 100$ can ever win, then none above $\left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^2 100$, and so on down to 0). So full rationality corresponds to infinite reasoning depth — the $k \to \infty$ limit — while any finite level gives a strictly positive guess. Since experimental subjects reason only a step or two, their guesses land well above zero, near $g_1$ and $g_2$. ∎

Key idea

Nash equilibrium assumes infinite depth of reasoning — everyone thinks about everyone thinking, without end. The level-k model says real people stop after one or two steps. This explains why equilibrium predictions are systematically off in games solved by deep iteration, and why "thinking one step ahead of the crowd" — not infinitely ahead — is often the winning move.

Learning, mistakes, and quantal response

Two further refinements round out the behavioral picture. First, people learn: in repeated play, behavior often drifts toward equilibrium as players adjust to experience, even if first-round play is far off. The beauty contest, replayed, sees guesses fall round after round toward 0 — reasoning depth effectively increases with feedback. Second, people make mistakes, and better options are chosen more often but not exclusively. The quantal response equilibrium (McKelvey and Palfrey, 1995) formalizes this: instead of always best-responding, players choose better actions with higher probability, so a little noise blurs the sharp edges of equilibrium. This single idea explains many small, systematic deviations that pure Nash analysis treats as error.

Together, social preferences, bounded reasoning, learning, and choice noise turn game theory from a theory of idealized agents into a far more accurate — and more useful — account of real strategic behavior, without discarding the elegant scaffolding the earlier chapters built.

Exercise 13.1 Easy

In the $\tfrac{2}{3}$-beauty contest, compute the guesses of a level-1, level-2, and level-3 player, each anchored at a level-0 guess of 50. Round to one decimal place.

Solution

Each level multiplies the previous expected average by $\tfrac{2}{3}$, starting from the level-0 anchor of 50:

Level-1: $\tfrac{2}{3}\times 50 = 33.3$.   Level-2: $\tfrac{2}{3}\times 33.3 = 22.2$.   Level-3: $\tfrac{2}{3}\times 22.2 = 14.8$.

Equivalently, level-$k$ guesses $50(\tfrac{2}{3})^k$: $33.3, 22.2, 14.8$. The guesses fall toward the Nash equilibrium of 0 but remain well above it at low reasoning depth — which is where most real players sit.

Exercise 13.2 Medium

A responder in the ultimatum game over $20 has utility $u = x - \tfrac{1}{2}\max(s - x, 0)$, where $x$ is their own payoff and $s$ is the proposer's payoff (so the term penalizes being behind). The proposer offers the responder $6, keeping $14. Does the responder accept? What is the smallest offer (in whole dollars) the responder would accept?

Solution

Offer of $6. Then $x = 6$, $s = 14$, and the responder is behind by $s - x = 8$. Utility from accepting: $u = 6 - \tfrac{1}{2}(8) = 6 - 4 = 2$. Rejecting gives $0$. Since $2 > 0$, the responder accepts.

Smallest acceptable offer. For an offer $x$ (with $s = 20 - x$ and $x < 10$ so the responder is behind), accepting gives

$u = x - \tfrac{1}{2}\big((20 - x) - x\big) = x - \tfrac{1}{2}(20 - 2x) = x - 10 + x = 2x - 10$.

The responder accepts when $u \geq 0$, i.e. $2x - 10 \geq 0 \Rightarrow x \geq 5$. So the smallest acceptable whole-dollar offer is $5 — far above the "any positive amount" of the purely selfish prediction. Inequity aversion has shifted the acceptance threshold up to a quarter of the pie, squarely in the experimentally observed range.

Exercise 13.3 Medium

Explain why the subgame-perfect prediction for the ultimatum game ("offer the smallest positive amount") is logically valid yet empirically wrong. What single assumption, when relaxed, reconciles the theory with the data — and how?

Solution

Why it's valid. The backward-induction argument is internally flawless: if players value only their own money, a responder strictly prefers any positive amount to zero, so accepts everything positive; anticipating this, the proposer offers the minimum. Given the premise, the conclusion is inescapable.

Why it's empirically wrong. The data contradict it sharply — offers cluster near 40-50% and low offers are routinely rejected. The flaw isn't the logic; it's the premise.

The assumption to relax: pure self-interest. If we let payoffs include a concern for fairness (inequity aversion), a responder facing a lopsided split suffers a fairness penalty that can exceed the small money offered, making rejection a genuine best response. Proposers, anticipating this — and often valuing fairness themselves — offer near-even splits. The game-theoretic machinery (backward induction, subgame perfection) is retained intact; only the utility function is enriched. This is the central move of behavioral game theory: keep the structure, fix the payoffs.

Exercise 13.4 Hard

Consider a beauty contest where the target is $p$ times the average, for a general $p \in (0,1)$. (a) Show the level-$k$ guess (anchored at 50) is $50 p^k$ and find its limit. (b) Now suppose $p > 1$ (target is, say, $\tfrac{3}{2}$ of the average, capped at 100). What happens to level-$k$ reasoning, and what is the Nash equilibrium?

Solution

(a) A level-1 player best-responds to a believed average of 50 by guessing $p\cdot 50$. A level-2 player guesses $p(p\cdot 50) = p^2\cdot 50$, and by induction level-$k$ guesses $50\,p^k$. Since $0 < p < 1$, $\lim_{k\to\infty} 50 p^k = 0$ — the guesses shrink to the Nash equilibrium of 0, just as in the $p = \tfrac{2}{3}$ case (only the speed of convergence changes: smaller $p$ converges faster).

(b) With $p > 1$, each level of reasoning multiplies the guess upward: $50 p^k$ grows with $k$. Reasoning now pushes guesses toward the top of the range. Iterated best response drives everyone to the cap, so the Nash equilibrium is for everyone to guess 100 (the maximum): if everyone guesses 100, the target is $\min(p\cdot 100, 100) = 100$, and 100 is indeed the best response — a consistent equilibrium at the upper corner. The direction of the iteration flips entirely, showing how the sign of $p - 1$ determines whether reasoning herds the population down to 0 or up to the ceiling.

Continuous Strategies: Calculus Applications

62 min read

Capstone chapter

Every game so far has had a handful of discrete choices. But the most powerful applications of game theory in economics live in the continuous world — where a firm sets any quantity or price, a shop picks any location, a contestant exerts any level of effort. Here strategies are real numbers, best responses become functions, and the tool for finding equilibrium is calculus. This capstone applies the first-order condition to the classic models that built modern industrial economics.

Two factories make the same steel. Neither posts a price or a fixed output; each morning the manager decides how many tons to roll, knowing the rival is deciding too, and knowing the market price will sag if together they flood the market. There is no short list of options to compare — output is a dial that turns smoothly from zero to the capacity of the plant. To find the resting point of this game, counting payoffs in a table is hopeless. You need the one move from calculus that finds the top of any smooth hill: set the derivative to zero.

That single move — the first-order condition — unlocks the whole chapter. When a player's strategy is a continuous variable and their payoff is a smooth function of it, the best response is found by differentiating the payoff with respect to one's own choice and setting it to zero. Do that for each player, solve the resulting equations together, and you have the Nash equilibrium. We'll apply this recipe to six landmark models.

Key idea

When strategies are continuous, the Nash equilibrium recipe becomes calculus: for each player, differentiate their payoff with respect to their own strategy, holding rivals' choices fixed, and set the derivative to zero. This first-order condition gives each player's reaction function (best response to the others). Solving all the reaction functions simultaneously yields the equilibrium.

Definition 14.1 Reaction function and the first-order condition

For a player with continuous strategy $s_i$ and payoff $\pi_i(s_i, s_{-i})$ that is smooth and concave in $s_i$, the best response solves the first-order condition

$$\frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial s_i} = 0.$$

The solution, written as a function of the rivals' strategies $s_{-i}$, is the player's reaction function (or best-response function) $s_i = R_i(s_{-i})$. A Nash equilibrium is a strategy profile where every player is on their reaction function simultaneously — the solution of the system of first-order conditions. (The partial derivative $\partial \pi_i / \partial s_i$ means differentiate with respect to $s_i$ while treating the rivals' choices as constants.)

Cournot competition: choosing quantities

The first and most famous continuous game, due to Antoine Cournot in 1838 — decades before game theory had a name — has firms competing by choosing quantities. Two firms produce an identical good. The market price falls as total output rises, according to the inverse demand $P = a - bQ$, where $Q = q_1 + q_2$ is total output. Each firm has constant marginal cost $c$ and chooses its own quantity to maximize profit, taking the rival's quantity as given.

Firm 1's profit is revenue minus cost:

$$\pi_1 = \bigl(a - b(q_1 + q_2) - c\bigr)\,q_1.$$
Proposition 14.2 The Cournot equilibrium

In the symmetric Cournot duopoly with inverse demand $P = a - bQ$ and marginal cost $c$, each firm produces

$$q^* = \frac{a - c}{3b},$$

so total output is $\frac{2(a-c)}{3b}$ and the price is $P = \frac{a + 2c}{3}$. With $n$ symmetric firms, each produces $\frac{a-c}{(n+1)b}$, and as $n \to \infty$ the price falls toward marginal cost $c$ — competition restores the competitive outcome.

Differentiate firm 1's profit with respect to its own quantity $q_1$, treating $q_2$ as fixed. Expanding $\pi_1 = (a - c)q_1 - b q_1^2 - b q_1 q_2$ and applying the first-order condition:

$$\frac{\partial \pi_1}{\partial q_1} = a - c - 2b q_1 - b q_2 = 0.$$

Solving for $q_1$ gives firm 1's reaction function — its best output as a function of the rival's:

$$q_1 = R_1(q_2) = \frac{a - c - b q_2}{2b}.$$

By symmetry, firm 2's reaction function is $q_2 = \frac{a - c - b q_1}{2b}$. In a symmetric equilibrium $q_1 = q_2 = q^*$; substitute into the first-order condition:

$$a - c - 2b q^* - b q^* = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; a - c = 3b q^* \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; q^* = \frac{a-c}{3b}.$$

Total output is $2q^* = \frac{2(a-c)}{3b}$ and price $P = a - b\cdot 2q^* = \frac{a+2c}{3}$. For $n$ firms the symmetric first-order condition becomes $a - c - b q^*(n+1) = 0$, giving $q^* = \frac{a-c}{(n+1)b}$; total output $\frac{n(a-c)}{(n+1)b} \to \frac{a-c}{b}$ as $n\to\infty$, driving $P \to c$. The second-order condition $\partial^2 \pi_1/\partial q_1^2 = -2b < 0$ confirms each is a maximum. ∎

The equilibrium is the point where both reaction functions hold at once — visually, where the two best-response lines cross.

Figure 14.1 · Cournot reaction functions
Each firm's reaction function gives its profit-maximizing output as a function of the rival's. Firm 1's (violet) slopes down — the more the rival makes, the less firm 1 wants to make — and so does firm 2's (teal). They cross once, at the Cournot-Nash equilibrium where both best-respond simultaneously. With $a = 100$, $b = 1$, $c = 10$, that crossing is at $q_1 = q_2 = 30$.
Example 14.1
A Cournot duopoly in numbers

Let inverse demand be $P = 100 - Q$ (so $a = 100$, $b = 1$) and marginal cost $c = 10$. Then each firm produces

$q^* = \dfrac{a - c}{3b} = \dfrac{100 - 10}{3} = 30$.

Total output is $Q = 60$, the price is $P = 100 - 60 = 40$, and each firm earns profit $(P - c)\,q^* = (40 - 10)\cdot 30 = 900$.

Compare the extremes: a monopoly would produce $\frac{a-c}{2b} = 45$ at price $55$, while perfect competition would drive output to $90$ at price $10$. The Cournot duopoly sits in between — more output and lower prices than monopoly, but short of full competition. Adding firms slides the outcome toward the competitive ideal.

Stackelberg competition: moving first

Cournot's firms choose simultaneously. What if one moves first? Heinrich von Stackelberg asked this in 1934, and the answer reveals a genuine first-mover advantage — a continuous-strategy cousin of the backward induction from Chapter 4. The leader chooses output first; the follower observes it and best-responds along the Cournot reaction function. The leader, anticipating this, optimizes knowing how the follower will react.

Proposition 14.3 The Stackelberg equilibrium

When firm 1 leads and firm 2 follows (same demand $P = a - bQ$, cost $c$), the leader produces $q_1^* = \frac{a-c}{2b}$ and the follower produces $q_2^* = \frac{a-c}{4b}$. The leader makes more than its Cournot output and earns higher profit; the follower makes less. Moving first, with commitment, is an advantage.

Solve by backward induction. The follower observes $q_1$ and best-responds along the Cournot reaction function from before:

$$q_2 = R_2(q_1) = \frac{a - c - b q_1}{2b}.$$

The leader anticipates this and substitutes it into its own profit, so the leader's profit depends on $q_1$ alone:

$$\pi_1 = \Bigl(a - b\bigl(q_1 + R_2(q_1)\bigr) - c\Bigr)q_1 = \frac{(a - c - b q_1)\,q_1}{2}.$$

Now apply the first-order condition to this reduced profit, differentiating with respect to $q_1$:

$$\frac{\partial \pi_1}{\partial q_1} = \frac{a - c - 2b q_1}{2} = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; q_1^* = \frac{a-c}{2b}.$$

The follower then produces $q_2^* = R_2(q_1^*) = \frac{a - c - b\cdot \frac{a-c}{2b}}{2b} = \frac{a-c}{4b}$. The leader's output equals the monopoly quantity and exceeds its Cournot level $\frac{a-c}{3b}$; the follower's is below it. By committing to a large output first, the leader forces the follower to cede ground — the value of commitment, exactly the lesson of Chapter 4 now in continuous form. ∎

Example 14.2
Leading the market

With $P = 100 - Q$ and $c = 10$ as before, the Stackelberg leader produces $q_1^* = \frac{90}{2} = 45$ and the follower $q_2^* = \frac{90}{4} = 22.5$. Total output is $67.5$ and the price is $P = 32.5$.

The leader earns $(32.5 - 10)\cdot 45 = 1012.5$ versus the follower's $(32.5 - 10)\cdot 22.5 = 506.25$ — exactly double. Both make less per unit than under Cournot (the price is lower because total output is higher), but the leader's larger volume more than compensates. Compare each firm's Cournot profit of $900$: leading lifts profit to $1012.5$, while following drops it to $506.25$. Commitment pays.

Bertrand competition: choosing prices

Cournot's firms set quantities; Joseph Bertrand objected in 1883 that firms really set prices. The change seems minor but the result is dramatic. With identical products, consumers buy entirely from whoever is cheaper. So if your rival prices above marginal cost, you can undercut by a hair, capture the entire market, and still profit. Your rival reasons the same way. The undercutting spiral stops only when price equals marginal cost.

Proposition 14.4 The Bertrand paradox

In price competition with identical goods and common constant marginal cost $c$, the unique Nash equilibrium has both firms set price equal to marginal cost, $p_1 = p_2 = c$, earning zero profit. Just two competitors are enough to produce the perfectly competitive outcome.

The argument is a clean undercutting logic rather than a calculus exercise. Suppose both charge some $p > c$. Either firm can cut its price slightly, still stay above $c$, and grab the whole market instead of half — a profitable deviation. So no common price above $c$ is stable. Pricing below $c$ means selling at a loss, worse than selling nothing. Only $p_1 = p_2 = c$ survives: neither can profitably undercut (there's no room left above cost) nor raise price (they'd lose all sales to the rival). This Bertrand paradox — that a mere duopoly yields the competitive price — is why the distinction between competing on quantity versus price matters so much in practice, and why real firms work hard to differentiate their products and escape it.

Key idea

Whether firms compete on quantity (Cournot) or price (Bertrand) transforms the outcome. Cournot duopoly keeps prices well above cost; Bertrand duopoly with identical goods crushes price down to marginal cost. The same two firms, the same demand — only the choice variable differs. This is why product differentiation, which softens Bertrand's brutal logic, is a central business strategy.

Hotelling competition: choosing location

Strategies need not be quantities or prices — they can be positions. Harold Hotelling's 1929 model imagines a "linear city": consumers spread evenly along a street of length 1, and two shops choose where to locate. Each consumer buys from the nearer shop. Where do the shops end up?

The surprising answer is that both crowd to the center. If one shop sits left of center, its rival can edge just to its right and capture the entire larger side of the street. Each keeps shifting toward the middle to avoid being outflanked, until both stand back-to-back at the midpoint $\tfrac{1}{2}$ — splitting the market evenly. This is the famous principle of minimum differentiation.

Proposition 14.5 Hotelling's principle of minimum differentiation

In the linear-city model with consumers uniform on $[0,1]$, fixed equal prices, and two shops choosing locations to maximize market share, the unique Nash equilibrium has both shops locate at the center $\tfrac{1}{2}$. Competition for the middle drives the rivals together, not apart.

With fixed equal prices, each consumer simply buys from the nearer shop, so a shop's market share is the fraction of the street closer to it. Let the shops locate at $x_1 \leq x_2$. Shop 1 captures everyone to its left plus everyone up to the midpoint between the two shops, a share of $\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}$; shop 2 captures the rest, $1 - \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}$.

Fix shop 2's position and consider shop 1's best response. Shop 1's share $\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}$ increases in $x_1$ as long as $x_1 < x_2$ — it always wants to move rightward, toward (and just left of) its rival, to swallow more of the middle. Symmetrically, shop 2 always wants to move leftward toward shop 1. Neither can profit by separating. The only configuration in which neither wants to move is when they meet: $x_1 = x_2$. And that common point must be the center $\tfrac{1}{2}$ — if both stood together off-center, say left of $\tfrac{1}{2}$, either could jump to the other side and seize the larger half of the street. At $x_1 = x_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}$ each gets exactly half and any deviation yields less. ∎

The same force explains why competing gas stations cluster at one intersection, why rival news networks converge on the political center, and why two ice-cream carts on a beach end up side by side in the middle. (Note: if the shops also compete on price, with a quadratic travel cost $t$, the logic reverses — firms differentiate to soften price competition, locating at the two ends with equilibrium price $p^* = c + t$. Whether rivals converge or separate hinges on what else they compete over.)

Tullock contests: the cost of competing for a prize

Many real contests — lobbying for a government contract, litigation, R&D races, sports rivalries — share a structure: players spend costly effort to raise their probability of winning a fixed prize. Gordon Tullock's contest model (1980) captures this and exposes how much value gets burned in the fight.

Two contestants exert efforts $x_1, x_2$. The prize $V$ goes to player 1 with probability $\frac{x_1}{x_1 + x_2}$ — your share of total effort is your chance of winning. Effort is costly one-for-one. Player 1's expected payoff is

$$\pi_1 = V\cdot\frac{x_1}{x_1 + x_2} - x_1.$$
Proposition 14.6 The Tullock contest equilibrium

In the symmetric two-player Tullock contest for a prize $V$, each contestant exerts effort

$$x^* = \frac{V}{4},$$

so total effort is $\frac{V}{2}$ — half the prize is dissipated in the struggle to win it. Each player's expected net payoff is $\frac{V}{4}$.

Differentiate player 1's payoff with respect to its own effort $x_1$, treating $x_2$ as fixed. Using the quotient rule on the win-probability term:

$$\frac{\partial \pi_1}{\partial x_1} = V\cdot\frac{x_2}{(x_1 + x_2)^2} - 1 = 0.$$

The derivative of $\frac{x_1}{x_1+x_2}$ is $\frac{(x_1+x_2) - x_1}{(x_1+x_2)^2} = \frac{x_2}{(x_1+x_2)^2}$, which is why the rival's effort $x_2$ appears in the numerator. Impose symmetry $x_1 = x_2 = x^*$, so $x_1 + x_2 = 2x^*$:

$$V\cdot\frac{x^*}{(2x^*)^2} = 1 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \frac{V}{4x^*} = 1 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x^* = \frac{V}{4}.$$

Each contestant spends $\frac{V}{4}$, so combined effort is $\frac{V}{2}$ — half the prize's value is consumed by the contest itself. Each wins with probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$, for expected gross winnings $\frac{V}{2}$, minus effort $\frac{V}{4}$, leaving net $\frac{V}{4}$. The dissipation is the dark side of competition: resources poured into winning a prize rather than creating value. ∎

Example 14.3
Lobbying for a contract

Two firms lobby for a government contract worth $V = 100$. Each spends $x^* = \frac{100}{4} = 25$ on lobbying. Total lobbying spend is $50$ — half the contract's value evaporates into lawyers, consultants, and influence campaigns that produce nothing.

Each firm wins with probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$, for expected payoff $\tfrac{1}{2}(100) - 25 = 25$. From society's view this is the rent-seeking waste Tullock warned of: the prize is merely transferred, but the competition for it consumes real resources. The model predicts that more valuable prizes — or more contestants — draw even more wasteful effort, a sobering counterpoint to the usual presumption that competition is benign.

Public goods: the free-rider problem

Our final application turns from rivalry to the failure of cooperation. A public good — clean air, a shared park, open-source software, a stable climate — benefits everyone once provided, regardless of who paid. Each of $n$ people decides how much to contribute. The trouble: you enjoy everyone's contributions but bear the full cost of your own.

Suppose each person has endowment $w$ and contributes $g_i$. Every dollar contributed returns $\alpha$ to each of the $n$ people, with $0 < \alpha < 1 < n\alpha$. Person $i$'s payoff is what they keep plus their share of the total public good:

$$\pi_i = (w - g_i) + \alpha \sum_{j=1}^{n} g_j.$$
Proposition 14.7 The free-rider theorem

In the voluntary-contribution public-goods game with $0 < \alpha < 1 < n\alpha$, the unique Nash equilibrium has everyone contribute nothing, $g_i^* = 0$. Yet the social optimum has everyone contribute their entire endowment. The gap between them is the tragedy of the commons.

Differentiate person $i$'s payoff with respect to their own contribution $g_i$, holding everyone else's fixed. Only two terms involve $g_i$: the $-g_i$ they give up, and their own share $\alpha g_i$ of the public good:

$$\frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial g_i} = -1 + \alpha.$$

Since $\alpha < 1$, this derivative is negative: every dollar contributed costs the individual $1$ but returns them only $\alpha < 1$ personally. Payoff strictly falls in $g_i$, so each person's best response is to contribute as little as possible: $g_i^* = 0$. This holds regardless of what others do — contributing nothing is a dominant strategy — so the unique Nash equilibrium is universal free-riding.

Now the social optimum. Total welfare is $\sum_i \pi_i = nw - \sum_i g_i + n\alpha\sum_i g_j = nw + (n\alpha - 1)\sum_j g_j$. Since $n\alpha > 1$, the coefficient $(n\alpha - 1) > 0$, so total welfare rises with every contribution — the social optimum is for everyone to contribute everything. The contradiction between the individual calculus ($\alpha < 1$, give nothing) and the collective one ($n\alpha > 1$, give all) is the mathematical signature of a social dilemma. ∎

Key idea

The public-goods game is the Prisoner's Dilemma (Chapter 2) writ large across $n$ players and continuous contributions. Each individual's calculus says "give nothing" (private return $\alpha < 1$ per dollar), while the group's calculus says "give everything" (social return $n\alpha > 1$). Voluntary provision fails, which is why public goods are funded by taxation, matched by institutions, or sustained — as Chapter 3 showed — by repetition and punishment.

Bargaining: deriving the Nash split with calculus

Chapter 6 introduced the Nash bargaining solution and asserted its formula. With calculus we can now derive it in one line — it is simply the maximizer of a smooth product. Two players split a pie of size 1: player 1 receives $x$ and player 2 receives $1 - x$, with disagreement payoffs $d_1$ and $d_2$. The Nash bargaining solution maximizes the Nash product of the two players' gains over disagreement.

Proposition 14.8 The Nash bargaining split by first-order condition

The split maximizing the Nash product $(x - d_1)\bigl((1-x) - d_2\bigr)$ is

$$x^* = d_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(1 - d_1 - d_2\bigr),$$

each player's disagreement payoff plus half the surplus — exactly the Chapter 6 result, now obtained by setting a derivative to zero. With unequal bargaining weights $\alpha, \beta$ (the generalized Nash solution maximizing $(x-d_1)^\alpha((1-x)-d_2)^\beta$), the surplus is split in proportion to the weights: $x^* = d_1 + \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}(1 - d_1 - d_2)$.

Write the Nash product as a function of player 1's share $x$:

$$N(x) = (x - d_1)\bigl((1 - x) - d_2\bigr).$$

Differentiate with respect to $x$ using the product rule and set the derivative to zero:

$$N'(x) = \bigl((1-x) - d_2\bigr) - (x - d_1) = (1 - d_1 - d_2) - 2x + 2d_1\cdots$$

Collecting terms, $N'(x) = d_1 - d_2 + 1 - 2x = 0$, which solves to

$$x^* = \frac{1 + d_1 - d_2}{2} = d_1 + \frac{1 - d_1 - d_2}{2}.$$

The second derivative is $N''(x) = -2 < 0$, confirming a maximum. The generalized version maximizes $\alpha\ln(x - d_1) + \beta\ln((1-x) - d_2)$ (the log of the weighted product); its first-order condition $\frac{\alpha}{x - d_1} = \frac{\beta}{(1-x) - d_2}$ rearranges to $x^* = d_1 + \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}(1 - d_1 - d_2)$ — the surplus shared in proportion to bargaining power, recovering the equal split when $\alpha = \beta$. ∎

So the bargaining outcome of Chapter 6 is a calculus optimum: take the derivative of the product of gains, set it to zero, and the fair split falls out. With $d_1 = 0.2$ and $d_2 = 0.1$, this gives $x^* = 0.2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.7) = 0.55$, matching Example 6.1 exactly.

Bayesian Cournot: competing under cost uncertainty

Back in Chapter 8 (Exercise 8.3) we promised to solve a Cournot game with private costs once we had calculus. Here it is — the calculus face of Bayesian Nash equilibrium. Firm 1's marginal cost $c_1$ is common knowledge, but firm 2 privately knows whether it is a low-cost type (cost $c_L$, probability $t$) or a high-cost type (cost $c_H$, probability $1-t$). Firm 1 must choose its output knowing only the odds of facing each type; each type of firm 2 chooses its own output knowing its cost. This is exactly the Harsanyi setup of Chapter 8, now with continuous strategies.

Proposition 14.9 Bayesian Cournot equilibrium

With demand $P = a - bQ$, the Bayesian Nash equilibrium has each type of firm 2 best-respond to firm 1's output, while firm 1 best-responds to firm 2's expected output $\mathbb{E}[q_2] = t\,q_2^L + (1-t)\,q_2^H$. The three outputs solve the system of first-order conditions simultaneously: firm 1 sets $q_1 = \frac{a - c_1 - b\,\mathbb{E}[q_2]}{2b}$, and each firm-2 type sets $q_2^k = \frac{a - b q_1 - c_k}{2b}$. The low-cost type produces more than the high-cost type.

There are three decisions to pin down: firm 1's output $q_1$, and firm 2's output in each of its two types, $q_2^L$ and $q_2^H$. Each is found from its own first-order condition.

Firm 2, each type. A firm-2 type with cost $c_k$ knows its cost and observes nothing of firm 1 beyond its equilibrium output $q_1$; it maximizes $(a - b(q_1 + q_2^k) - c_k)q_2^k$. Its first-order condition gives the familiar reaction function

$$q_2^k = \frac{a - b q_1 - c_k}{2b}, \qquad k \in \{L, H\}.$$

Firm 1. Firm 1 does not know which type it faces, so it maximizes expected profit, averaging over firm 2's types with the prior:

$$\mathbb{E}[\pi_1] = t\,(a - b(q_1 + q_2^L) - c_1)q_1 + (1-t)(a - b(q_1 + q_2^H) - c_1)q_1.$$

Differentiating with respect to $q_1$ and setting to zero, the type-specific terms collapse into firm 2's expected output $\mathbb{E}[q_2] = t q_2^L + (1-t)q_2^H$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi_1]}{\partial q_1} = a - c_1 - 2b q_1 - b\,\mathbb{E}[q_2] = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; q_1 = \frac{a - c_1 - b\,\mathbb{E}[q_2]}{2b}.$$

These three equations — one for $q_1$, one for each firm-2 type — form a linear system. Solving it simultaneously yields the Bayesian Nash equilibrium. Because $c_L < c_H$, the reaction functions give $q_2^L > q_2^H$: the low-cost type always produces more. Firm 1, unable to tell which it faces, optimally responds to the average. ∎

Example 14.4
Cournot against an unknown rival

Let $P = 100 - Q$ and firm 1's cost be $c_1 = 10$. Firm 2 is low-cost ($c_L = 16$) or high-cost ($c_H = 40$), each with probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$. Solving the three first-order conditions together gives

$q_1^* = 36, \qquad q_2^L = 24, \qquad q_2^H = 12$,

with firm 2's expected output $\mathbb{E}[q_2] = \tfrac{1}{2}(24) + \tfrac{1}{2}(12) = 18$. Check firm 1's best response: $\frac{100 - 10 - 18}{2} = 36$ ✓. The realized price is $40$ if firm 2 is low-cost and $52$ if high-cost.

Here's the subtle payoff of the analysis. If firm 2's low cost were public knowledge, firm 1 would face a strong rival for certain and produce only $32$. The uncertainty — the chance firm 2 is the weak high-cost type — leads firm 1 to produce more ($36$), hedging toward the more favorable scenario. Incomplete information doesn't just add noise; it systematically shifts the equilibrium. This is the Harsanyi framework of Chapter 8 delivering a concrete, calculable prediction.

First-price auctions: deriving the equilibrium bid

Chapter 5 stated that in a first-price auction with $n$ bidders whose values are uniform on $[0,1]$, the equilibrium is to bid $b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}v$. That, too, is a Bayesian Nash equilibrium — each bidder's value is their private type — and calculus delivers the bidding function. The trick is to ask: if everyone else bids by the increasing rule $b(\cdot)$, what is my best bid, and when is bidding "as if my value were the truth" optimal?

Proposition 14.10 First-price auction equilibrium bid

In a first-price sealed-bid auction with $n$ bidders whose values are independent and uniform on $[0,1]$, the symmetric Bayesian Nash equilibrium has each bidder bid

$$b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}\,v.$$

Each bidder shades their bid below their value by the factor $\frac{n-1}{n}$. With two bidders this is half the value; as $n \to \infty$ the shading vanishes and bids approach true values.

Suppose every other bidder uses the increasing strategy $b(\cdot)$, and consider a bidder with value $v$ deciding what to bid. Because $b$ is increasing, choosing a bid is equivalent to choosing a "pretend value" $z$ and bidding $b(z)$. This bidder wins only if their bid is highest — i.e. if all $n-1$ rivals have values below $z$. With uniform values, each rival's value is below $z$ with probability $z$, so the win probability is $z^{n-1}$.

The bidder's expected payoff from pretending to have value $z$ is the surplus $(v - b(z))$ times the win probability:

$$U(z; v) = \bigl(v - b(z)\bigr)\,z^{n-1}.$$

Maximize over $z$. The first-order condition is

$$\frac{\partial U}{\partial z} = -b'(z)\,z^{n-1} + \bigl(v - b(z)\bigr)(n-1)z^{n-2} = 0.$$

In equilibrium honesty must be optimal — the bidder should want to choose $z = v$ (no profitable misrepresentation). Imposing $z = v$ turns the first-order condition into a differential equation for $b$:

$$b'(v)\,v + (n-1)\,b(v) = (n-1)\,v.$$

One verifies the linear rule $b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}v$ solves it: then $b'(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}$, and the left side is $\frac{n-1}{n}v + (n-1)\frac{n-1}{n}v = \frac{(n-1)v}{n}\bigl(1 + (n-1)\bigr) = (n-1)v$, matching the right side. So bidding $\frac{n-1}{n}v$ is the symmetric equilibrium. ∎

The intuition: you shade your bid down to leave yourself some surplus, but shading too much costs you the win. The optimal compromise is $\frac{n-1}{n}$ of your value — and the more rivals there are, the less you dare shade, because the competition is fiercer.

All-pay auctions: when everyone pays

A final continuous game with private information sharpens the contest idea from earlier in this chapter. In an all-pay auction, every bidder pays their bid whether or not they win — a model of lobbying, war, litigation, and any costly competition where effort is sunk regardless of outcome. It is the continuous, incomplete-information cousin of the Tullock contest, and its equilibrium reveals a striking connection back to Chapter 5.

Proposition 14.11 All-pay auction equilibrium and revenue equivalence

In a two-bidder all-pay auction with values independent and uniform on $[0,1]$ (highest bid wins, everyone pays their bid), the symmetric equilibrium bid is

$$b(v) = \frac{v^2}{2}.$$

The seller's expected revenue is $\frac{1}{3}$ — exactly the revenue of the first-price and second-price auctions, an instance of the revenue equivalence theorem from Chapter 5.

With two bidders and the rival using increasing strategy $b(\cdot)$, a bidder with value $v$ who pretends to have value $z$ wins when the single rival's value is below $z$ — probability $z$ under the uniform distribution. But the bidder pays $b(z)$ regardless of winning. So the expected payoff is the prize times the win probability minus the certain payment:

$$U(z; v) = v\cdot z - b(z).$$

The first-order condition is $\frac{\partial U}{\partial z} = v - b'(z) = 0$. Imposing the equilibrium requirement that truth-telling is optimal, set $z = v$ to obtain $b'(v) = v$. Integrating, with $b(0) = 0$,

$$b(v) = \int_0^v s\,ds = \frac{v^2}{2}.$$

For revenue, both bidders pay their bids, so expected revenue is $2\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{v^2}{2}\right] = \mathbb{E}[v^2] = \int_0^1 v^2\,dv = \frac{1}{3}$. This equals the expected revenue of the first-price auction (Example 5.1) and the second-price auction — different rules, identical expected revenue. The all-pay auction extracts the same total even though losers pay too: bidders compensate by shading their bids far more aggressively (note $\frac{v^2}{2} \leq \frac{v}{2}$ for $v \leq 1$). ∎

Key idea

Continuous strategies and calculus reach far beyond oligopoly. The same set-the-derivative-to-zero recipe derives the Nash bargaining split, solves Bayesian Cournot under cost uncertainty, and produces the equilibrium bid functions of first-price and all-pay auctions — closing the loops opened in Chapters 5, 6, and 8. Whenever a strategy is a continuous variable and a payoff is smooth, the first-order condition is the master key, even when private information makes the payoff an expected value.

Repeated Cournot: sustaining collusion

Chapter 3 showed that repetition can sustain cooperation through the folk theorem and grim-trigger strategies. Now we make that concrete in the continuous setting: when can two Cournot rivals quietly hold output down to the monopoly level — colluding without a contract — and split the higher profit? The answer is a critical discount factor, derived by comparing the gain from cheating against the cost of the punishment that follows.

Suppose the two firms could, if they cooperated, jointly restrict output to the monopoly quantity and split it, each earning the collusive profit $\pi_M = \frac{(a-c)^2}{8b}$ per period — more than the one-shot Cournot profit $\pi_C = \frac{(a-c)^2}{9b}$. The temptation: a firm can deviate, best-responding to its rival's restrained output and grabbing a one-period windfall $\pi_D = \frac{9(a-c)^2}{64b}$. Under grim-trigger, that defection triggers a reversion to Cournot competition forever after.

Proposition 14.12 The critical discount factor for collusion

In the infinitely repeated Cournot duopoly with grim-trigger punishment, collusion at the monopoly output is sustainable as a subgame-perfect equilibrium if and only if the discount factor satisfies

$$\delta \;\geq\; \delta^* = \frac{9}{17} \approx 0.53.$$

Patient enough firms $(\delta \geq \tfrac{9}{17})$ collude tacitly and charge near-monopoly prices; impatient firms $(\delta < \tfrac{9}{17})$ cannot resist cheating, and competition prevails.

Under grim-trigger, a firm compares two paths. Cooperate forever: earn the collusive profit $\pi_M$ every period, for a discounted total of

$$\frac{\pi_M}{1 - \delta}.$$

Cheat once, then suffer punishment: earn the one-shot deviation windfall $\pi_D$ this period, then the Cournot profit $\pi_C$ forever after:

$$\pi_D + \frac{\delta\,\pi_C}{1 - \delta}.$$

Collusion holds when cooperating is at least as good as cheating:

$$\frac{\pi_M}{1-\delta} \;\geq\; \pi_D + \frac{\delta\,\pi_C}{1-\delta}.$$

Now substitute the three profits. They all share the factor $\frac{(a-c)^2}{b}$, which cancels, leaving the pure numbers $\pi_M = \tfrac{1}{8}$, $\pi_C = \tfrac{1}{9}$, $\pi_D = \tfrac{9}{64}$ (in those units). Multiplying through by $(1-\delta)$ and collecting terms in $\delta$:

$$\pi_M \geq (1-\delta)\pi_D + \delta\,\pi_C \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \pi_M - \pi_D \geq \delta(\pi_C - \pi_D).$$

Since $\pi_D > \pi_C$ (deviating beats Cournot) the bracket $\pi_C - \pi_D$ is negative, so dividing flips the inequality:

$$\delta \;\geq\; \frac{\pi_D - \pi_M}{\pi_D - \pi_C} = \frac{\tfrac{9}{64} - \tfrac{1}{8}}{\tfrac{9}{64} - \tfrac{1}{9}}.$$

Computing: the numerator is $\tfrac{9}{64} - \tfrac{8}{64} = \tfrac{1}{64}$, and the denominator is $\tfrac{81}{576} - \tfrac{64}{576} = \tfrac{17}{576}$. The ratio is $\frac{1/64}{17/576} = \frac{576}{64\cdot 17} = \frac{9}{17}$. Hence $\delta^* = \tfrac{9}{17} \approx 0.53$. ∎

Example 14.5
Will the duopolists collude?

With $P = 100 - Q$ and $c = 10$, the per-period profits are $\pi_M = \frac{90^2}{8} = 1012.5$ each from collusion, $\pi_C = \frac{90^2}{9} = 900$ from Cournot, and a deviation windfall of $\pi_D = \frac{9\cdot 90^2}{64} \approx 1139$.

If the firms discount the future at $\delta = 0.6$ (above $\tfrac{9}{17} \approx 0.53$), collusion holds: the extra $112.5$ per period from colluding, sustained forever, outweighs the one-time gain of $\approx 127$ from cheating once. At $\delta = 0.5$ (below the threshold), the temptation wins and the firms compete.

This is the folk theorem of Chapter 3 made quantitative: cartels are stable precisely when the participants are patient and the punishment is severe. It also explains why antitrust authorities watch for the conditions — few firms, frequent interaction, transparent prices — that lower the effective $\delta^*$ and make tacit collusion easy.

Screening: designing a menu for hidden types

Signaling (Chapter 7) had the informed party move first to reveal its type. Screening flips this: the uninformed party moves first, designing a menu of options so that each hidden type voluntarily self-selects — revealing itself through its choice. It is the mechanism-design mirror image of signaling, and the engine behind airline fare classes, insurance menus, and "good-better-best" product lines.

A monopolist sells a good of variable quality $q$ at cost $c(q) = \frac{q^2}{2}$ to two consumer types: a high type who values quality at $\theta_H$ per unit and a low type valuing it at $\theta_L < \theta_H$, in proportions $\beta$ and $1-\beta$. The seller cannot observe types, so it offers a menu of (quality, price) pairs and lets buyers choose. Two constraints bind the design: each type must want to participate (individual rationality), and each must prefer its own bundle to the other's (incentive compatibility — the screening counterpart of the no-mimicking conditions from Chapter 7).

Proposition 14.13 Optimal screening: no distortion at the top

With cost $c(q) = \frac{q^2}{2}$, the profit-maximizing menu sets the high type's quality at the efficient level $q_H = \theta_H$ (no distortion at the top), while the low type's quality is distorted downward below its efficient level:

$$q_L = \theta_L - \frac{\beta}{1-\beta}\,(\theta_H - \theta_L) \;<\; \theta_L.$$

The high type earns a positive information rent; the low type is held to zero surplus. Distorting the low type's quality is what lets the seller limit the rent it must concede to the high type.

A buyer of type $\theta$ choosing bundle $(q, T)$ (quality $q$, price $T$) gets surplus $\theta q - T$; the seller earns $T - \frac{q^2}{2}$. Standard arguments pin down which constraints bind: the low type's individual-rationality constraint (its surplus is driven to zero) and the high type's incentive-compatibility constraint (it must not prefer the low bundle). Using these to substitute for the prices, the seller's expected profit becomes a function of the two qualities alone:

$$\beta\!\left(\theta_H q_H - \tfrac{q_H^2}{2}\right) + (1-\beta)\!\left(\theta_L q_L - \tfrac{q_L^2}{2}\right) - \beta(\theta_H - \theta_L)\,q_L.$$

The first two terms are the efficient surplus from each type; the last term, $\beta(\theta_H - \theta_L)q_L$, is the information rent the seller must leave to the high type — and notice it grows with $q_L$.

Maximize by taking first-order conditions. For $q_H$, only the first term depends on it:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial q_H} = \beta(\theta_H - q_H) = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; q_H = \theta_H,$$

the efficient quality — no distortion at the top. For $q_L$, the rent term bites:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial q_L} = (1-\beta)(\theta_L - q_L) - \beta(\theta_H - \theta_L) = 0,$$

which solves to $q_L = \theta_L - \frac{\beta}{1-\beta}(\theta_H - \theta_L)$. Because the rent term subtracts, $q_L$ is pushed strictly below the efficient $\theta_L$: downward distortion at the bottom. The logic is a pure trade-off — lowering the low type's quality makes its bundle less tempting to the high type, shrinking the rent the seller surrenders, at the cost of some lost efficiency on the low type. The optimum balances the two. ∎

Example 14.6
Good, better, best

Let $\theta_H = 10$, $\theta_L = 6$, and suppose half the market is each type ($\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}$). The high type gets the efficient quality $q_H = \theta_H = 10$. The low type's quality is

$q_L = 6 - \frac{1/2}{1/2}(10 - 6) = 6 - 4 = 2$,

distorted well below its efficient level of $6$. The high type earns an information rent of $(\theta_H - \theta_L)\,q_L = 4 \times 2 = 8$ — surplus it captures purely because the seller can't observe types.

This is why the cheap version of a product is often deliberately hobbled — the stripped-down software tier, the no-legroom fare, the limited warranty. The damage isn't incidental; it is engineered to stop high-value customers from buying down. Screening turns the seller's information disadvantage into a menu that makes buyers sort themselves, completing the picture begun with signaling in Chapter 7: there the informed moved first, here the uninformed does.

Wrapping up — the whole arc

Continuous strategies turn equilibrium-hunting into calculus: write each player's payoff, take the partial derivative with respect to their own choice, set it to zero, and solve the resulting reaction functions together. That single recipe carried us through the pillars of industrial economics — Cournot quantity competition with its downward-sloping reaction functions, Stackelberg leadership and the value of commitment, the Bertrand paradox where price competition alone restores the competitive outcome, Hotelling location and minimum differentiation, Tullock contests and the waste of rent-seeking, and the public-goods free-rider problem. Each is the same Nash equilibrium you met in Chapter 1, now living on a continuum and found with a derivative. With this capstone, you have travelled the full arc of game theory: from a single dominant strategy to the calculus of markets, from perfectly rational players to the fairness-minded humans of the laboratory. The games never stop — but now you know how to find their equilibria.

Problem 14.1 · Cournot Easy

Two firms face inverse demand $P = 120 - Q$ with marginal cost $c = 30$. Find each firm's Cournot output, the market price, and each firm's profit.

Solution

Here $a = 120$, $b = 1$, $c = 30$. Each firm's Cournot output is

$q^* = \frac{a-c}{3b} = \frac{120 - 30}{3} = 30$.

Total output $Q = 60$, price $P = 120 - 60 = 60$, and each firm's profit is $(P - c)q^* = (60 - 30)\cdot 30 = 900$.

Problem 14.2 · Cournot Medium

With inverse demand $P = 120 - Q$ and $c = 30$, now suppose $n = 5$ identical firms compete in Cournot fashion. Find each firm's output, total output, and the price. Compare the price to the duopoly case from Problem 14.1.

Solution

The $n$-firm symmetric Cournot output is $q^* = \frac{a-c}{(n+1)b}$. With $n = 5$:

$q^* = \frac{120 - 30}{(5+1)\cdot 1} = \frac{90}{6} = 15$ per firm.

Total output $Q = 5 \times 15 = 75$, price $P = 120 - 75 = 45$.

Compared to the duopoly price of $60$, five firms push the price down to $45$ — closer to marginal cost $30$. As $n$ grows, the price keeps falling toward $c$, illustrating how entry erodes market power.

Problem 14.3 · Stackelberg Medium

Using $P = 120 - Q$ and $c = 30$, suppose firm 1 is a Stackelberg leader and firm 2 follows. Find both outputs and both profits, and confirm the leader does better than in Cournot.

Solution

Leader output $q_1^* = \frac{a-c}{2b} = \frac{90}{2} = 45$; follower output $q_2^* = \frac{a-c}{4b} = \frac{90}{4} = 22.5$.

Total $Q = 67.5$, price $P = 120 - 67.5 = 52.5$.

Leader profit $(52.5 - 30)\cdot 45 = 22.5 \times 45 = 1012.5$; follower profit $(52.5 - 30)\cdot 22.5 = 22.5 \times 22.5 = 506.25$.

The leader earns $1012.5 > 900$ (its Cournot profit from Problem 14.1), confirming the first-mover advantage; the follower earns $506.25 < 900$, worse than under simultaneous play. Committing to a large output first pays.

Problem 14.4 · Bertrand Easy

Two firms sell an identical product with common marginal cost $c = 30$ and compete by setting prices. What is the equilibrium price, and what profit does each earn? Explain in one sentence why no higher common price can hold.

Solution

The unique Bertrand equilibrium is $p_1 = p_2 = c = 30$, and each firm earns zero profit (price equals marginal cost).

No higher common price $p > 30$ can hold because either firm could shave its price slightly below the other, still stay above cost, and capture the entire market — so undercutting is always profitable until price is driven down to marginal cost.

Problem 14.5 · Hotelling Medium

In Hotelling's linear city $[0,1]$ with uniform consumers and fixed equal prices, suppose shop 1 is currently at $0.3$ and shop 2 at $0.7$. Is this a Nash equilibrium? If not, show the direction each shop wants to move and state where they end up.

Solution

At $(0.3, 0.7)$, the midpoint between the shops is $0.5$, so shop 1 captures $[0, 0.5]$ (share $0.5$) and shop 2 captures $[0.5, 1]$ (share $0.5$). It looks balanced — but it is not an equilibrium.

Shop 1 can increase its share by moving right, toward shop 2: if it moves to $0.5$, the new midpoint becomes $0.6$, giving shop 1 the share $[0, 0.6] = 0.6 > 0.5$. Symmetrically, shop 2 wants to move left.

Each keeps edging toward the center to grab more of the middle, so the only rest point is $x_1 = x_2 = 0.5$ — both at the center, splitting the market 50/50, with neither able to gain by moving. That is the unique Nash equilibrium (minimum differentiation).

Problem 14.6 · Tullock contest Medium

Two firms compete for a prize worth $V = 80$ in a Tullock contest (win probability proportional to effort). Find each firm's equilibrium effort, the total effort, and each firm's expected net payoff. What fraction of the prize is dissipated?

Solution

Each firm's equilibrium effort is $x^* = \frac{V}{4} = \frac{80}{4} = 20$.

Total effort $= 2 \times 20 = 40$. Each wins with probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$, so expected net payoff is $\tfrac{1}{2}(80) - 20 = 40 - 20 = 20$ (equivalently $\frac{V}{4} = 20$).

The fraction dissipated is $\frac{40}{80} = \tfrac{1}{2}$ — half the prize is consumed by the contest, the hallmark rent-seeking waste of the Tullock model.

Problem 14.7 · Public goods Medium

Four neighbors each have $w = 100$ and may contribute to a shared good. Each dollar contributed returns $\alpha = 0.4$ to every neighbor. (a) What does each contribute in Nash equilibrium, and why? (b) What is the socially optimal contribution, and why does it differ?

Solution

(a) Nash equilibrium. The private return to a neighbor's own dollar is $\alpha = 0.4 < 1$: giving up $1 yields them only $0.40 back. So $\frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial g_i} = -1 + 0.4 = -0.6 < 0$, and each neighbor's best response is to contribute nothing: $g_i^* = 0$. Free-riding is a dominant strategy.

(b) Social optimum. The social return per dollar is $n\alpha = 4 \times 0.4 = 1.6 > 1$: one dollar contributed creates $1.60 of total benefit across the four neighbors. So collective welfare rises with every contribution, and the optimum is for everyone to contribute their entire $100. The clash between the private incentive ($0.4 < 1$, give nothing) and the social return ($1.6 > 1$, give all) is the free-rider problem — the reason public goods are typically funded through taxation or enforced cooperation rather than voluntary giving.

Problem 14.8 · Synthesis Hard

For the demand $P = a - bQ$ with cost $c$, rank the total industry output under four regimes — monopoly, Cournot duopoly, Stackelberg duopoly, and Bertrand duopoly — from lowest to highest, deriving each total. What does the ranking reveal about how the mode of competition affects market outcomes?

Solution

Let $M = \frac{a-c}{b}$ denote the "competitive output gap" for brevity. Derive each total output:

Monopoly: maximize $(a - bQ - c)Q$; first-order condition $a - 2bQ - c = 0$ gives $Q_M = \frac{a-c}{2b} = \tfrac{1}{2}M$.

Cournot duopoly: each makes $\frac{a-c}{3b}$, so $Q_C = \frac{2(a-c)}{3b} = \tfrac{2}{3}M$.

Stackelberg duopoly: leader $\frac{a-c}{2b}$ plus follower $\frac{a-c}{4b}$, so $Q_S = \frac{3(a-c)}{4b} = \tfrac{3}{4}M$.

Bertrand duopoly: price equals cost, so the market clears at the competitive quantity $Q_B = \frac{a-c}{b} = M$.

Ranking the coefficients $\tfrac{1}{2} < \tfrac{2}{3} < \tfrac{3}{4} < 1$, total output rises:

$$Q_M < Q_C < Q_S < Q_B.$$

Monopoly restricts output most (highest price), Cournot duopoly produces more, Stackelberg more still (the leader's aggression expands the market), and Bertrand reaches the full competitive output at price $= c$. The lesson: with the same demand and costs, the structure of competition — how many firms, whether they set quantities or prices, whether they move together or in sequence — determines everything about price, output, and welfare. Market structure is destiny.
